# Seals 1-6 -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

Revelation 6-7 presents the first six of seven seals opened by the Lamb. This study examined the literary structure of the four horsemen, the shift in tone at the fifth seal's altar cry, how the interlude of Revelation 7 answers the sixth seal's climactic question, and how the entire sequence relates to Jesus' Olivet Discourse in Matthew 24.

---

## The Four Horsemen Follow a Repeating Pattern

The first four seals follow a consistent formula: the Lamb opens a seal, one of the four living creatures commands "Come," a colored horse appears, the rider is described, and the rider's effect is stated. This template repeats across all four seals but with deliberate variations that distinguish each one.

A key phrase in seals 1, 2, and 4 is "was given" -- indicating that the horsemen's authority comes from God, not from themselves. The fourth horseman is the only one who is named:

> And I looked, and behold a pale horse: and his name that sat on him was Death, and Hell followed with him. And power was given unto them over the fourth part of the earth, to kill with sword, and with hunger, and with death, and with the beasts of the earth. (Rev 6:8)

The four killing means -- sword, hunger, death, and beasts -- match a specific list of covenant-violation judgments from the Old Testament. Ezekiel 14:21 names the same four: "the sword, and the famine, and the noisome beast, and the pestilence." The same list appears in Leviticus 26 and Deuteronomy 28. The horsemen are not random catastrophes; they draw on a fixed set of divine judgments tied to covenant violation, administered with measured restraint ("the fourth part of the earth").

---

## The Fifth Seal Breaks the Pattern Entirely

At the fifth seal, every element of the horseman formula disappears. No living creature speaks. No horse appears. Instead, the scene shifts to the heavenly sanctuary:

> And when he had opened the fifth seal, I saw under the altar the souls of them that were slain for the word of God, and for the testimony which they held: And they cried with a loud voice, saying, How long, O Lord, holy and true, dost thou not judge and avenge our blood on them that dwell on the earth? (Rev 6:9-10)

The "How long?" cry is a recognized form in the Old Testament. It appears in Psalms 13, 79, and 94, in Habakkuk 1, and in Zechariah 1:12. In every case the cry is not faithless complaint but a plea of faith under prolonged suffering, directed to a God who is sovereign and capable of acting. The martyrs address God as "Lord" (a title emphasizing absolute sovereign authority), "holy," and "true" -- framing their question around God's character.

This cry at 6:10 opens a vindication arc that runs through the rest of Revelation. The midpoint confirmation comes at 16:7: "true and righteous are thy judgments." The resolution comes at 19:2: God "hath avenged the blood of his servants at her hand." The shift from "dost thou not avenge?" (ongoing, present tense) to "hath avenged" (completed, past tense) marks the grammatical close of the arc.

---

## The Sixth Seal Asks a Question the Interlude Answers

The sixth seal unleashes cosmic upheaval -- earthquake, darkened sun, moon like blood, falling stars, the sky rolled back like a scroll. Every level of human society tries to hide. Their question closes the chapter:

> For the great day of his wrath is come; and who shall be able to stand? (Rev 6:17)

This is the same question Joel 2:11 asks in a similar cosmic-judgment context. It is not a rhetorical flourish -- it demands a real answer, and Revelation 7 provides one.

---

## Revelation 7 Answers: The Sealed and the Standing

The interlude between the sixth and seventh seals introduces two groups. John first hears the number 144,000, sealed from twelve tribes of Israel (Rev 7:1-8). He then sees an innumerable multitude from every nation, standing before the throne:

> After this I beheld, and, lo, a great multitude, which no man could number, of all nations, and kindreds, and people, and tongues, stood before the throne, and before the Lamb, clothed with white robes, and palms in their hands. (Rev 7:9)

The word "stood" in verse 9 uses the same Greek root as "stand" in 6:17. The sixth seal asks who can stand; the interlude shows people standing. The answer is the sealed, redeemed people before the throne.

The multitude holds palm branches and the text uses a word for God dwelling among them that echoes the word "tabernacle." These are both specific Feast of Tabernacles details, placing this scene in a post-harvest, post-judgment celebration. Their elder identifies them as those who "came out of great tribulation" and "washed their robes... in the blood of the Lamb" (Rev 7:14) -- a paradox where blood produces white.

---

## How the Seals Relate to Matthew 24

The sequence of seals bears a recognizable resemblance to the Olivet Discourse in Matthew 24 -- wars, famine, death, persecution, and cosmic signs appear in both, roughly in the same order. The cosmic signs in particular share strong vocabulary overlap between Matthew 24:29 and Revelation 6:12-14:

> Immediately after the tribulation of those days shall the sun be darkened, and the moon shall not give her light, and the stars shall fall from heaven, and the powers of the heavens shall be shaken. (Matt 24:29)

The parallel holds clearly from positions 2 through 6. The contested point is position 1: if the parallel is strict, the white horseman should represent deception and false Christs, as in Matthew 24:4-5. But the words for "deceive" and "false Christ" from Matthew 24 are entirely absent from Revelation 6:2 -- a meaningful gap. The parallel appears to be structural (shared pattern, shared Old Testament sources) rather than a rigid one-to-one correspondence at every point.
