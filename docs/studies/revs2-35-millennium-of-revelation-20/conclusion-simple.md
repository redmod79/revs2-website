# The Millennium -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

Revelation 20 describes a period of one thousand years that falls between the second coming of Christ and the creation of a new heaven and earth. This study examines what the chapter actually says about that period -- who is involved, what happens, and in what order -- by looking closely at the words John uses and how they function across the rest of Revelation.

---

## Satan Is Locked Away -- And the Text Shows It Is Future

When the thousand years begin, an angel descends from heaven and takes five escalating actions against Satan: he seizes him, binds him, casts him into the abyss, shuts it, and seals it. Five separate verbs, each building on the last. The stated purpose is clear: "that he should deceive the nations no more" (Rev 20:3).

The strongest evidence that this binding is still future from our standpoint is the way the word "deceive" shifts tense across the book. In Revelation 12:9, Satan "deceiveth the whole world" -- present tense, ongoing activity. In 20:3, the binding is intended to stop that deception -- future interruption. In 20:8, after his release, he goes out again "to deceive the nations" -- resumed activity. The grammar requires a future period when the deception stops, flanked by periods when it does not.

This is why the amillennial view -- which holds that the binding happened at the cross and Satan is currently bound -- runs into trouble. Paul, writing after the cross, calls Satan "the god of this world" who blinds minds (2 Cor 4:4). Peter describes him as "a roaring lion" who "walketh about, seeking whom he may devour" (1 Pet 5:8). That is not the description of a being sealed in a locked abyss.

---

## Two Resurrections, Not One

> "And they lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years. But the rest of the dead lived not again until the thousand years were finished. This is the first resurrection." -- Rev 20:4-5

The word translated "lived" in verse 4 is the Greek word *ezesan*. The same word form appears four times in Revelation: once for Christ who "was dead and came to life" (Rev 2:8), once for the beast whose wound "lived" again (Rev 13:14), and twice here in chapter 20 -- once positively for the saints, once negated for the wicked dead. In every uncontested use, the word means bodily coming-to-life. The study finds no grammatical basis for giving it a different meaning in chapter 20.

The word John uses for "resurrection" in verse 5 -- *anastasis* -- occurs 42 times in the New Testament. In 41 of those occurrences, it refers to bodily resurrection. John also had available a different Greek word specifically used for spiritual co-resurrection (*synegeiro*, used in Colossians 2:12 and Ephesians 2:6), but he did not use it here. He chose the standard bodily resurrection word.

The paired language of "first resurrection" and "second death" implies a logical matrix: a first death (physical), a first resurrection (the righteous), a second resurrection (the wicked, at the millennium's end), and a second death (the lake of fire). Revelation 2:11 promises that those in the first resurrection will not be "hurt of the second death."

## What the Saints Do During the Thousand Years

The saints who are resurrected at the millennium's start receive judicial authority. The phrase "judgment was given unto them" (Rev 20:4) is a divine passive -- the same construction used in Daniel 7:22, where "judgment was given to the saints of the Most High." They are not standing before judgment; they are exercising it.

Paul confirms the content of this review: "Do ye not know that the saints shall judge the world? ... Know ye not that we shall judge angels?" (1 Cor 6:2-3). The millennial reign appears to be a period of judicial review in which the saints examine the cases of the wicked and the fallen angels, understanding why God's verdicts are just.

The verb for "reign" tracks through three stages in Revelation: the saints begin to reign at the millennium's start (aorist tense, inception), continue reigning through the thousand years (future tense, 20:6), and reign "for ever and ever" beyond it (22:5). The millennium is where the reign begins in its judicial function, not where it ends.

---

## After the Thousand Years: Gog, Magog, and the Final Judgment

When the thousand years end, Satan is released and goes out to deceive resurrected nations from "the four corners of the earth" -- called Gog and Magog. Their number is described as "the sand of the sea" (Rev 20:8), language that deliberately inverts the Abrahamic covenant promise. They surround the city of God, and fire from heaven devours them.

Satan is then cast into the lake of fire, and the text's phrasing confirms the sequence: "where the beast and the false prophet are" (Rev 20:10). That word "where" presupposes the beast and false prophet are already there -- they were cast in at Revelation 19:20, before the millennium. The chapter does not loop back; it moves forward.

The great white throne judgment follows. Two categories of books are opened: record books containing the evidence of deeds, and the book of life containing names. Death and Hades are then cast into the lake of fire, fulfilling Paul's statement in 1 Corinthians 15:26: "The last enemy that shall be destroyed is death."

## How the Old Testament Supports This Reading

Two Old Testament passages provide structural templates for what Revelation 20 describes. Isaiah 24:21-22 outlines a two-stage punishment for the enemies of God: first, they are "gathered as prisoners in the pit" and "shut up in the prison"; then, "after many days" they are visited and punished. This exactly matches Revelation 20 -- Satan confined for the millennium, then released and destroyed.

The Day of Atonement ceremony in Leviticus 16 supplies an even more specific parallel. After all atonement work is complete, the high priest places both hands on the live "scapegoat," confesses Israel's sins over it, and sends it by the hand of an appointed man to "a land of cutting off" -- a desolate wilderness. In Revelation, after the completion of atonement is marked (Rev 15:5-8) and the judgments are executed, an unnamed angel binds Satan and sends him to the abyss. The parallel is precise: the scapegoat does not make atonement; it carries sin away to desolation. Satan in the abyss is not being punished yet -- he is being removed from access to humanity.
