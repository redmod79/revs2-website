2. **Trinitarian greeting structure at Rev 1:4-5:**
The greeting formula at Rev 1:4-5 reads 'Grace to you and peace from (apo) him who is and was and is to come, and from (apo) the seven Spirits which are before his throne, and from (apo) Jesus Christ.' The research agent should run greek_parser.py --verse on Rev 1:4 and Rev 1:5 to verify that the same preposition apo (G575) governs all three sources. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G4151-pneuma.md for the Spirit term. Compare this structure with standard Pauline greetings (Rom 1:7, 1 Cor 1:3, 2 Cor 1:2, Gal 1:3, Eph 1:2, Phil 1:2) which use 'from God our Father and the Lord Jesus Christ' — two sources, no angels. If the seven Spirits are angels, Rev 1:4-5 uniquely inserts created beings as a co-equal source of divine grace. The grammar reference entry on genitive-of-quality-apposition ($GRAMMAR_REF/greek/genitive-of-quality-apposition.md) may be relevant for the genitive construction 'Spirits of God.'
**Verses:** Rev 1:4-5, Rom 1:7, 1 Cor 1:3, 2 Cor 1:2
**Strong's:** G575, G4151, G5485, G1515

3. **The Spirit-angel distinction: Rev 3:1 and Rev 8:2:**
Rev 3:1 has Christ holding (echon G2192, present active participle) two distinct objects: 'the seven Spirits of God AND (kai) the seven stars.' Since Rev 1:20 identifies stars as angels of the churches, echon + kai coordinates two distinct items — if seven Spirits WERE angels, the phrase would be redundant (TM091 from revs-25). Run greek_parser.py --verse on Rev 3:1 to confirm the conjunction structure. Separately, Rev 8:2 introduces 'the seven angels which stood before God' (hoi hepta angeloi hoi enopion tou theou hestekotes) — an entirely different phrase from 'the seven Spirits which are before his throne' (Rev 1:4). Ian Paul's argument: if John already meant seven angels at 1:4, why introduce them with different language at 8:2? Run greek_parser.py --verse on Rev 8:2. Also check whether Tobit 12:15 or 1 Enoch 20:1-7 use similar phrasing — these are the Second Temple texts cited by the angelic-view proponents. Note that the grammar reference entry on constructio-ad-sensum ($GRAMMAR_REF/greek/constructio-ad-sensum.md) documents Rev 11:4 where feminine subjects take masculine participle — this shows John's awareness of personal referents behind symbols.
**Verses:** Rev 3:1, Rev 8:2, Rev 1:4, Rev 1:20, Rev 11:4
**Strong's:** G4151, G32, G792, G2192

10. **The sealed thunders as structural boundary marker:**
The three competing theories are: (a) Bousset's 'fourth judgment series' — the thunders represent an unrevealed set of seven judgments parallel to seals, trumpets, and bowls. This connects to Lev 26's four sevenfold plague cycles (Lev 26:18, 21, 24, 28 — verify that these are genuinely four distinct sevenfold escalations). (b) Caird's 'gracious cancellation' — God suppresses the thunders in mercy. Beale counters that sphragizo means 'seal for preservation,' not 'cancel.' (c) Aune's 'prophetic authenticity' — withholding marks the visionary as genuine.
