# Trumpets 1-4 -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

Revelation 8:7-12 describes four trumpets that strike the earth, sea, freshwater, and sky — each destroying exactly one-third of its target. This study examined how those four trumpets are structured, where their imagery comes from in the Old Testament, and what separates them from the three trumpets that follow.

---

## The "One-Third" Pattern Is the Backbone of These Four Trumpets

The most consistent feature of trumpets 1-4 is a repeating fraction: one-third of whatever is struck is destroyed. The phrase "the third part" appears eleven times across just six verses (Rev 8:7-12), and it builds to a climax in the fourth trumpet, where it appears five times in a single verse.

This fraction did not originate with Revelation. It comes from Ezekiel 5, where God commanded the prophet to divide his hair into three parts, each destroyed by a different means — fire, sword, and scattering to the wind. That passage applied the same tripartite destruction to Jerusalem's population:

> A third part of thee shall die with the pestilence, and with famine shall they be consumed in the midst of thee: and a third part shall fall by the sword round about thee; and I will scatter a third part into all the winds. — Ezek 5:12

In Ezekiel, all three thirds are destroyed — total judgment. Revelation shifts the pattern: only one third is struck. This points to Zechariah 13:8-9, where two thirds die but one third survives and is refined. The surviving majority in Revelation represents an opportunity for repentance — one the text says was not taken.

---

## Each Trumpet Targets a Different Part of Creation

The four trumpets move through distinct domains: the land and its vegetation (trumpet 1), the sea (trumpet 2), rivers and freshwater springs (trumpet 3), and the sun, moon, and stars (trumpet 4). These correspond to the domains God created in Genesis 1 — the earth, seas, and lights in the sky.

The fourth trumpet makes this creation-reversal connection most clearly. God created the sun and moon on the fourth day "to rule over the day and over the night, and to divide the light from the darkness" (Gen 1:18). The fourth trumpet directly undoes that function:

> And the fourth angel sounded, and the third part of the sun was smitten, and the third part of the moon, and the third part of the stars; so as the third part of them was darkened, and the day shone not for a third part of it, and the night likewise. — Rev 8:12

The trumpet judgments are not random catastrophes — they strike at what God made, domain by domain.

---

## The Plague Imagery Comes from the Exodus, but Not in Order

The four trumpets draw heavily on the ten plagues of Egypt. Hail and fire (trumpet 1) recall the seventh plague. The sea turning to blood and the death of sea creatures (trumpet 2) recall the first plague. Darkness (trumpet 4) recalls the ninth plague. But the order is not the same as in Exodus, and the connections are sometimes blended — trumpet 1 adds blood to its hail and fire, which belongs to the first plague, not the seventh.

This kind of reordering was an established practice. Psalms 78 and 105 each retell the Exodus plagues in a different sequence from Exodus itself. Revelation draws on the prophets as well — Isaiah, Joel, Jeremiah, and Ezekiel all use plague imagery in their own warnings of judgment.

Revelation itself confirms the Exodus connection. At the end of the trumpet sequence, the survivors are called out for not repenting in the face of "these plagues" (Rev 9:20) — the text's own word for what the trumpets were.

---

## The Wormwood Trumpet Has No Direct Plague Parallel

The third trumpet stands apart. A star named Wormwood falls and poisons a third of the rivers and springs:

> And the name of the star is called Wormwood: and the third part of the waters became wormwood; and many men died of the waters, because they were made bitter. — Rev 8:11

No Exodus plague matches this directly. The imagery comes instead from Jeremiah, where God threatened to feed the unfaithful people with wormwood as a consequence of abandoning the law and following other gods (Jer 9:13-15, 23:13-15). Wormwood in the Old Testament is consistently tied to apostasy and covenant unfaithfulness — bitter consequences for turning away.

---

## An Eagle Divides the First Four Trumpets from the Last Three

After the fourth trumpet, a voice announces a formal break in the sequence:

> And I beheld, and heard an angel flying through the midst of heaven, saying with a loud voice, Woe, woe, woe, to the inhabiters of the earth by reason of the other voices of the trumpet of the three angels, which are yet to sound! — Rev 8:13

The three "woes" map directly to trumpets 5, 6, and 7 — the text accounts for each one as it passes (Rev 9:12, 11:14). This 4+3 division is not just a label. Trumpets 1-4 are brief, strike natural domains with impersonal forces (hail, fire, a falling star), and are each described in one or two verses. Trumpets 5-7 introduce personified agents, demonic figures, and extended descriptions that span entire chapters.

---

## The Purpose of the Trumpets: Warning That Was Not Heeded

The trumpet sequence ends with a summary statement that reveals its purpose:

> And the rest of the men which were not killed by these plagues yet repented not of the works of their hands, that they should not worship devils, and idols of gold, and silver, and brass, and stone, and of wood: which neither can see, nor hear, nor walk: Neither repented they of their murders, nor of their sorceries, nor of their fornication, nor of their thefts. — Rev 9:20-21

The one-third fraction left a surviving majority — two-thirds of those affected remained alive. The purpose was to give them opportunity to turn. They did not. This pattern mirrors Amos 4, where five escalating judgments each close with the refrain "yet have ye not returned unto me." The trumpets operate as warning judgments in the tradition of the Old Testament prophets: judgment is graduated, partial, and aimed at producing repentance before the final reckoning.
