The critical grammatical observation is the SHIFT between pairs: Visions 1-2 use egenomen en pneumati (ginomai, first person, John becomes 'in the Spirit' as his own experience) while Visions 3-4 use apenengken me en pneumati (apophero G667, third person, an angel carries John away in the Spirit). All four use pneuma in the dative singular neuter without the definite article. Compare with Ezekiel's Spirit-transport passages (Ezek 3:12-14, 8:3, 11:24, 37:1, 40:2, 43:5) as intertextual parallels.

3. **The meta tauta system: exhaustive catalog and the chronological question:**
The meta tauta system creates the most scholarly disagreement because its interpretation directly affects whether Revelation's visions are parallel (recapitulation) or sequential (linear). Run greek_parser.py --verse on REV 4:1, REV 7:1, REV 7:9, REV 15:5, REV 18:1, and REV 19:1 to capture the exact Greek forms. Also parse REV 1:19, REV 9:12, and REV 20:3 which Larsen includes but which are disputed. The critical distinction is between meta tauta eidon (a structural-transition formula meaning 'after these things I saw') and meta tauta in non-formulaic usage (simple temporal reference). Rev 7:1 uses meta TOUTO (singular, neuter — G5124) while 7:9 uses meta TAUTA (plural — G5023); this grammatical variation needs parsing verification. Rev 20:3 uses meta tauta as 'after that' referring to a future event, not a vision transition. Swete's principle: if John intended chronological sequence, he would use meta tauta eidon; kai eidon alone is not inherently chronological. Yet at 13:11 and 15:1, kai eidon IS chronological. 5. **The semeion ophthe markers and their distinction from kai eidon:**
The semeion ophthe markers constitute a minor but structurally significant marker system distinct from kai eidon. READ $WORD_STUDIES/G4592-semeion.md for the full semantic range of semeion (sign). Run greek_parser.py --verse on REV 12:1, REV 12:3, and REV 15:1. At 12:1 and 12:3, the text uses ophthe (aorist passive of horao) — 'a great wonder/sign appeared [was seen]' rather than the active 'I saw'. The passive voice shifts agency: the sign presents itself rather than John actively seeing it. This passive construction occurs only here in Revelation's structural markers. At 15:1, the formula is hybrid: kai eidon allo semeion ('and I saw another sign') — combining the active kai eidon with the semeion vocabulary from 12:1, 12:3. The three sign markers cluster at the structural center of Revelation (chs. 12-15), which multiple scholars (Paulien, Strand) identify as the central section of the book. The woman (12:1), the dragon (12:3), and the seven last plagues (15:1) are explicitly identified as 'signs' — a term suggesting symbolic significance beyond the narrative surface.
**Verses:** Rev 12:1, Rev 12:3, Rev 15:1
**Strong's:** G4592, G3708, G3700

9. **Chronological neutrality of kai eidon: testing the sequential reading:**
The chronological question is the single most disputed issue in Revelation's structural analysis. READ the grammar reference entry at $GRAMMAR_REF/greek/apocalyptic-narrative-prolepsis.md which discusses the convention of visionary recursion — summary-tableau followed by rewind to bounded phases. The key test cases: (1) Rev 12:1-5 narrates Christ's birth AFTER the 7th trumpet's kingdom declaration at 11:15 — this is the single most decisive proof that kai eidon does NOT always signal forward chronological movement. (2) Rev 13:11 introduces the second beast with kai eidon, but this beast operates CONTEMPORANEOUSLY with the first beast (13:1), not after it. (3) The elthen he orge ('his wrath is come') formula appears identically at Rev 6:17 (seal climax) and Rev 11:18 (trumpet climax) — the same event described at the climax of two different sequences. (4) Mountains and islands appear at both 6:14 and 16:20 — the same subjects at climaxes of different sequences. (5) Rev 19:11-21:1 contains 7 kai eidon in rapid succession — does this dense cluster represent a progressive sequence from Second Coming through new creation, or could some elements be concurrent? Swete's principle: if chronological intent, John would have used meta tauta eidon; kai eidon is not inherently chronological. However, at 13:11 and 15:1, kai eidon IS chronological. The aorist tense of eidon describes completed action without specifying temporal relationship between events.
**Verses:** Rev 12:1-5, Rev 13:1, Rev 13:11, Rev 6:17, Rev 11:18, Rev 6:14, Rev 16:20, Rev 19:11, Rev 20:1, Rev 20:4
**Strong's:** G3708, G2532

10. **Competing structural models: four-vision vs. six-block vs. eight-vision vs. seven-section:**
The competing structural models are not all equally supported by the textual markers, and documenting which markers each model privileges (and which it must explain away) is the study's analytical payoff. The four-vision model (Bauckham, Hicks, Bandy) privileges en pneumati as the primary organizer — 4 clear divisions at 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, 21:10. But it must explain why meta tauta creates SUB-divisions within Vision 2 (at 7:1, 7:9, 15:5) that other models treat as primary divisions. The six-block model (Korner) privileges meta tauta eidon — 5 occurrences creating 6 blocks. But it must explain why en pneumati creates only 4 divisions that don't align with the 6 blocks. The eight-vision model (Strand, AUSS 1987) privileges victorious-introduction scenes and chiastic pairing, creating a historical/eschatological divide at chs. 14-15. But the en pneumati markers don't support this division point. The seven-section model (Paulien) privileges sanctuary introductions — lampstands (1:12), throne room (4:1-5:14), altar (8:2-6), temple (15:5-8). But it must explain why en pneumati doesn't mark the sanctuary transitions. Bandy's integrative three-layer approach (surface structure, intertextual, intratextual) may resolve the competition by showing that different markers operate at different structural LEVELS rather than competing at the same level. The research agent should catalog which model each ambiguity point from Focus Area 8 supports or undermines.
**Verses:** Rev 1:10, Rev 4:1-2, Rev 7:1, Rev 11:19, Rev 15:5, Rev 17:3, Rev 21:10
**Strong's:** G4151, G3708, G3326

11. **The 'Spirit saith unto the churches' formula within Vision 1:**
The formula 'He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches' occurs exactly 7 times in Revelation, all within Vision 1 (TM004 from revs-01). Run greek_parser.py --verse on REV 2:7 to capture the exact Greek form. Note the significant grammatical difference: this formula uses to pneuma (the Spirit, with the definite article) while the en pneumati visionary-state formula uses pneuma WITHOUT the article (anarthrous dative). This distinction suggests different functions for the same word: pneuma without the article in en pneumati = the visionary state/means of transport; to pneuma with the article in 'the Spirit saith' = the Holy Spirit as speaker/revealer. The formula's strict confinement to Vision 1 (none appears in Visions 2-4) is itself a structural observation. The formula creates an internal cadence within the seven churches section, marking each letter's close. After Rev 3:22 (the last occurrence), the formula never appears again — suggesting that Vision 1 has a unique structural character (direct communication to the churches) not shared by the subsequent visions.
**Verses:** Rev 2:7, Rev 2:11, Rev 2:17, Rev 2:29, Rev 3:6, Rev 3:13, Rev 3:22
