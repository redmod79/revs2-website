# Structural Markers -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

This study mapped the literary skeleton of the book of Revelation by tracing three repeated phrases that John uses to signal transitions in his vision. The goal was to understand how these phrases divide the book into sections — and what they can and cannot tell us about whether those sections describe events in chronological order.

---

## Four Visions Built on a Single Phrase

The clearest structural division in Revelation comes from the phrase "I was in the Spirit" (or "he carried me away in the Spirit"), which appears exactly four times: at 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, and 21:10. Each occurrence marks the beginning of a major vision and is paired with a specific location.

> **Rev 1:10** — I was in the Spirit on the Lord's day, and heard behind me a great voice, as of a trumpet,

> **Rev 4:1-2** — After this I looked, and, behold, a door was opened in heaven... And immediately I was in the spirit: and, behold, a throne was set in heaven, and one sat on the throne.

> **Rev 17:3** — So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast...

> **Rev 21:10** — And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain, and shewed me that great city, the holy Jerusalem, descending out of heaven from God,

There is also a subtle grammatical shift between the four. In the first two visions, John "became in the Spirit" (he is the one entering the state). In the last two, an angel "carried him away in the Spirit" (he is being transported). This 2+2 pairing is reinforced by the fact that Visions 3 and 4 share nearly identical introductions — an angel says "come hither, I will shew thee" and then shows John a woman associated with a great city (the harlot/Babylon in Vision 3; the bride/New Jerusalem in Vision 4). The mirroring is deliberate.

---

## "And I Saw" — Scene Breaks Within Each Vision

Once the four major visions are established, a second phrase — "and I saw" — repeatedly subdivides each vision into individual scenes. It appears roughly 35 times as a structural marker throughout the book, functioning somewhat like paragraph breaks in a modern text.

The critical observation here is that "and I saw" does not necessarily mean "and then this happened next." Two cases prove this. First, at Revelation 13:1 and 13:11, John says "and I saw" to introduce both the beast from the sea and the beast from the earth — but the text tells us the second beast operates simultaneously with the first, not after it. Second, after the climactic declaration at Revelation 11:15 that the kingdom of God has arrived, the narrative in chapter 12 jumps back centuries to the birth of Christ. No transition marker is used. The "and I saw" formula marks the sequence of John's visionary experience, not the chronological order of the events themselves.

The double form "and I saw, and behold" marks the most significant scene openings — the Lamb, the four horsemen, the great multitude, heaven opened. Nine of these elevated markers appear at major revelatory moments.

---

## "After These Things" — A Middle Level of Division

A third phrase, "after these things I saw," creates sub-divisions within the four major visions. It appears at Rev 4:1, 7:9, 15:5, and 18:1 in its most recognized form, with some additional disputed occurrences.

The most important convergence in the whole book occurs at Revelation 4:1-2, where both "after these things I saw" and "I was in the Spirit" appear within two verses of each other. This double marker makes the transition from Vision 1 (the letters to the churches) to Vision 2 (the heavenly throne room) the single clearest structural boundary in Revelation. Every scholar, regardless of how they outline the rest of the book, agrees that a major transition happens here.

---

## A Fourth System: Escalating Judgment Formulas

The study also identified a fourth marker system that operates independently of the three primary ones. A cluster of vocabulary drawn from the thunderstorm at Mount Sinai — lightnings, voices, thunderings, earthquake, hail — appears four times in Revelation, each time with more elements than before: three elements at 4:5, four at 8:5, five at 11:19, and five with added superlatives at 16:18-21.

> **Rev 11:19** — And the temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament: and there were lightnings, and voices, and thunderings, and an earthquake, and great hail.

This escalating formula marks structural transitions that none of the three primary markers signal. At Revelation 11:19 in particular, it creates a major pivot point that the "I was in the Spirit" system does not recognize as a division. This is the study's key finding about structural divergence: different marker systems mark different boundaries because they operate at different levels of the text's architecture.

---

## What the Markers Cannot Settle

The structural markers are consistent with several competing ways of outlining Revelation — a four-vision model, a six-block model, an eight-section model, a seven-section model. Each model privileges a different marker system and produces a different outline, and the textual evidence supports all of them to varying degrees.

More significantly, the markers do not resolve the chronological question. Whether the visions of chapters 6 through 20 describe events in sequence, or circle back to cover the same period from different angles, or nest inside one another — none of that is determined by the structural formulas themselves. The "and I saw" formula is demonstrably chronologically neutral. The dense cluster of seven "and I saw" statements in Revelation 19-21 is consistent with forward progression, but the formula's proven neutrality means that density alone does not prove sequence.

What the markers do establish is the literary architecture John built: four major visions framed by a prologue and epilogue, subdivided by scene-break formulas, punctuated by escalating judgment signals, and held together by verbal parallels that link distant passages across the book.
