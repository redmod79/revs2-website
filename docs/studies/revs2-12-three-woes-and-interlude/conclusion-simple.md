# Three Woes & Interlude -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

This study examines Revelation 8:13 through 11:19 -- the shift from the first four trumpets to the three "woe" trumpets, the prophetic interlude with the little book and two witnesses, and the seventh trumpet's kingdom announcement. The central question is how these sections fit together structurally and what the seventh trumpet's declaration has to do with the bowl judgments that follow later in Revelation.

---

## The Trumpets Split Into Two Groups

The first four trumpets (Rev 8:6-12) are brief and formulaic. Each one strikes a domain of creation -- land, sea, freshwater, sky -- and destroys one third of it. They average about one and a half verses each and read like a series of impersonal cosmic events.

Trumpets five through seven are entirely different. The fifth trumpet alone takes twelve verses. The sixth takes nine. The transition is announced by an eagle crying out:

> And I beheld, and heard an angel flying through the midst of heaven, saying with a loud voice, Woe, woe, woe, to the inhabiters of the earth by reason of the other voices of the trumpet of the three angels, which are yet to sound! (Rev 8:13)

The phrase "inhabiters of the earth" carries a specific meaning throughout Revelation -- it refers consistently to the ungodly, not to people in general. The woe-trumpets are directed at them. Where the first four trumpets damaged nature, the woe-trumpets target humanity. The fifth trumpet makes this explicit: the locust-army is forbidden to harm grass or trees and is told to strike only those who do not have the seal of God.

---

## Demonic Agents, Not Natural Disasters

The woe-trumpets also introduce a new kind of agency. The fifth trumpet opens the abyss -- a pit from which smoke pours out, blocking the sun -- and releases a locust-army with a king named Abaddon in Hebrew and Apollyon in Greek. Both names mean "the destroyer." The sixth trumpet releases four bound angels at the Euphrates and a cavalry army of 200 million. These are not natural phenomena; they are organized, named demonic forces operating under divine permission.

The fifth trumpet's locust-army draws on the prophet Joel's vision of an invading army. But where Joel described God's own army sent as judgment, Revelation's locusts rise from the abyss under a destroyer-king. The imagery is borrowed and deliberately inverted.

After the sixth trumpet kills a third of humanity, the study reaches its narrative turning point:

> And the rest of the men which were not killed by these plagues yet repented not of the works of their hands, that they should not worship devils, and idols of gold, and silver, and brass, and stone, and of wood: which neither can see, nor hear, nor walk. (Rev 9:20)

This failure to repent drives the rest of the book's logic. The trumpet judgments were warnings with limits. When they produce no repentance, the bowl judgments that come later carry no such limits.

---

## The Interlude: Little Book and Two Witnesses

Between the sixth and seventh trumpets, Revelation inserts an interlude -- exactly as it did between the sixth and seventh seals. A mighty angel descends holding a small scroll that is already open, and John is commissioned to eat it and prophesy again. The scroll is sweet in his mouth and bitter in his stomach -- the prophetic word contains both promise and judgment simultaneously.

> But in the days of the voice of the seventh angel, when he shall begin to sound, the mystery of God should be finished, as he hath declared to his servants the prophets. (Rev 10:7)

The interlude then introduces two witnesses who prophesy for 1,260 days (three and a half years). They are identified by their powers:

> These are the two olive trees, and the two candlesticks standing before the God of the earth. (Rev 11:4)

Their powers divide between Moses and Elijah: they shut the sky from rain (Elijah, 1 Kings 17), turn water to blood (Moses, Exodus 7), and call fire that consumes their enemies (Elijah, 2 Kings 1). Moses and Elijah together represent the Law and the Prophets -- the complete Old Testament witness. The legal basis is Deuteronomy 19:15: a matter is established by two witnesses.

After their ministry ends, a beast from the abyss kills them. But after three and a half days, they are raised and ascend to heaven -- a resurrection sequence that mirrors Christ's. The earthquake that follows produces the one unique response in all of Revelation: survivors "gave glory to the God of heaven," a positive acknowledgment that stands alone against the universal impenitence of chapters 9 and 16.

---

## The Seventh Trumpet: A Declaration, Not a Judgment

When the seventh trumpet sounds, no plague follows. Instead, voices in heaven announce:

> The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for ever and ever. (Rev 11:15)

The grammar of this announcement is significant. The verb "are become" is an aorist -- a tense that treats the action as a completed fact. The kingdom transfer is declared accomplished from a heavenly vantage point even before the events that will execute it have unfolded on earth. The hymn that follows catalogs five events that are coming:

> And the nations were angry, and thy wrath is come, and the time of the dead, that they should be judged, and that thou shouldest give reward unto thy servants the prophets, and to the saints, and them that fear thy name, small and great; and shouldest destroy them which destroy the earth. (Rev 11:18)

These five items map to the rest of Revelation: the nations' anger to chapters 12-13, God's wrath to the bowl judgments, the judgment of the dead to chapter 20, the reward to chapters 21-22, and the destruction of the destroyers to chapter 19.

---

## The Gateway to the Bowl Judgments

Revelation 11:19 closes the trumpet sequence with the temple in heaven opened and the ark of the covenant becoming visible -- the only point in Revelation where the ark appears. Lightnings, voices, thunder, earthquake, and great hail accompany the moment.

This verse is a hinge. The temple-opening language reappears at Revelation 15:5, where the bowls are introduced. The hail connects back to trumpet one (Rev 8:7) and forward to the seventh bowl (Rev 16:21, where the hail weighs about a talent). The third woe was announced at Rev 11:14 as "coming quickly," but its closure formula -- used for woes one and two -- never appears in Revelation. This suggests the third woe extends beyond the seventh trumpet into the bowl sequence.

The relationship between the seventh trumpet and the bowls is supported by several structural observations: the seventh trumpet and the seventh bowl share a great voice, a similar declaration, and an origin in the heavenly temple. The word for "finished" used at Rev 10:7 (for the mystery of God) reappears at Rev 15:1 (for the wrath of God). The bowls strike the same four domains as the first four trumpets, in the same order, but without the one-third limitation -- total rather than partial destruction.

The seventh trumpet functions as a summary announcement: it declares the full eschatological program as accomplished, and the subsequent chapters unpack how that program executes.
