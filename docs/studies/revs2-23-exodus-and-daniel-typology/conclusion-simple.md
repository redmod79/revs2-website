# Exodus & Daniel Typology -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

This study examines how Revelation builds its judgment sequences on the ten plagues of Exodus and how it draws the beast imagery of Revelation 13 directly from Daniel 7. Two separate but related questions: are the bowl judgments closer to the Exodus plagues than the trumpets, and what does Daniel 7 actually say that unlocks Revelation's composite beast?

---

## The Bowls Are the Real Exodus Replay

Revelation contains two parallel series of seven judgments — trumpets and bowls — and both echo the Exodus plagues. But they do so at different intensities. The trumpet judgments strike at one-third scale: one-third of the sea, one-third of rivers, one-third of the sky. The word "one-third" appears eleven times in the trumpet chapters alone. The bowl judgments operate at total scope — every sea creature dies, all rivers become blood, the beast's entire kingdom goes dark.

The Exodus plagues were total: "blood throughout all the land of Egypt," "thick darkness in all the land of Egypt three days." The bowls match that totality; the trumpets do not. Six of the seven bowls have direct vocabulary links to specific Exodus plagues. Bowl 1's "grievous sore" uses the same Greek word as the Septuagint's translation of Exodus's boil plague. Bowl 6's frog-spirits use the same rare Greek word for frogs that appears nowhere else in the New Testament. Bowl 7's massive hailstones echo the hail of Plague 7 through vocabulary that ties them together across both testaments.

Revelation itself announces this connection before the bowls even begin:

> "And they sing the song of Moses the servant of God, and the song of the Lamb, saying, Great and marvellous are thy works, Lord God Almighty; just and true are thy ways, thou King of saints." (Rev 15:3)

The victors sing the Song of Moses — the victory hymn from Exodus 15 — immediately before the bowls are poured out. Revelation is telling the reader directly: what follows is the antitypical Exodus.

## Why Three Plagues Are Missing

Seven of the ten Exodus plagues have echoes in Revelation. Three do not: gnats (Plague 3), flies (Plague 4), and livestock disease (Plague 5). These three are consecutive in Exodus and share a common character — they are domestic and agricultural, affecting household insects, house-flies, and farm animals. Revelation's judgment register operates at a different scale: ocean systems, river systems, celestial bodies, political kingdoms.

This is not a gap unique to Revelation. Psalm 78 and Psalm 105 both recite the Exodus plagues and both omit Plagues 3 and 5 from their lists. The same two plagues that Revelation drops were already being excluded in Israel's liturgical tradition, apparently because they did not carry the same theological weight as the cosmic-scale judgments. Revelation follows an established pattern.

## The Goshen Principle, Transformed

In Exodus, God protected Israel from five of the plagues by keeping them in the land of Goshen:

> "And I will put a division between my people and thy people." (Exod 8:23)

Revelation carries this same logic forward but changes the mechanism. Geographic protection (a land called Goshen) becomes personal identification (a seal on the forehead):

> "And it was commanded them that they should not hurt the grass of the earth, neither any green thing, neither any tree; but only those men which have not the seal of God in their foreheads." (Rev 9:4)

Then the principle inverts in the bowls: the first bowl's sores fall only on "the men which had the mark of the beast, and upon them which worshipped his image" (Rev 16:2). Those marked by God are protected; those marked by the beast receive the plague. The Exodus logic — divine discrimination between the protected and the judged — is constant throughout. What changes is the register: from a territory, to a household doorpost (Passover blood), to an individual forehead seal.

## Daniel 7 Decodes the Composite Beast

Daniel 7 presents four empires as four beasts in sequence: a lion (Babylon), a bear (Medo-Persia), a leopard (Greece), and a terrible unnamed beast (the fourth empire). Revelation 13:2 presents a single composite beast: "like unto a leopard, and his feet were as the feet of a bear, and his mouth as the mouth of a lion."

The key is that Daniel himself provides the interpretation within his own text. At Daniel 7:23:

> "Thus he said, The fourth beast shall be the fourth kingdom upon earth, which shall be diverse from all kingdoms, and shall devour the whole earth, and shall tread it down, and break it in pieces." (Dan 7:23)

Beast equals kingdom. That equation comes from Daniel, not from later interpreters. When Revelation combines the leopard, bear, and lion into one beast, it is applying Daniel's own decoder to all four kingdoms at once — a composite that represents every empire absorbed into a single final form. The math confirms it: Daniel's four beasts have a total of seven heads (1 + 1 + 4 + 1). Revelation's composite beast has exactly seven heads. The ten horns come specifically from Daniel's fourth beast. Neither number is invented; both are the direct result of combining Daniel's four beast descriptions.

## The Judgment Scene: Two Texts, One Pattern

Daniel 7:9-10 describes a throne set in heaven, the Ancient of Days seated with hair "like the pure wool," a vast assembly of thousands surrounding him, and the books opened for judgment:

> "I beheld till the thrones were cast down, and the Ancient of days did sit, whose garment was white as snow, and the hair of his head like the pure wool... the judgment was set, and the books were opened." (Dan 7:9-10)

Revelation does not reproduce this as a single scene. It distributes the elements across two passages. The throne and the surrounding multitude appear in Revelation 4-5, where John sees a throne in heaven and hears "ten thousand times ten thousand, and thousands of thousands" of angels around it (Rev 5:11). The books opened for judgment appear in Revelation 20:

> "And the books were opened: and another book was opened, which is the book of life: and the dead were judged out of those things which were written in the books, according to their works." (Rev 20:12)

And the figure who sits on that great white throne bears the Ancient of Days' identifying mark. Revelation 1:14 describes Christ: "His head and his hairs were white like wool, as white as snow" — the same wool-white hair that Daniel uses for the Ancient of Days. In Daniel, the Ancient of Days and the Son of Man are two distinct figures. Revelation merges them into one. Christ receives the kingdom as the Son of Man (Dan 7:13-14) and executes the judgment as the Ancient of Days (Dan 7:9-10).
