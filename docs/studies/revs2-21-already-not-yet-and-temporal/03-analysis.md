# Analysis: Revelation's Already/Not-Yet Temporal Framework

## Verse-by-Verse Analysis

---

### Rev 1:1 — En Tachei: The Book's Opening Temporal Claim

**Context:** The prologue frames the entire book's mandate — "things which must shortly come to pass" — using en tachei (dative of G5034 tachos). This single clause sets the temporal expectation for all 404 verses that follow.

**Direct statement:** The contents of Revelation carry an urgency marker from the first sentence. Whatever happens within the book, it is introduced as something that "must" (dei — present indicative of necessity) "shortly" (en tachei) "come to pass" (genesthai — aorist infinitive).

**Original language:** τάχει [N-DSN] G5034 — dative of tachos, "in swiftness/in a short time." BDAG gives "soon, in a short time" as the primary gloss for this verse. The infinitive genesthai (aorist of ginomai G1096) links to the entire ginomai arc running through the book. Critically, the phrase "ha dei genesthai" is verbatim from LXX Dan 2:28, which reads "ha dei genesthai ep' eschatōn tōn hēmerōn" — "what must come to pass in the last days." John substitutes "en tachei" for Daniel's "in the last days," converting a distance marker to a nearness marker (AN083, already registered).

**Cross-references:** The verbatim repetition at Rev 22:6 creates the prologue-epilogue frame (TM015, already registered). Dan 2:28 is the explicit source text (AN083). The three-camp debate about en tachei — chronological imminence (Farrar, BDAG, Aland), prophetic imminence across all ages (Mounce, Thomas), and manner of execution (Walvoord, Rogers) — is documented in TM188 and IC071/IC072 from revs-39.

**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse is the anchor for the entire temporal debate. The structural observation that hypomonē calls (TM190, SP089) throughout the book presuppose a temporal extension argues against strict immediate fulfillment. But the BDAG lexical support for "soon, in a short time" argues against reducing the phrase to mere manner-of-action. Both senses appear to be present.

### Rev 1:3 — Ho Kairos Engus: First Inclusio Marker

**Context:** First beatitude of Revelation. The blessing is given to those who read, hear, and keep the prophecy — grounded in the temporal declaration "for the time is at hand."

**Direct statement:** καιρὸς ἐγγύς — the appointed prophetic season is near. This is the book's opening temporal frame statement alongside the en tachei of v.1.

**Original language:** καιρὸς [N-NSM] G2540 + ἐγγύς [ADV] G1451. The choice of kairos over chronos is significant — kairos marks appointed prophetic seasons rather than measured elapsed time (a distinction established in revs2-18 and confirmed in the G2540 word study). G1451 engus: 30 NT occurrences; the Revelation pair (1:3, 22:10) creates the book-spanning inclusio (TM017, already registered). Hebrew source H7138 with PMI 7.99.

**Cross-references:** The identical formula "ho kairos engus" closes the book at Rev 22:10, forming the inclusio. Phil 4:5 uses engus of the Lord's nearness in a manner that could be temporal, relational, or both — the word does not restrict itself to one semantic range.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The kairos (appointed season) rather than chronos (elapsed duration) reinforces that the book declares a prophetically appointed time has arrived, not merely that clock-time is short. This aligns with the already-inaugurated-but-not-consummated framework.

### Rev 1:4 — Three-Element Divine Formula: Full Form

**Context:** Epistolary prescript. The divine self-description uses three elements: "who is and who was and who is to come."

**Direct statement:** God is described across all three temporal dimensions simultaneously — present being (ho ōn, present participle), past being (ho ēn, imperfect indicative), and anticipated coming (ho erchomenos, present participle of erchomai G2064).

**Original language:** ὢν [V-PAP-NSM] G1510 Pres Act Ptcp — "which is." ἦν [V-IAI-3S] G1510 Impf Act Ind — "which was." ἐρχόμενος [V-PNP-NSM] G2064 Pres M/P Ptcp — "which is to come." The erchomenos element is a present participle meaning "the coming one" — an ongoing approach, not merely a future fact. This form appears again at 1:8 and 4:8.

**Cross-references:** The dropping of ho erchomenos at Rev 11:17 and Rev 16:5 is the key structural moment in the divine formula reduction arc. When the coming element drops, the text signals that the anticipated arrival has been realized in that context.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The full three-element formula establishes the baseline from which the later reductions will be measured. The progression from three-element (1:4, 1:8, 4:8) to two-element (11:17, 16:5) is the divine name encoding of the already/not-yet.

### Rev 1:8 — Three-Element Divine Formula with Pantokrator

**Context:** Divine self-declaration. The full three-element formula appears again with the added title Pantokrator (Almighty).

**Direct statement:** Same three-element formula as 1:4, reinforcing the baseline before the reductions at 11:17 and 16:5.

**Original language:** ὢν + ἦν + ἐρχόμενος [V-PNP-NSM] G2064 + Παντοκράτωρ [N-NSM] G3841. The Pantokrator title emphasizes divine sovereignty — all power belongs to God regardless of how the earthly situation appears. This grounds the "already" claims that follow.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The Pantokrator element here gains force when contrasted with the beast's titles in Rev 13 — the beast's authority is explicitly derivative (edothē passives), while God's authority is inherent and self-declared.

### Rev 1:9 — Hypomonē Co-Partaken: First Occurrence

**Context:** John's self-introduction. He describes himself as "companion in tribulation, and in the kingdom and patience of Jesus Christ."

**Direct statement:** The triad "tribulation + kingdom + patience" is the already/not-yet formula presented in personal terms. John lives simultaneously in tribulation (present earthly reality), kingdom (inaugurated but not consummated), and hypomonē (the required posture of the interval).

**Original language:** ὑπομονή [G5281] — patient endurance. The G5281 word study documents 32 NT occurrences, with 7 in Revelation (highest density at 1.7 per 100 verses). Hebrew source H4723 miqveh with PMI 9.98. The co-occurrence of hypomonē with basileia (kingdom) and thlipsis (tribulation) in the same phrase is theologically precise: the kingdom is present enough to be shared in, yet the tribulation continues to require endurance.

**Cross-references:** The subsequent hypomonē occurrences (2:2, 2:3, 2:19, 3:10, 13:10, 14:12) build on this opening co-participation framework. The structural argument (SP089, already registered) is that hypomonē calls throughout the book presuppose temporal extension — patient endurance is meaningless if all events are immediate.

**Relationship to other evidence:** This first occurrence establishes John as a model of the already/not-yet existence he calls the churches to: exiled for witness, sharing the kingdom, exercising endurance. The structural function of hypomonē as a not-yet marker begins here.

### Rev 2:2-3 — Hypomonē Commended in the Letters

**Context:** Letter to Ephesus. Hypomonē is commended twice in three verses ("thy patience" v.2, "hast patience" v.3).

**Direct statement:** The Ephesian church's endurance is both a record of past acts (ebastasas, aorist — "hast borne") and an ongoing state (hypomonēn echeis, present indicative — "hast patience"). Both aspects of hypomonē — completed acts of bearing and ongoing state of endurance — are present.

**Original language:** G5281 hypomonē used twice. The aorist/present contrast here mirrors the already/not-yet: past acts of faithfulness ground a present state of endurance that continues.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The repeated commendation in the letters establishes hypomonē as a structural virtue expected throughout the churches. Combined with the tachy threats (2:5, 2:16), it creates the already/not-yet tension within the letters themselves.

---

### Rev 2:5 — Tachu: Conditional-Disciplinary Use

**Context:** Letter to Ephesus. Conditional threat: "I will come unto thee quickly" if they do not repent.

**Direct statement:** ταχύ [ADV] G5035 appears here in a conditional disciplinary context — the "coming quickly" is a threatened intervention against non-repentance, not the Parousia.

**Original language:** The conditional structure ("or else I will come") limits this tachy to a specific disciplinary action, not the eschatological final coming. This is the conditional-disciplinary category (TM189 taxonomy, already registered).

**Cross-references:** Acts 22:18 uses tachy of urgent departure — a non-eschatological speed reference. The semantic range of tachy allows both manner-of-action and temporal meaning depending on context.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The conditional uses in the letters (2:5, 2:16) demonstrate that tachy is not inherently eschatological — it can refer to local, specific, disciplinary interventions. This complicates any single-valence reading of the epilogue tachy declarations, though the epilogue context differs.

### Rev 2:16 — Tachu: Conditional-Disciplinary, Pergamos

**Context:** Letter to Pergamos. Second conditional tachy threat.

**Direct statement:** "I will come unto thee quickly" — same conditional structure as 2:5. The "sword of my mouth" verbal judgment connects to the Christophany at 1:16.

**Original language:** ταχύ [ADV] G5035. Identical construction to 2:5. The disciplinary use establishes that the "coming quickly" vocabulary operates at multiple levels — not only the final Parousia.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The two conditional uses in letters 2 and 3 bracket the middle of the seven-letter sequence, suggesting the "I come quickly" vocabulary is built into the very structure of the ongoing church age, not only the eschatological climax.

### Rev 3:10 — Hypomonē as Covenant Condition

**Context:** Letter to Philadelphia. The church's past hypomonē (keeping "the word of my patience") conditions a future divine keeping.

**Direct statement:** The conditional structure is temporal: "Because thou hast kept [aorist] the word of my patience, I also will keep thee [future] from the hour of temptation."

**Original language:** G5281 hypomonē — described as belonging to Christ ("my patience"), indicating that the church participates in Christ's own endurance. The future keeping (tēresō — I will keep) is the divine response to the church's present endurance.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The "hour of temptation which shall come upon all the world" is future from this vantage, confirming that hypomonē is required precisely because the consummation has not yet arrived. This is the structural not-yet.

---

### Rev 3:11 — Tachu: Transitional Promise

**Context:** Letter to Philadelphia. "Behold, I come quickly: hold that fast which thou hast."

**Direct statement:** This tachy appears mid-letters as the first non-conditional tachy declaration — it functions as a promise of encouragement rather than a threat, bridging the letters and the visions.

**Original language:** ταχύ [ADV] G5035. The shift from conditional (2:5, 2:16) to unconditional promise (3:11) marks a transition in how the vocabulary functions. The attached imperative "hold fast" reveals that the tachy does not eliminate the need for active endurance — it grounds it.

**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the transitional category of TM189 (tachy taxonomy). The "I come quickly" here establishes the pattern that will be repeated three times in the epilogue (22:7, 22:12, 22:20).

### Rev 3:21 — Nikao Aorist/Present Contrast: Christ's Victory Grounds Believer's Ongoing Overcoming

**Context:** Letter to Laodicea, seventh and climactic overcomer promise. Christ's completed victory grounds the promise to ongoing overcomers.

**Direct statement:** "To him that overcometh [nikōn — present participle] will I grant to sit with me in my throne, even as I also overcame [enikesan — aorist] and am set down with my Father in his throne."

**Original language:** νικῶν [V-PAP-NSM] G3528 Pres Act Ptcp — the believer's overcoming is ongoing. ἐνίκησα [V-AAI-1S] G3528 Aor Act Ind 1S — Christ's own overcoming is a completed historical act. This is TM195 nikao taxonomy (already registered): Christ's self-originated aorist grounds the believer's present participle. The tense contrast is the already/not-yet in grammatical form.

**Cross-references:** Rev 5:5 (enikēsen, same aorist, third person) — the Lion's completed victory. John 16:33 (nenikēka, perfect active — "I have overcome") — the perfect tense there marks ongoing relevance of the completed victory.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The asymmetry is structural. Christ's aorist is indicative (he acted); the believer's present is participial (they are in process). The ground of promise is the completeness of Christ's action; the condition of fulfillment is the continuity of the believer's response.

### Rev 4:8 — Three-Element Divine Formula in Throne Room

**Context:** Throne room vision. The four living creatures declare the divine formula continuously.

**Direct statement:** The three-element formula (was, is, is to come) appears here with past listed first — ἦν (was) + ὢν (is) + ἐρχόμενος (is to come). All three elements present.

**Original language:** Same G1510/G2064 elements as 1:4 and 1:8, confirming the three-element baseline before the later reductions.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The incessant praise ("they rest not day and night") indicates the formula is not a temporal sequence but a comprehensive description of God's eternal being. The "coming one" element is part of God's identity, not a calendar projection. Its later absence is therefore theologically significant.

### Rev 5:1-5 — Sealed Scroll and the Lion Who Has Prevailed

**Context:** Throne room. The sealed scroll of Rev 5:1 echoes Dan 12:4. The Lion has "prevailed" (aorist) — a completed act. His opening of the seals mirrors the Daniel sealed/unsealed contrast.

**Direct statement:** ἐνίκησεν [V-AAI-3S] G3528 Aor Act Ind 3S — "hath prevailed" (nikao aorist). The Lion's qualification to open the sealed scroll rests entirely on a past completed victory.

**Original language:** The sealed scroll (sphragís G4973, katasphragizō at 5:1) connects to Dan 12:4's chatham (H2856) commands. Christ's opening of the seals is the epistemological unsealing — the SP090 seal progression (already registered) begins here.

**Cross-references:** The sealed scroll at Rev 5:1 and Daniel's sealed book at Dan 12:4 are related through the sphragizō/chatham vocabulary chain (G4972 word study).

**Relationship to other evidence:** The Lion's completed aorist victory is the "already" that licenses the unveiling of what was sealed for Daniel's future. The Lamb who opens the seals is simultaneously the historical victor and the present revealer.

### Rev 5:9-10 — New Song: Proleptic Aorist Redemption and Future Reign

**Context:** Throne room worship. ēgorasas (redeemed) and epoiēsas (made) are aorists referring to the completed cross-work. basileusousin (shall reign) is future.

**Direct statement:** The cross-work is completed (aorist); the reign is anticipated (future). Past acts of redemption ground a future reign. This is the already/not-yet pattern in hymnic form.

**Original language:** ἠγόρασας [V-AAI-2S] G59 Aor Act Ind 2S — "hast redeemed" (completed act). ἐποίησας [V-AAI-2S] G4160 Aor Act Ind 2S — "hast made" (completed act). βασιλεύσουσιν [V-FAI-3P] G936 Fut Act Ind 3P — "shall reign" (future). VP178 (already registered) covers the blood/slain + basileian pattern.

**Cross-references:** Rev 22:5 (basileusousin, future — the saints' reign continues into eternity) and Rev 1:5-6 (the initial completed-work declaration) frame this pattern at the book's opening and closing.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The "already" of redemption (aorist) and the "not yet" of reigning (future) within a single hymn is the clearest compact expression of the framework in the early chapters.

### Rev 6:9-11 — Seal 5: Martyrs' Cry and Chronos Mikron

**Context:** Fifth seal — martyrs under the altar. The "how long?" cry and the "little season" response.

**Direct statement:** The martyrs' cry "How long?" is the voice of the not-yet gap. The answer "rest yet for a little season" (chronon mikron G5550) places an explicit temporal extension on the not-yet interval.

**Original language:** χρόνον μικρόν — "a little time" (G5550 chronos + G3398 mikros). The G5550 word study categorizes this as "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" — all four Revelation chronos occurrences carry this sense. The divine passive ἐδόθη at v.11 (robes given) parallels the edothē passives that govern the beast's authority (13:5,7) — both the martyrs' consolation and the beast's authority come through divine permission.

**Cross-references:** The parallel "chronon mikron" (or oligon) at Rev 20:3 marks the dragon's release period. The martyrs wait a chronon mikron while the dragon has an oligon kairon (12:12) — both are bounded not-yet intervals.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The martyrs' heavenly perspective (they see the throne, they are given robes, they are told to rest) contrasts with the earthly suffering of those yet to be killed. This is SP094's heaven-earth dual perspective operating at the seal level.

### Rev 6:17 — Ēlthen Hē Hēmera: Day of Wrath Declared Come at Seal 6

**Context:** Sixth seal. The terrified earth-dwellers declare the day of wrath "is come" — though the bowls are still chapters away.

**Direct statement:** ἦλθεν [V-2AAI-3S] G2064 Aor Act Ind 3S — "is come" (the great day of wrath). The declaration precedes the actual bowl judgments. This is VP174 (already registered).

**Original language:** The ēlthen formula here (ēlthen hē hēmera hē megalē) is one of several proleptic aorist announcements of arrived judgment across the book. The earth-dwellers' confession that the day has arrived is itself proleptic — they see the seal events and interpret them as the final judgment, though the full sequence continues.

**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse confirms that the "day is come" announcement can occur well before the final events it announces — consistent with the proleptic aorist as a compositional device (SP093, already registered).

### Rev 7:9-17 — Great Multitude in Heaven: Already Perspective

**Context:** Interlude between seals 6 and 7. The great multitude stands before the throne in completed form.

**Direct statement:** The heavenly perspective shows the redeemed multitude as an already-present reality — they stand, they hold palms, they are clothed in white. The elder explains they "came out of great tribulation" (aorist — completed exit) and have washed their robes.

**Original language:** The dominance of aorist and perfect forms in vv.14-17 contrasts with the ongoing tribulation implied for those still on earth. "God shall wipe away all tears" (v.17 — future) points to a final state not yet fully realized.

**Cross-references:** This multitude stands in heaven while the earthly martyrs of Rev 6:9-11 wait under the altar. The dual perspective (heaven: already standing before the throne; earth: waiting a little season) is SP094 operating at the interlude level.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The interlude itself embodies the already/not-yet — it shows the completed state of the redeemed from heaven's perspective, inserted between the sixth seal (earth trembling) and the seventh seal (silence in heaven). The reader sees both perspectives simultaneously.

### Rev 10:1-7 — The Open Book and Chronos No Longer

**Context:** Interlude between trumpets 6 and 7. The little book is open (contrast sealed scroll of Rev 5). The oath declares chronos ouketi estai. The seven thunders are sealed — the only sealing in Revelation.

**Direct statement:** χρόνος οὐκέτι ἔσται — "time shall be no longer" (future indicative). The cessation of chronos marks the end of the delay interval. The mystery of God will be "finished" when the seventh angel sounds (v.7).

**Original language:** χρόνος [N-NSM] G5550 + οὐκέτι [ADV-N G3765] + ἔσται [V-FDI-3S] G1510 Fut Mid Ind 3S. The construction is a future-tense negation: chronos will cease to exist. Chronos = measured elapsed duration (not kairos = appointed season). Two competing readings: (1) "Time shall be no more" — the end of sequential historical time; (2) "There will be no more delay" — ESV/NASB reading. The lexical note from the G5550 word study is that chronos in all four Revelation occurrences functions as "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" — the cessation declared here is the cessation of the remaining not-yet interval.

**Cross-references:** Dan 12:7's lifted-hand oath is the structural parallel — the parallels file confirms the Dan 12:7 / Rev 10:5-6 connection. The angel's lifting of the hand to heaven (v.5) directly echoes Dan 12:7's lifted hand. The "mystery of God" to be finished at the seventh trumpet corresponds to the kingdom announcement of Rev 11:15.

**Relationship to other evidence:** Rev 10:4's sealing of the seven thunders is the only sealing in Revelation — the entire book is disclosure, not concealment, except for this one item. This single exception reinforces that Revelation's default mode is unsealing (contrast Daniel's default of sealing).

### Rev 11:14 — Tachu: Narrative-Sequential Use

**Context:** The second woe has passed; the third comes quickly.

**Direct statement:** ἀπῆλθεν [V-2AAI-3S] G565 Aor Act Ind 3S — "has passed" (second woe, completed). ἔρχεται [V-PNI-3S] G2064 Pres M/P Ind 3S — "cometh" (third woe, present ongoing). ταχύ [ADV] G5035 — "quickly" (manner or imminence). The past/future contrast is explicit in the grammar.

**Original language:** The aorist for the past woe and the present indicative for the coming woe — with tachy added — creates a deliberate temporal sequence marker. This is the narrative-sequential category of TM189.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The structure "second woe HAS PASSED [aorist] / third woe IS COMING [present] quickly" is a microcosm of the already/not-yet within the trumpet sequence itself. The book self-narrates its temporal progression.

### Rev 11:15 — Kingdom Announcement as Proleptic Aorist

**Context:** Third woe / seventh trumpet. The kingdom announcement uses aorist egeneto while the future reign is expressed as basileusei.

**Direct statement:** Ἐγένετο [V-2ADI-3S] G1096 Aor Mid Ind 3S — "are become" (kingdoms transferred — proleptic aorist). βασιλεύσει [V-FAI-3S] G936 Fut Act Ind 3S — "he shall reign" (future). The same verse declares the transfer accomplished and the reign anticipated.

**Original language:** The tense contrast within a single announcement is the clearest grammatical already/not-yet in the book: the transfer of the kingdoms is declared accomplished (aorist = past action) while the reign is expressed as future (future indicative). The context is heavenly voices in a hymnic register — the declarations are from heaven's perspective (VP176, already registered).

**Cross-references:** Rev 12:10 is the highest NT parallel (0.507 score) — both passages are heavenly kingdom-and-salvation declarations using similar aorist vocabulary. Dan 2:44 and 7:27 are the OT parallels for kingdom establishment. The revs2-12 study confirmed the hymn frame: 13 of 22 verbs in Rev 11:15-18 are aorists, the prophetic aorist as the grammatical mechanism (SP093, already registered).

**Relationship to other evidence:** The fact that the kingdom transfer is declared accomplished here while the beast still reigns (13:5-7) and the final battle still comes (19:11-21) is not a chronological error. It is a theological statement: from heaven's perspective the outcome is settled. The proleptic aorist announces the certain future as accomplished fact.

### Rev 11:17 — Two-Element Formula: Ho Erchomenos Absent

**Context:** 24 elders' response to the kingdom announcement. The KJV's "art to come" is a translation addition not in the Greek.

**Direct statement:** ὁ ὢν [V-PAP-NSM] + ὁ ἦν [V-IAI-3S] — two-element formula only. The "coming one" (ho erchomenos) is absent. εἴληφας [V-2RAI-2S] G2983 Perf Act Ind 2S — "hast taken" (power — perfect = completed with ongoing result). ἐβασίλευσας [V-AAI-2S] G936 Aor Act Ind 2S — "hast reigned" (ingressive aorist — the reign has begun).

**Original language:** The parsing confirms: ho erchomenos is ABSENT. The KJV adds "art to come" without Greek support. The perfect eilephas signals that the taking of great power is completed and its results are in effect. The ingressive aorist ebasileusas signals that the reign has commenced.

**Cross-references:** The second two-element formula appears at Rev 16:5 during the bowl judgments. The three-element baseline is at 1:4, 1:8, and 4:8. The reduction happens at the structural moments of realized arrival: 11:17 (seventh trumpet kingdom announcement) and 16:5 (bowl judgments in progress).

**Relationship to other evidence:** The dropping of ho erchomenos is Revelation's most precise temporal signal. When the "coming one" element drops from the divine name, the text communicates that "the coming" has been realized in the immediate context. The not-yet element of the divine identity disappears as the already expands. This is a new structural pattern not previously registered (new element for DB).

### Rev 11:18 — Ēlthen Orgē: Proleptic Wrath Arrival

**Context:** Part of the 24 elders' extended declaration at the seventh trumpet. Judgment announced as having arrived.

**Direct statement:** ἦλθεν [V-2AAI-3S] G2064 Aor Act Ind 3S — "is come" (wrath). The five-element programmatic map of Rev 12-22 (SP044 from revs2-12, already registered) is contained in this verse.

**Original language:** The ēlthen + subject = "X has come" formula is used here for orgē (wrath) at the seventh trumpet — the same formula used at 6:17 (ēlthen hē hēmera) and at 14:7, 14:15, 18:10 for hour/judgment. VP174 (already registered) covers the 6:17 / 11:18 pairing.

**Relationship to other evidence:** This verse is the programmatic hinge for the rest of the book. The kingdom is announced (v.15), the taking of power declared (v.17), and the full agenda of judgment/reward mapped (v.18) — all in aorist declarations that the subsequent narrative will elaborate.

### Rev 11:19 — Naos Opened: Theophany Escalation

**Context:** Immediate sequel to the seventh trumpet. The naos opens in heaven; the ark of the covenant becomes visible.

**Direct statement:** The heavenly naos opens — access to the ark becomes available. This contrasts with Rev 15:8 where the naos is closed until the bowls are finished.

**Original language:** The open/closed contrast (11:19 open → 15:8 closed → 16:17 gegonen/complete) tracks the judgment sequence. The ark's visibility connects to Exodus sanctuary imagery.

**Relationship to other evidence:** The open/closed naos sequence establishes a temporal framework within the judgment sequences: open access (already) → closed for execution (interim) → completed (declared at 16:17). This is part of the IC077 hybrid model's escalation pattern.

### Rev 12:7-9 — War in Heaven: Dragon Cast Out (Ballo Arc Phase 1)

**Context:** Rev 12 cosmic vision. The war in heaven and the dragon's expulsion. Aorist passives mark completed action; a present participle marks continuing activity.

**Direct statement:** ἐβλήθη [V-API-3S] G906 Aor Pass Ind 3S — "was cast out" (aorist passive, twice for singular dragon, and once plural for his angels). The casting is grammatically completed. πλανῶν [V-PAP-NSM] G4105 Pres Act Ptcp — "deceiveth" — the dragon's deceptive activity continues in present tense even after the completed casting.

**Original language:** The verb eblēthē (G906 ballō) is the first of three uses in the dragon's displacement arc (TM196, SP095 — already registered). The same root G906 is used at 20:3 (earth→pit) and 20:10 (pit→lake of fire). The present participle "deceiveth" is attached to the description of the cast-out dragon — his nature and activity continue even as his domain contracts.

**Cross-references:** John 12:31 uses ekblēthēsetai (future passive of G1544 ekballō — a cognate but distinct root from G906). The parallels file confirms this: John 12:31 = future passive of G1544 (Satan not yet cast from heaven's court perspective); Rev 12:9 = aorist passive of G906 (Satan cast, from heaven's retrospective). The same event, different temporal vantages, different vocabulary (cognate roots).

**Relationship to other evidence:** The casting is "already" in grammatical form, while the deception is "not yet" ended in present participial form. This is the already/not-yet encoded in the grammar of a single verse.

### Rev 12:10-12 — Heaven's Proclamation: Arti Egeneto and Dragon's Continuing Wrath

**Context:** Heavenly proclamation following the expulsion. The heaven perspective (v.10-11) and the earth-danger perspective (v.12) show the dual already/not-yet reality simultaneously.

**Direct statement:** Heaven declares: Ἄρτι [ADV] G737 — "NOW." ἐγένετο [V-2ADI-3S] G1096 — "is come" (salvation, proleptic/prophetic aorist). Earth is warned: κατέβη [V-2AAI-3S] G2597 — "is come down" (dragon, aorist). ἔχων [V-PAP-NSM] — "having" great wrath (present). ἔχει [V-PAI-3S] G2192 — "hath" but a short time (present).

**Original language:** Arti (G737) is the single Revelation occurrence of this word — it appears at the most theologically loaded moment: the heaven-perspective declaration of accomplished salvation following the dragon's expulsion. The G737 word study confirms: arti marks "just now, this moment, this very instant" — the strongest present-moment marker in Greek. The contrast with nyn (broader present) is significant: arti is punctiliar.

The grammatical structure of vv.10-12 is a dual perspective: heaven uses completed aorists (is come — egeneto; is cast down — eblēthē) while earth receives a description of ongoing present-tense reality (having wrath — ἔχων; knoweth — εἰδὼς perfect participle; hath a short time — ἔχει present). The same event is simultaneously accomplished (heaven) and intensifying (earth).

**Relationship to other evidence:** This is the clearest heaven-earth dual perspective passage in the book (SP094, already registered). ## Patterns Identified

### Pattern 1: The Ginomai Completion Arc

The verb ginomai (G1096) traces Revelation's temporal progression through three grammatical stages:

- Proleptic aorist egeneto (11:15; 12:10): declared as accomplished before narrative execution
- Perfect singular gegonen (16:17): bowl sequence completed, results in effect
- Perfect plural gegonan (21:6): all things accomplished at new creation

The movement from aorist (certainty declared) to perfect singular (sequence completed) to perfect plural (everything completed) is Revelation's own grammatical spine for the already/not-yet. TM197 / SP096 (already registered) catalog this arc.

### Pattern 2: The Nikao Tense Taxonomy

The verb nikaō (G3528) creates a complete taxonomy of the already/not-yet paradox:
- Christ's completed self-originated victory (aorist indicative: Rev 3:21, 5:5)
- Believer's ongoing overcoming (present participle: Rev 3:21, 21:7)
- Beast's permitted dependent conquest (aorist infinitive after edothē: Rev 13:7)
- Eschatological Lamb victory (future indicative: Rev 17:14)

TM195 / IC075 (already registered) catalog this taxonomy.

### Pattern 3: The Ballo Arc

The verb ballō (G906) traces the dragon's three-stage displacement:
- Heaven→earth (eblēthē aorist passive, 12:9)
- Earth→abyss (ébalen aorist active, 20:3)
- Abyss→lake of fire (eblēthē aorist passive, 20:10)

TM196 / SP095 (already registered) catalog this arc.

### Pattern 4: The Divine Formula Reduction

The divine temporal formula reduces from three elements (ho ōn + ho ēn + ho erchomenos, at 1:4, 1:8, 4:8) to two elements (ho ōn + ho ēn, erchomenos absent, at 11:17 and 16:5). The reduction occurs at the structural moments of realized arrival — the "coming one" element drops when the anticipated coming is realized in the immediate context.

### Pattern 5: The Hypomonē Cluster

Hypomonē (G5281) appears at structurally significant positions — especially in pairs (13:10 / 14:12) at the points of maximum eschatological tension during the beast's permitted authority. The calls to endurance are embedded precisely where the not-yet is most severe.

### Pattern 6: The Proleptic Hōra Formula

The hōra + ēlthen formula (judgment hour declared arrived — 14:7; harvest hour declared arrived — 14:15; Babylon's judgment declared arrived — 18:10) constitutes a structural series of proleptic declarations within narrative proclamation. Each uses the same aorist ēlthen to announce what has not yet been narratively executed.

### Pattern 7: The Daniel-Revelation Seal Inversion

The seal vocabulary creates a four-step canonical arc: Daniel seals for distance (Dan 12:4, chatham/satam imperatives) → Daniel describes the sealed state (Dan 12:9, chatham/satam participles) → Revelation seals only the seven thunders (Rev 10:4 — the one exception) → Revelation prohibits sealing because time is near (Rev 22:10, mē sphragisēs). SP090 / TM023 (already registered) catalog this arc.

### Pattern 8: The En Tachei / Tachu Frame

The en tachei inclusio (Rev 1:1 / 22:6, TM015) and the tachy vocabulary throughout (TM189, TM020) create an urgency-consciousness that pervades the entire book. The semantic range allows both temporal imminence and manner-of-action readings.

## Word Study Integration

### Ginomai (G1096)

The ginomai word study confirms that egeneto is the dominant form (233 of 668 forms = 34.9%). The proleptic aorist use at 11:15 and 12:10 (declared) vs. the perfect singular at 16:17 (completed) vs. perfect plural at 21:6 (all completed) represents Revelation's most concentrated temporal arc in a single verb.

28 NT occurrences — 17 in Revelation (61%). The tense taxonomy across the book's nikao occurrences (aorist indicative for Christ/heaven; present participle for believers; aorist infinitive after edothē for beast; future indicative for eschatological Lamb) encodes the entire already/not-yet framework within a single verb family.

### Ballō (G906)

127 NT occurrences; 23 in Revelation (highest density at 5.7 per 100 verses). The three-stage dragon displacement arc uses the same G906 root at each stage — verbal unity across chapters 12 and 20 that makes the arc explicit.

### Hypomonē (G5281)

32 NT occurrences — 7 in Revelation (highest density at 1.7 per 100 verses). The seven occurrences are not random — they cluster at structural points of tribulation narrative (letters, seal interlude, beast section). Hebrew source H4723 miqveh (hope/expectation vocabulary) suggests that hypomonē in Revelation is not mere stoic endurance but hope-grounded waiting.

36 NT occurrences — 1 in Revelation (12:10). The single Revelation use appears at the most theologically loaded moment in the book: the heaven-perspective declaration of accomplished salvation following the dragon's expulsion. The punctiliar "this very moment" sense of arti marks the arti egeneto as the sharpest already-marker in the book.

### Chronos (G5550) vs. Kairos (G2540)

All four Revelation chronos occurrences carry "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" meaning. All five Revelation kairos occurrences mark appointed prophetic seasons. The distinction governs Rev 10:6: it is chronos (measured duration) that ceases, not kairos (appointed seasons). The not-yet interval is a chronos-bounded period, not a kairos-bounded one.

### Engus (G1451) and En Tachei (G5034)

The engus inclusio (1:3 / 22:10) and the en tachei frame (1:1 / 22:6) are complementary but not identical: engus marks the appointed season as near (stative); en tachei marks the events as soon/swiftly (manner-of-action or temporal). Both contribute to the already/not-yet temporal consciousness without resolving the three-way debate between imminence readings.

## Difficult or Complicating Passages

### 1. The En Tachei / Tachu Debate

The three-position debate (chronological imminence / prophetic imminence across all ages / manner-of-action) is not fully resolved by lexical data alone. BDAG gives "soon, in a short time" for en tachei at Rev 1:1, supporting temporal imminence. Acts 12:7 and 22:18 demonstrate that tachy can mean manner-of-speed without temporal imminence. Rom 16:20 uses en tachei in a clear temporal context. The structural argument (SP089: hypomonē co-occurrence presupposes temporal extension) argues against strict immediate fulfillment. Isaiah 13:22 shows that OT prophetic "at hand" language can span centuries.

The most honest position is that both senses are present — the events are both temporally near (in the sense that the end-time fulfillment phase has been inaugurated by Christ's first advent) and swiftly executed (when they come, they come without delay). Neither sense fully excludes the other. IC071 (beginning of fulfillment phase) and IC072 (manner-of-action) both represent valid textual observations, as documented by revs-39. This study does not add a new interpretive claim beyond what IC071/IC072 already register.

### 2. The Timing of the Dragon's Casting Down

Rev 12:9-10 presents the casting as accomplished (aorists), and Rev 12:10's arti marks it as "now" from the heavenly perspective. Bohr and Beale both argue this corresponds to the cross event (John 12:31-33). The vocabulary connection is real (G1544 ekballō / G906 ballō are cognate), but not identical roots. Luke 10:18 ("Satan fall as lightning") is Jesus' pre-cross statement about the outcome already visible in the disciples' exorcisms.

The grammatical data supports that the casting of Rev 12:9 is presented as accomplished before the dragon's ongoing earthly activity in 12:13-17. Whether this corresponds specifically to the cross (Bohr, Beale, EGW) or to a primordial pre-creation expulsion or to the parousia is a chronological judgment the grammar itself does not resolve with precision. What the grammar clearly shows is: the casting is "already" (completed aorist), the earthly activity is "not yet ended" (present and imperfect tenses). The specific historical event that constitutes the "already" is the exegetical question the grammar opens but does not close.

### 3. The Beast's 42-Month Reign vs. Christ's Already-Completed Victory

The beast's edothē (divine passive, aorist) authority for 42 months operates concurrently with Christ's already-completed nikao (5:5, aorist). This is not a contradiction — the beast's authority is explicitly derivative (edothē = permitted) and bounded (42 months = built-in time limit). But the coexistence of Christ's completed victory and the beast's ongoing permitted conquest is the central tension of the book.

Beale's solution (the 42 months spans the entire church age from ascension) is consistent with the grammar: edothē (aorist passive) points to a past grant of authority (consistent with ascension), and the present-tense activity of the beast throughout Rev 13 supports an ongoing not-yet period. The IC077 hybrid model (revs2-20) accommodates this without requiring a specific dating of when the 42 months began.

### 4. Chronos at Rev 10:6

"Time shall be no longer" (chronos ouketi estai) is either (a) the end of measured sequential time itself (supported by the eschatological context) or (b) the end of the delay period before the mystery is finished (supported by the ESV rendering and Dan 12:7 parallel). The G5550 word study's identification of all four Revelation chronos occurrences as "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" supports reading (b) — the cessation declared is the cessation of the remaining delay interval, not the abolition of time itself. But reading (a) remains grammatically available. The Dan 12:7 oath parallel (parallels file confirmed) is the strongest contextual argument for reading (b): Daniel's oath is about when the 3.5-year period ends; Rev 10:6's oath echoes that structure, declaring the delay before the mystery is finished will end.

### 5. Revelation 11:17's KJV Addition

KJV's "art to come" at Rev 11:17 is a translation addition not supported by the Greek text. The parsing confirms: ὁ ὢν + ὁ ἦν only — two elements. The addition obscures one of the most significant structural signals in the book. Any analysis depending on the KJV text of 11:17 to support a full three-element reading misses the point of the reduction.

## Preliminary Synthesis

The evidence from all 76 verses points consistently in one direction: Revelation's already/not-yet temporal framework is not incidental to the book but architecturally embedded in its grammar, vocabulary, and structural design.

The grammatical evidence is the strongest pillar:
- Proleptic aorists systematically declare future events as accomplished (SP093)
- Perfect tenses mark completions whose results stand (gegonen, gegonan, eilephas, kekritai)
- Present participles mark ongoing activities in tension with completed acts (nikōn alongside enikesan; plananōn alongside eblēthē)
- The divine formula reduction (erchomenos dropped at 11:17 and 16:5) signals realized arrival at structural moments

The vocabulary evidence is the second pillar:
- Arti (G737) at Rev 12:10 marks the punctiliar "this moment" of realized salvation
- En tachei / tachu frame the entire book in urgency
- Hypomonē clusters at tribulation-peak moments, marking the not-yet structurally
- Chronos (measured duration) is distinguished from kairos (appointed season) throughout

The structural evidence is the third pillar:
- Heaven-earth dual perspective at every major seam (SP094) — heaven celebrates accomplished realities while earth endures ongoing tension
- The ballo arc spans chapters 12 to 20 with verbal unity (TM196)
- The nikao taxonomy spans the book's seven sections with grammatical precision (TM195)
- The Daniel sealed / Revelation unsealed contrast creates a canonical already/not-yet at the level of prophetic disclosure itself (SP090)

Revelation does not present events as either strictly past or strictly future. It presents the decisive victory as already accomplished (from heaven's perspective, in Christ's aorist victories, in the proleptic hymns), while the full earthly realization remains not-yet (beast still reigns, dragon still rages, saints still endure). These are not two separate timelines — they are two perspectives on the same reality. The book is written to help its readers inhabit both perspectives simultaneously.
