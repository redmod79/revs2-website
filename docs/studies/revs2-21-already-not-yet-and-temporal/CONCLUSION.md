# Revelation's Already/Not-Yet Temporal Framework

## Question

How does Revelation construct its already/not-yet temporal framework through verb tense, vocabulary, and structural markers — and what does this reveal about the book's eschatological logic?

---

## Summary Answer

Revelation is not simply a book about the future. It is a book that holds the already-accomplished and the not-yet-realized in deliberate, architecturally constructed tension. This tension is not the result of prophetic imprecision or inconsistent authorship. It is the theological point. The book's Greek verb tenses, its selection of specific vocabulary across repeated structural positions, its heaven-earth dual-perspective passages, its progressive completion markers, and its canonical dialogue with the book of Daniel all work together to declare one thing: the decisive victory has already occurred in Christ, and the full earthly realization of that victory is not yet complete. Every believer living between the first and second advents exists in this interval — the dragon defeated but still raging, the kingdom announced but saints still enduring, the judgment hour declared arrived yet the bowls still being poured.

## Analysis

### The Proleptic Aorist as Revelation's Primary Compositional Device

The most significant structural device in Revelation's temporal framework is the proleptic aorist — the use of Greek aorist (past tense) to declare future events from a perspective of divine certainty. This is not a grammatical error or inconsistency. It is a recognized device with documented OT precedent and consistent NT usage.

The OT precedent is the Hebrew prophetic perfect (qatal tense). Isaiah 21:9 uses naphal naphal (fallen, fallen — qatal doublet) to announce Babylon's fall as accomplished before the event. Isaiah 53:4-5 uses qatal forms throughout to describe the Servant's future suffering as if already past. The LXX translates these qatal forms with Greek aorists — creating the formal precedent that Revelation inherits. When Revelation at Rev 14:8 and 18:2 says epesen epesen Babylōn (fallen, fallen is Babylon), it is consciously echoing Isaiah 21:9's doublet in the same verb (G4098 piptō = LXX rendering of naphal). The 80% aorist-form dominance in the G4098 word study across 93 NT occurrences confirms this verb family carries punctiliar-completed-action force.

Paul uses the same device in Romans 8:30: "them he also glorified" — an aorist for future believers, treating certain future glory as accomplished past. This Pauline precedent shows the proleptic aorist is not unique to Revelation's style but belongs to the common NT toolbox for declaring certain future realities from heaven's perspective.

Within Revelation itself, the proleptic aorists form a consistent series:

At Rev 6:17, the earth-dwellers cry "the great day of his wrath is come" (ēlthen — aorist of erchomai G2064) — this declaration precedes the bowl judgments of chapters 15-16 by nine chapters of narrative.

At Rev 11:15, egeneto (G1096 aorist) declares the kingdoms "are become" the Lord's while the beast has not yet been introduced and the final battle is eight chapters away.

At Rev 12:10, egeneto again announces "now is come salvation, and the kingdom of our God" — an aorist announcement made while the dragon is actively persecuting (12:13-17).

At Rev 14:8, epesen epesen announces Babylon fallen three chapters before the Babylon narrative begins.

At Rev 18:2, the same doublet opens the Babylon narrative — announcing again what has not yet been executed.

At Rev 19:6-7, ebasileusen (G936 aorist) and ēlthen (aorist of erchomai G2064) declare the kingdom commenced and the marriage arrived in a hymn that precedes the final battle narration.

The pattern is not random. Proleptic aorists appear specifically in the mouth of heavenly voices, angelic proclamations, and liturgical hymns — the contexts where heaven's perspective is given direct voice. The contrast with present-tense and imperfect-tense verbs in earthly narrative descriptions is deliberate: heaven speaks in completed forms; earth experiences ongoing processes. This is the structural grammar of the already/not-yet.

### The Ginomai Completion Arc

The verb ginomai (G1096, 702 NT occurrences — 87 in Revelation, 12.4% density) traces Revelation's own temporal progression through three grammatical stages. The same verb that declares the kingdom come (aorist) also declares the sequence completed (perfect singular) and finally declares everything accomplished (perfect plural).

Stage 1: Proleptic Aorist Declaration (Rev 11:15; 12:10). Egeneto (aorist, V-2ADI-3S) declares the kingdoms transferred and salvation arrived. The certainty is complete from heaven's vantage; the earthly execution continues.

Stage 2: Perfect Singular Completion (Rev 16:17). Gegonen (perfect singular, V-2RAI-3S) — "It is done" — announces the bowl judgment sequence completed. The perfect tense in Greek marks a completed action whose results remain in effect. This is not merely another announcement but a distinct grammatical upgrade: from proleptic certainty (aorist) to completed result (perfect).

Stage 3: Perfect Plural Completion (Rev 21:6). Gegonan (perfect plural, V-2RAI-3P) — "It is done" again, but in plural. This is the comprehensive completion — not just the judgment sequence but all things accomplished in the new creation.

The move from aorist (11:15, 12:10) to perfect singular (16:17) to perfect plural (21:6) is Revelation's own built-in grammar for the eschatological progression. The book does not simply declare things done and then narrate them — it uses grammatically distinct completion levels to mark the escalating realization of what was first declared in proleptic aorists. This is TM197 / SP096 (revs-38 study).

The three-stage arc also parallels the cross-language completion markers: John 19:30 tetelestai (perfect — "it is finished" at the cross) → Rev 16:17 gegonen (perfect — "it is done" at bowl completion) → Rev 21:6 gegonan (perfect plural — "it is done" at new creation). Cross, judgment, new creation — marked with progressive perfect completions in Johannine theological writing.

### The Divine Formula Reduction

One of the most precise temporal signals in Revelation is structural — not a single verse but a pattern across six appearances of the divine self-description formula.

The full three-element formula appears at Rev 1:4, 1:8, and 4:8: ho ōn (who is) + ho ēn (who was) + ho erchomenos (who is to come). All three elements. The "coming one" is built into the divine identity as an anticipated future dimension.

At Rev 11:17 and Rev 16:5, the formula appears with only two elements: ho ōn + ho ēn. The third element — ho erchomenos — is absent. This is not an accident of transmission or a compressed reference. The Greek parsing confirms it: there is no ho erchomenos in either verse. The KJV adds "art to come" at Rev 11:17 without Greek support — a translation interpretation rather than translation. The two-element formula is what the Greek text actually says.

The structural significance is substantial. The three-element formula includes the "coming one" because from the perspective of 1:4, 1:8, and 4:8 (the opening visions), the divine "coming" is anticipated but not yet realized. The formula holds the future arrival as an element of God's temporal identity.

At Rev 11:17, the elders' response to the seventh trumpet's kingdom announcement drops ho erchomenos because in that hymnic declaration, the coming is realized. The perfect tense eilephas (hast taken) and the ingressive aorist ebasileusas (hast reigned) at the same verse confirm that the kingdom arrival is the grammatical context — the reign has begun. The anticipated "coming one" is no longer anticipated; the arriving has arrived.

The same reduction at Rev 16:5 occurs during the bowl judgments — where the completed wrath (aorist ekrīnas, thou hast judged) makes the "coming one" element unnecessary. The divine name shrinks precisely as its forward-looking element is fulfilled.

This is the deepest structural encoding of the already/not-yet in Revelation. The divine identity itself is presented as having a "not yet" element (the erchomenos of 1:4, 1:8, 4:8) which is progressively realized (absent at 11:17 and 16:5) as the eschatological sequence unfolds. God is not changing — the representation of God's temporal relationship to human history changes as the already expands.

### The Nikao Tense Taxonomy

The verb nikaō (G3528, 29 NT occurrences, 17 in Revelation — 59% density) creates a complete already/not-yet taxonomy across the book through its different tense forms and grammatical constructions. Each category carries a distinct theological meaning:

**Christ's self-originated completed victory (aorist indicative):** Rev 3:21 enikesan (V-AAI-1S) and Rev 5:5 enikēsen (V-AAI-3S). Christ's overcoming is expressed in the aorist indicative — a first-person and third-person completed action. The aorist indicative is self-originated action: Christ acted, he overcame, it is done. John 16:33's nenikēka (perfect active) describes the same reality with emphasis on the ongoing resultant state.

**The believer's ongoing overcoming (present participle):** Rev 3:21 nikōn (V-PAP-NSM) and Rev 21:7 ho nikōn (V-PAP-NSM). The believer's overcoming is in present participial form — ongoing, continuous action. The contrast within Rev 3:21 alone is structurally explicit: "to him that overcometh [present participle] will I grant, even as I also overcame [aorist indicative]." Christ's completed act grounds an ongoing believer response.

**The beast's permitted dependent conquest (aorist infinitive under edothē):** Rev 13:7 nikēsai (V-AAN) under edothē (V-API-3S). The beast's overcoming is expressed as an aorist active infinitive governed by the divine passive edothē (it was given). This grammatical construction is the theological opposite of Christ's self-originated aorist indicative: the beast's conquest depends on sovereign permission, while Christ's victory is inherent and self-originated. Same verb, opposite grammatical authority.

**The eschatological Lamb victory (future indicative):** Rev 17:14 nikēsei (V-FAI-3S). The Lamb "shall overcome" — future tense, securing the final eschatological victory whose "already" ground is the aorist victories of 3:21 and 5:5.

The arc from Christ's aorist (already, self-originated) to the believer's present participle (not yet, ongoing) to the beast's infinitive (not yet, permitted) to the eschatological future (guaranteed not-yet) is the entire already/not-yet structure in a single verb family across seventeen occurrences.

### The Ballo Arc

The verb ballō (G906, 127 NT occurrences, 23 in Revelation — 18% density) traces the dragon's progressive confinement through three uses of the same root in three distinct narrative contexts eight chapters apart.

Phase 1 (Rev 12:9): eblēthē (V-API-3S) — aorist passive. The dragon is cast from heaven to earth. The passive voice indicates an external agent acting on the dragon. Heaven → earth.

Phase 2 (Rev 20:3): ébalen (V-2AAI-3S) — aorist active. An angel casts the dragon into the abyss. The active voice marks a different agent-grammar: an angel acts directly. Earth → abyss.

Phase 3 (Rev 20:10): eblēthē (V-API-3S) — aorist passive again. The dragon is cast into the lake of fire. Divine passive resumes for the final disposal. Abyss → lake of fire.

The return to the passive voice at phase 3 mirrors phase 1 — both use the same grammatical form (divine passive of G906) for the beginning and end of the arc, creating verbal bookends. Phase 2's active is the intermediate step where an angelic agent acts as direct instrument.

The verbal unity across chapters 12 and 20 is not accidental. John chose the same root (G906 ballō) for all three stages when he could have used different vocabulary. The choice creates a tracking mechanism: the same verb follows the dragon through his three-stage displacement over eight chapters, making the arc explicit through lexical repetition. The dragon's "fall" from heaven is not the end — it is stage 1 of a progressive confinement that ends only at the lake of fire.

Critically, the dragon at Rev 12:9 is described with a present participle: "the deceiver of the whole world" (plananōn, V-PAP-NSM) — ongoing deceptive activity described in present tense immediately after the completed casting. And at Rev 20:10, the same participial description appears: "that deceived" (present participial in Greek). The dragon's nature and activity as deceiver is present-tense throughout the arc — only the spatial domain changes. The "already" is the completed casting; the "not yet" is the ongoing deception.

### The Heaven-Earth Dual Perspective

At every major structural seam in Revelation, heavenly declarations of accomplished reality are juxtaposed with earthly descriptions of ongoing tension. This is not a problem to be resolved by harmonization — it is the architectural principle.

Rev 12:10-12 is the clearest example. Verse 10 speaks from heaven, using aorists: "Now [arti — this very moment] is come [egeneto] salvation, and the kingdom of our God... for the accuser of our brethren is cast down [eblēthē]." Verse 12 speaks to the earth, using present tenses and participles: "Woe to the inhabiters of the earth... for the devil is come down unto you, having great wrath [echōn — present participle], because he knoweth [eidōs — perfect participle] that he hath [echei — present indicative] but a short time."

The same event (the dragon's casting) is celebrated in heaven as accomplished (aorists) and dreaded on earth as an intensifying threat (presents). The contrast is not between two different events but two perspectives on the same one. Heaven sees the completed displacement; earth experiences the increased pressure that results from it. Both are true simultaneously. This is SP094 (revs-38 study).

The same dual-perspective structure appears at:
- Rev 5 (throne room: sealed scroll, Lamb prevailed) vs. the seals of chapters 6-8 (earthly tribulation)
- Rev 7 (great multitude standing in heaven) vs. the martyrs waiting under the altar (6:9-11)
- Rev 11:15-18 (heavenly kingdom declaration) vs. the beast's coming authority in chapter 13
- Rev 19:1-8 (hallelujah for accomplished judgment) vs. the mourning of Rev 18:9-19

John alternates systematically between the two perspectives, teaching the reader to hold both simultaneously. The heavenly declarations are not premature — they are the correct view of the settled cosmic reality. The earthly experience is not deceptive — it is the real not-yet interval that the heavenly reality has not yet fully expressed on the ground.

### The Hypomonē Structural Marker

The word hypomonē (G5281, 32 NT occurrences, 7 in Revelation) appears at structurally significant positions throughout the book, and its placement is not random. Patient endurance is called for precisely at the points where the not-yet is most pressing.

The seven Revelation occurrences cluster as follows: three in the letters to the churches (1:9; 2:2,3,19; 3:10), two in the heart of the beast narrative with explicit structural placement (13:10; 14:12), and the remaining one in the middle of the letter section (2:2 and 2:3 count as two of the seven). The structural pair at 13:10 and 14:12 is particularly significant.

At Rev 13:10, immediately after the beast's edothē authority is granted and his war against the saints is permitted (13:5-7), the text says: "Here is the patience and the faith of the saints." The formula places hypomonē as the appropriate response to beast-authority.

At Rev 14:12, immediately after the three angels announce the judgment hour (14:7), Babylon fallen (14:8), and the doom of the mark-of-beast recipients (14:9-11), the text says again: "Here is the patience of the saints: here are they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus." The formula appears after eschatological declarations of accomplished judgment.

The structural logic is this: when heaven declares the judgment arrived (proleptic aorists), the earthly saints are called to hypomonē — because the not-yet has not ended. The proleptic declarations do not make endurance unnecessary. They ground it. The saints endure because they know the outcome is settled, not because the outcome has already been experienced on earth. This is the practical not-yet.

The co-occurrence of hypomonē with "here" (hōde) creates an explicit structural marker: "Here [in this moment, in this condition] is the endurance of the saints." The calls to patience are embedded at tribulation-peak moments to tell the saints what disposition is appropriate for the interval they are living in.

### The Daniel Sealed / Revelation Unsealed Contrast

One of the most structurally important already/not-yet arguments in Revelation is canonical rather than internal. The contrast between Daniel's sealing commands and Revelation's unsealing prohibition creates a cross-book temporal arc that marks the entire NT canon as the fulfillment-phase of what Daniel awaited.

The arc has four steps:

Dan 8:26: "shut thou up the vision; for it shall be for many days" (satam H5640, Qal imperative). Distance marker: "many days."

Dan 12:4: "shut up the words and seal the book, even to the time of the end" (satam + chatham, both Qal imperatives). Distance marker shifted: "time of the end" — more defined than "many days" but still future.

Dan 12:9: "the words are closed up and sealed till the time of the end" (satam + chatham, both Qal participles). The imperatives of 12:4 are now described as accomplished states. The sealing is in effect.

Rev 22:10: "seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book: for the time is at hand" (mē sphragisēs — prohibitive aorist subjunctive of G4972 sphragizō). Distance marker reversed: "near" (engus G1451) — not "many days," not "time of end," but "time is near."

The vocabulary chain works through the LXX: Daniel's chatham (H2856) is rendered by the LXX with sphragizō (G4972) — the same root John uses in his prohibitive mē sphragisēs at Rev 22:10. John is deliberately inverting Daniel's command using Daniel's own vocabulary. Where Daniel is told "seal," John is told "do not seal." The reason for the inversion is the temporal shift: Daniel seals because fulfillment is distant; John does not seal because the appointed season is near.

The "ho kairos engus" formula at Rev 1:3 and Rev 22:10 creates the book-level inclusio (TM017, revs-39 study) that frames this unsealing stance as the book's consistent posture. Revelation does not conceal — it discloses. The entire book is written to communicate what Daniel was told to hide. SP090 / TM023 (already registered) document this pattern.

The canonical implication is significant. Daniel lived before the "time of end" that his visions anticipated. John lives after the events that inaugurated the fulfillment phase — the cross, the resurrection, the ascension. The "time is near" at Rev 22:10 is not the same as "the end has come." It is the declaration that the fulfillment phase has begun — the not-yet of Daniel is now the already-inaugurated of Revelation.

### The En Tachei / Tachu Temporal Frame

The vocabulary of swiftness and nearness creates the book's urgency-consciousness from beginning to end.

En tachei (G5034, dative of tachos) appears at Rev 1:1 and 22:6, creating the prologue-epilogue frame (TM015, revs-38 study). The entire book's contents are introduced as things that must "shortly" come to pass. BDAG's primary gloss for this phrase at Rev 1:1 is "soon, in a short time" — the temporal-imminence reading. Romans 16:20 uses the same en tachei in a clearly temporal context ("God shall bruise Satan shortly"), providing Pauline precedent for the temporal reading.

Tachu (G5035, the adverb) appears six times across the book with three distinct uses:
- Conditional-disciplinary (Rev 2:5, 2:16): "I will come unto thee quickly" — if they do not repent, a specific disciplinary intervention follows.
- Transitional-promise (Rev 3:11): "Behold, I come quickly: hold that fast which thou hast" — encouragement that grounds ongoing faithfulness.
- Eschatological-epilogue (Rev 22:7, 22:12, 22:20): Three declarations in the closing chapter — the final "I come quickly" triplet.

The progression through the three categories is itself a temporal arc: disciplinary intervention (local and immediate) → transitional promise (encouragement for the interval) → eschatological return (final event). The word tachy does the semantic work appropriate to its context in each category.

The community's response to the final tachy at 22:20 — "Even so, come, Lord Jesus" — is the liturgical not-yet. The response is petition, not reception. The community is not greeting an arrival but praying for one. This shows that the eschatological tachu declarations are not describing something already complete but are prompting active hope for the not-yet arrival.

The contrast between Acts 12:7 and 22:18 (non-eschatological manner-of-speed uses of tachy) and Rev 22:7, 12, 20 (eschatological epilogue uses) confirms that the word's meaning is context-dependent. The epilogue context — the close of the book, Christ's direct speech, the community's petitionary response — argues that the epilogue tachy declarations carry temporal-imminence meaning alongside manner-of-action meaning. The scholarly debate between Walvoord (manner-of-action), Mounce (prophetic imminence), and Farrar/Aland (chronological nearness) is genuine, but the community's marana tha response is the most direct textual evidence for how the first readers understood the declaration.

### Chronos, Kairos, and the Temporal Structure of the Not-Yet

Revelation uses two Greek words for time — chronos (G5550, measured duration) and kairos (G2540, appointed season) — with apparent precision. The G5550 and G2540 word studies confirm that all four Revelation chronos occurrences carry "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" meaning, while all Revelation kairos occurrences mark appointed prophetic seasons.

The significance becomes clear at Rev 10:6: "that there should be time [chronos] no longer." The word is chronos — measured duration. The cessation declared is the cessation of the remaining delay interval before the mystery of God is finished. This is not the end of time as a philosophical category (kairos language would suit that differently) but the end of the bounded not-yet interval — the chronos remaining between the angel's oath and the seventh trumpet's completion.

The contrast between the dragon's "short time" (oligon kairon at Rev 12:12, using kairos) and the martyrs' "little time" (chronon mikron at Rev 6:11, using chronos) is subtle but consistent: the martyrs wait a measured duration while the dragon has a bounded appointed season. Both are not-yet intervals with divine limits, expressed in different temporal vocabulary.

The divine formula temporal arc mirrors this distinction: the anticipated "coming one" (erchomenos) is God's future as appointed from eternity (kairos-type description of divine purpose) which becomes realized at the structural seams where erchomenos drops (11:17, 16:5).

### The Synthesis: Inaugurated Eschatology as Structural Architecture

The accumulated grammatical, lexical, and structural evidence converges on a single framework: Revelation is the NT's most concentrated expression of inaugurated eschatology — the theological understanding that the last days have begun but not concluded, that the decisive act has occurred but its full realization awaits.

The framework is not borrowed from later systematic theology and imposed on the text. It is generated by the text's own grammatical choices. The proleptic aorists, the ginomai arc from aorist through two perfects, the divine formula reduction from three elements to two, the nikao tense taxonomy, the ballo arc, the hypomonē structural markers, the heaven-earth dual perspective passages, and the Daniel/Revelation sealed/unsealed contrast all point to the same theological reality: expressed in as many different grammatical and structural ways as John had at his disposal.

The D-Day/V-Day imagery from Cullmann's scholarship (noted in the web research) aptly captures the structure: the decisive battle has been fought and won (D-Day = the cross), but the full surrender and peace have not yet been implemented across all territory (V-Day = the Parousia). The soldiers fighting in isolated pockets after D-Day are not wrong that the enemy has power — they experience it directly. But they are wrong if they conclude from that experience that the war's outcome is uncertain. It was settled at D-Day. The soldiers endure not in ignorance of the outcome but in confidence of it.

This is precisely the posture Revelation's already/not-yet calls its readers to. The beast has edothē authority — permitted, bounded, real. The saints experience real tribulation and martyrdom. The dragon rages with genuine wrath. None of this is false. But none of it is cosmically uncertain. The ginomai arc has declared the completion in heaven's perspective. The nikao arc has established Christ's victory as the ground of everything. The ballo arc has begun the dragon's progressive confinement. The divine name has already started dropping its "coming one" element as the arrival is realized.

The readers of Revelation, like the churches in the letters, live in this interval — between the already of Christ's completed cross-victory and the not-yet of the full earthly realization. The book's structural message is precisely suited to that interval: do not be deceived by the beast's permitted authority; do not be discouraged by the dragon's earthly rage; do not mistake the ongoing not-yet for cosmic uncertainty about the outcome. The outcome is settled. The grammar says so. The divine name says so. The ballo arc says so. The ginomai arc says so.

The call to hypomonē is the book's practical response to this structure. Endurance is the posture of those who know the already but live in the not-yet — who have seen the heavenly declaration of accomplished reality (aorists from heaven) but experience the ongoing earthly tension (present-tense tribulation). Endurance is not passive resignation. It is active confidence in a settled outcome that has not yet fully arrived on the ground.

**G1096 ginomai** — 702 NT occurrences, 87 in Revelation (12.4%). The most grammatically complex verb in the temporal framework, tracing the entire eschatological arc from proleptic aorist declaration to perfect completions. Egeneto (V-2ADI-3S) at 11:15 and 12:10 = proleptic certainty. Gegonen (V-2RAI-3S) at 16:17 = sequence completed. Gegonan (V-2RAI-3P) at 21:6 = all things completed.

**G3528 nikaō** — 29 NT occurrences, 17 in Revelation (59% density). Four-category tense taxonomy: Christ's aorist indicative (self-originated completed victory), believer's present participle (ongoing overcoming), beast's aorist infinitive under edothē (permitted dependent conquest), Lamb's future indicative (eschatological certain victory).

**G906 ballō** — 127 NT occurrences, 23 in Revelation (18% density). Three-stage arc: heaven→earth (eblēthē, passive, 12:9), earth→abyss (ébalen, active, 20:3), abyss→lake of fire (eblēthē, passive, 20:10). Verbal unity across chapters 12 and 20.

**G5281 hypomonē** — 32 NT occurrences, 7 in Revelation (1.7 per 100 verses — highest NT density). Structural not-yet marker. Pairs at 13:10 and 14:12 placed at maximum eschatological tension points. Hebrew source H4723 miqveh (expectation vocabulary) — endurance grounded in hope.

**G737 arti** — 36 NT occurrences, 1 in Revelation (12:10). Single occurrence placed at the most theologically loaded moment: the heaven-perspective declaration of accomplished salvation. Punctiliar "this very instant" present-moment marker. No other NT word is more precisely momentary.

**G5034 tachos / G5035 tachy** — Combined: 20 NT occurrences. Revelation uses: en tachei (1:1, 22:6) for the prologue-epilogue frame; tachy ×6 across letters (2:5, 2:16, 3:11) and epilogue (22:7, 22:12, 22:20). Semantic range: temporal imminence (BDAG "soon") + manner of action (Walvoord "swiftly"). Both senses documented in NT precedents.

**G1451 engus** — 30 NT occurrences, 2 in Revelation (1:3, 22:10). Book-level inclusio. Hebrew source H7138 with PMI 7.99. Kairos + engus = "the appointed prophetic season is near." Complementary to en tachei (which governs specific events) — engus governs the overall temporal proximity of the kairos itself.

**G2540 kairos vs G5550 chronos** — kairos (84 NT occurrences): appointed prophetic seasons in all Revelation uses. Chronos (49 NT occurrences): measured elapsed duration in all four Revelation uses. The distinction governs Rev 10:6 (cessation of the delay-interval chronos) and 12:12 (dragon's bounded oligon kairon).

**G4972 sphragizō** — 28 NT occurrences. The LXX translation vehicle for H2856 chatham — the same Greek verb connects Daniel's sealing commands (via LXX) to Revelation's unsealing prohibition. The verbal link makes the canonical inversion explicit.

**H2856 chatham** — 26 OT occurrences. Qal imperative (Dan 12:4) → Qal participle (Dan 12:9) — commands fulfilled, now described as state. LXX sphragizō creates the direct Greek bridge to Rev 22:10.

## Difficult Passages

### The En Tachei Debate

Three scholarly positions compete for the meaning of en tachei at Rev 1:1: (1) chronological imminence — events will happen soon (BDAG, Farrar, Aland); (2) prophetic imminence — the events are on the prophetic horizon for every age (Mounce, Thomas); (3) manner of action — when they begin, they will execute swiftly (Walvoord, Rogers).

The lexical data most naturally supports (1): BDAG's primary gloss is "soon, in a short time." Romans 16:20's Pauline use is clearly temporal. Acts 12:7 and 22:18 show tachy can mean manner-of-action in non-eschatological contexts, but these do not resolve what the dative phrase en tachei means when introducing a prophetic book.

The structural observation (SP089) — that hypomonē calls throughout the book presuppose temporal extension — argues against strict immediate completion of all events. The book cannot simultaneously demand patient endurance across a long interval and claim all events happen immediately.

The most consistent reading is that en tachei marks the inauguration of the fulfillment phase (the end-time sequence has begun with Christ's first advent) rather than the immediate completion of every event narrated. The urgency is real: the appointed season is near, the book's contents are directly relevant to readers in every generation of the fulfillment phase. But "shortly" does not mean "all completed within John's lifetime" given the book's own call to endure.

### The Casting of the Dragon at Rev 12:9

The timing of the eblēthē at Rev 12:9 is debated. John 12:31 (future passive, ekblēthēsetai — "shall be cast out") places the cross as the event of the dragon's casting. Luke 10:18 shows Jesus "beholding" Satan's fall in pre-cross ministry. Hebrews 2:14 and 1 John 3:8 describe the cross as the destruction of the devil's power. Revelation 12:9's aorist passive describes the casting as completed.

If the cross is the referent of Rev 12:9's eblēthē, the not-yet of 12:13-17 (dragon's earthly persecution) runs concurrently with the already of 12:9-10 (cosmic expulsion/victory) — consistent with the inaugurated eschatology framework.

What the grammar does not do is specify the exact historical moment. It shows the casting as completed from heaven's perspective, in contrast with ongoing earthly activity. The cross-referent reading (Beale, Bohr, EGW) is consistent with the grammar without being demanded by it alone.

The John 12:31 / Rev 12:9 vocabulary distinction matters: G1544 ekballō (John) vs. G906 ballō (Rev 12:9) are related (ekballō is a compound of ballō) but distinct roots. The parallels file confirms this: the highest NT parallel for Rev 12:9 is Luke 10:18 (0.349), not John 12:31. This does not undermine the theological connection — it cautions against treating them as identical vocabulary citations.

### Chronos Ouketi Estai at Rev 10:6

"Time shall be no longer" raises two competing interpretations: (1) the end of sequential historical time itself (eschatological termination of the age); (2) the end of the delay interval before the mystery of God is finished (ESV: "there will be no more delay").

The G5550 word study places all four Revelation chronos occurrences in the "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" semantic category — the cessation declared is most naturally read as the cessation of the remaining delay, not the abolition of time as a metaphysical category. The Daniel 12:7 structural parallel (confirmed in the parallels file) strengthens reading (2): Daniel's oath is about when the 3.5-year period ends; Rev 10:6 echoes the oath structure for declaring when the delay before the mystery's completion ends.

Reading (1) is not lexically impossible — chronos can refer to time in general. But the context (the mystery will be finished "as he hath declared") connects to the seventh trumpet's programmatic announcement (11:15-18) rather than to an eschatological termination of time as such.

## Conclusion

Revelation's already/not-yet temporal framework is not a theme imposed on the book by later theologians. It is what the book is doing grammatically, structurally, and canonically from its first verse to its last.

The book opens with en tachei (Rev 1:1) and ho kairos engus (Rev 1:3) — urgency-consciousness from the first sentence. It closes with ho kairos engus (Rev 22:10) and the triple tachy declarations of the epilogue — the same urgency-consciousness to the last verse. The frame is not decoration. It tells the reader what posture the book requires.

Within the frame, the grammar encodes the theological reality with precision. Proleptic aorists declare the certain future as accomplished from heaven's perspective. The divine formula reduces from three elements to two as the anticipated arrival is realized. The ginomai arc moves from aorist declaration to perfect singular to perfect plural, tracking the escalating completion of what was first declared as certain. The nikao taxonomy maps Christ's completed victory, the believer's ongoing response, the beast's permitted but bounded authority, and the eschatological future — all in the same verb. The ballo arc traces the dragon's three-stage displacement with verbal unity across eight chapters.

The heaven-earth dual perspective passages hold both realities simultaneously rather than resolving them: heaven celebrates accomplished realities in aorists while earth endures ongoing tension in presents and imperfects. The hypomonē calls appear precisely at the points of maximum eschatological tension — not because the outcome is uncertain but because the ground hasn't yet caught up with the sky.

The canonical argument seals it: Daniel is told to seal because the time is far off. John is told not to seal because the time is near. The same vocabulary, the same verbal root in the LXX, but opposite commands — because the fulfillment phase that Daniel awaited has been inaugurated. Revelation is written from inside the already/not-yet interval that Daniel could only look toward from outside it.

For the churches of Asia — and for every subsequent generation of readers — the book's message is this: you are living in a cosmic interval bounded on both ends by divine action. The decisive victory has been won. The cross accomplished what the proleptic aorists declare. The dragon's days are numbered, his domain contracted, his final disposal guaranteed. The not-yet is real — tribulation, beast-authority, martyrdom, waiting under the altar — but it is bounded. The chronos of the delay will cease. The gegonan will come. The kairos is near.

The call is hypomonē — patient, hope-grounded endurance from those who know the already and live in the not-yet.
