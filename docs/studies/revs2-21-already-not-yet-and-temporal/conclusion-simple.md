# Already/Not-Yet -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

This study examined how Revelation constructs its sense of time — why the book speaks of events as already accomplished before they have fully happened on earth, and why it calls for patient endurance at the same time. The answer turns out to be built into the book's structure and word choices from its first sentence to its last.

---

## Revelation Declares Future Events as Already Done

One of the most consistent patterns in Revelation is that heavenly voices announce coming events in the past tense — as if they have already happened. This is not imprecision or error. It is a deliberate device with deep roots in Old Testament prophecy. Isaiah announced "Babylon is fallen" before Babylon fell. Revelation does the same thing at multiple points.

The most striking examples are paired with the phrase "fallen, fallen is Babylon" — appearing at Revelation 14:8 and again at 18:2, before the Babylon narrative has even fully unfolded. Similarly, the seventh trumpet at Revelation 11:15 declares:

> "The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for ever and ever."

This announcement is made eight chapters before the final battle. Revelation 12:10 announces in the same completed form that "now is come salvation, and the kingdom of our God" — while the dragon is still actively persecuting believers in the very next verses. Heaven declares the victory settled; earth is still experiencing the conflict. Both are true at the same time.

---

## The Dragon Is Defeated — But Still Active

Revelation 12 contains one of the clearest pictures of the already/not-yet dynamic. Verse 9 declares that the dragon "was cast out" — that is the already. But verses 12 through 17 immediately describe his furious activity on earth — that is the not-yet:

> "Woe to the inhabiters of the earth and of the sea! for the devil is come down unto you, having great wrath, because he knoweth that he hath but a short time."

The same casting event is celebrated in heaven as a completed victory and dreaded on earth as an intensifying threat. The reason for the dragon's increased rage is precisely because he has been expelled from heaven — his domain is now limited, and he knows it. His power on earth is real but bounded. His final disposal is certain: the same verb used for his casting from heaven (chapters 12) is used again when he is thrown into the abyss and then into the lake of fire (chapter 20), tracking his three-stage confinement through the book.

---

## The Word "Overcome" Carries the Whole Framework

The Greek word for "overcome" or "conquer" appears 17 times in Revelation — more than half of all its New Testament occurrences. The study found that the word's grammatical form shifts depending on who is doing the overcoming:

- **Christ's victory** is expressed as a completed past action — done, self-originated, settled.
- **The believer's overcoming** is expressed as an ongoing present action — continuous, active.
- **The beast's conquering of the saints** is expressed as a permitted action — dependent on divine allowance, not inherently his own.
- **The Lamb's final victory** is expressed as a future certainty.

Revelation 3:21 contains the contrast explicitly:

> "To him that overcometh will I grant to sit with me in my throne, even as I also overcame, and am set down with my Father in his throne."

Christ's overcoming (past, completed) grounds the believer's ongoing overcoming (present, continuing). The beast's permitted authority is real but structurally subordinate — it depends on divine permission, while Christ's victory is inherent and self-originated.

---

## God's Own Name Encodes the Already/Not-Yet

In the opening chapters of Revelation, God is described with a three-part title: the one who is, who was, and who is to come. All three elements appear at Revelation 1:4, 1:8, and 4:8.

But later in the book, at Revelation 11:17 and 16:5, the formula appears with only two elements — "who is" and "who was." The "who is to come" element is absent. This is not a mistake. The elders' hymn at 11:17 drops that element precisely because the kingdom arrival is what they are celebrating. The anticipated "coming" is no longer merely anticipated — it has been declared realized.

> "And the seventh angel sounded; and there were great voices in heaven, saying, The kingdoms of this world are become the kingdoms of our Lord, and of his Christ; and he shall reign for ever and ever."

The divine name itself tracks the movement from anticipation to realization as the book progresses. What was a "not yet" in chapter 1 becomes an "already declared" by chapter 11.

---

## Daniel Sealed It; Revelation Opens It

One of the study's most significant findings is a cross-book comparison between Daniel and Revelation. In Daniel, the prophet is repeatedly told to seal up his visions because the time is far off:

> "But thou, O Daniel, shut up the words, and seal the book, even to the time of the end." (Dan 12:4)

In Revelation, John is told the exact opposite:

> "Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book: for the time is at hand." (Rev 22:10)

The two commands use the same word in the original languages — Daniel seals, John must not seal. The reason is the changed situation: Daniel stood outside the fulfillment phase, looking forward to it. John stands inside it, writing from within the interval that Daniel could only await. The decisive events have already been set in motion. The not-yet of Daniel's day has become the already-inaugurated of John's.

---

## Endurance Is the Right Posture for the Interval

Given all of this — the declared victory, the ongoing conflict, the bounded time of the dragon's activity — Revelation repeatedly uses one word to describe the appropriate response: patient endurance. The word appears seven times in Revelation, more densely than anywhere else in the New Testament.

It appears at two especially significant points: immediately after the beast is given permission to war against the saints (Revelation 13:10), and immediately after the three angels announce the judgment hour has arrived (Revelation 14:12):

> "Here is the patience of the saints: here are they that keep the commandments of God, and the faith of Jesus." (Rev 14:12)

The placement is deliberate. When heaven declares the judgment arrived and the outcome settled, the earthly saints are not told to relax — they are called to endure. The heavenly declarations do not eliminate the not-yet; they provide the ground for enduring it. Saints endure not because the outcome is uncertain, but because they know it is settled while still living in the interval before it is fully expressed on earth.
