## Question
How do the seven heads/mountains/kings of the beast (Rev 17:9-11) function as a structural and symbolic element? What does the 'five are fallen, and one is, and the other is not yet come' formula (17:10) reveal about the beast's temporal structure? How do the seven beatitudes (makarios — Rev 1:3, 14:13, 16:15, 19:9, 20:6, 22:7, 22:14) create a structural spine distributed through the entire book? Do the beatitudes form a chiastic or progressive pattern? Document each beatitude's position in the book's narrative and its thematic content.

### Scarlet beast with seven heads — third entity bearing the seven-headed symbol

**Context:** The fourth en pneumati vision (cf. 1:10, 4:2, 21:10). The scarlet beast is the third entity with seven heads and ten horns. Names of blasphemy now cover the entire beast ('full of'), not just the heads (13:1). A woman sits upon this beast.

### Angel's interpretation begins — beast's temporal parody formula

> And the angel said unto me, Wherefore didst thou marvel? I will tell thee the mystery of the woman, and of the beast that carrieth her, which hath the seven heads and ten horns. The beast that thou sawest was, and is not; and shall ascend out of the bottomless pit, and go into perdition: and they that dwell on the earth shall wonder, whose names were not written in the book of life from the foundation of the world, when they behold the beast that was, and is not, and yet is.

**Context:** The angel offers to explain the mystery (mystērion) of both woman and beast. The beast's temporal formula appears: ēn (was, imperfect) + ouk estin (is not, present) + parestai (will be present, future). This inverts God's formula: ho ōn (present) + ho ēn (past) + ho erchomenos (future). The beast ascends from the abyss — counterfeit resurrection.

**Notes:** The beast's formula ēn kai ouk estin kai parestai inverts the divine ōn kai ēn kai erchomenos. Key differences: (1) divine formula starts with present (IS), beast starts with past (WAS); (2) divine uses erchomenos (coming continuously), beast uses parestai (will be present — a fixed arrival); (3) divine has no negation phase, beast has 'is not' — a death/absence period.

### Core interpretive text — seven heads = mountains = kings, temporal formula, eighth king

> And here [is] the mind which hath wisdom. The seven heads are seven mountains, on which the woman sitteth. And there are seven kings: five are fallen, and one is, [and] the other is not yet come; and when he cometh, he must continue a short space. And the beast that was, and is not, even he is the eighth, and is of the seven, and goeth into perdition.

**Context:** The angel's dual identification: heads = mountains AND kings. The temporal formula divides seven kings into past (five fallen, epesan aorist), present (one is, estin), and future (one not yet come, oupo ēlthen). The seventh continues oligon (a short space). The beast itself becomes the eighth (ogdoos, anarthrous) and is ek tōn hepta (of/from the seven, masculine genitive agreeing with basileis).

### Ten horns as contemporaneous kings — contrasting the sequential seven heads

> And the ten horns which thou sawest are ten kings, which have received no kingdom as yet; but receive power as kings one hour with the beast. These have one mind, and shall give their power and strength unto the beast. These shall make war with the Lamb, and the Lamb shall overcome them: for he is Lord of lords, and King of kings: and they that are with him [are] called, and chosen, and faithful.

**Context:** Ten horns = ten kings who receive power 'one hour' (mian hōran) WITH the beast — simultaneously, not sequentially. This contrasts the seven heads which are sequential (five fell, one is, one not yet come). The Lamb overcomes them — using nikao (G3528).

**Notes:** The consecutive vs. contemporaneous distinction between heads and horns is structurally important. Seven heads = sequential historical powers; ten horns = simultaneous end-time coalition. The Lamb is 'King of kings' (basileus basileōn) — using the same basileus word as the seven kings.

### Beatitude 1 — Blessed is he that readeth, heareth, and keepeth (prophecy inclusio opening)

> The Revelation of Jesus Christ, which God gave unto him, to shew unto his servants things which must shortly come to pass; and he sent and signified [it] by his angel unto his servant John: Who bare record of the word of God, and of the testimony of Jesus Christ, and of all things that he saw. Blessed [is] he that readeth, and they that hear the words of this prophecy, and keep those things which are written therein: for the time [is] at hand.

**Context:** The book's prologue. Three distinct actions governed by makarios: (1) ho anaginōskōn (the one reading, singular articular participle), (2) hoi akouontes (the ones hearing, plural articular participle), (3) tērountes (the ones keeping, plural present active participle). The objects are 'the words (logous) of this prophecy (prophēteias)' and 'the things written (gegrammena) therein.' The time is 'near' (engys). Position: before Vision 1, at the structural opening.

**Notes:** The three actions confirm EGW's observation (7LtMs 1891): reading (anaginōskō G314), hearing (akouō G191), and keeping (tēreō G5083) are grammatically distinct — three articular participles sharing the predicate adjective makarios. The singular reader (ho anaginōskōn) and plural hearers (hoi akouontes) suggest a public reading/hearing context. Shared vocabulary with beatitude 6 (Rev 22:7): makarios, tēreō, logous, prophēteias — four identical lexemes creating the inclusio.

### Beatitude 5 — Blessed and holy, first resurrection, kings and priests convergence node

> And I saw thrones, and they sat upon them, and judgment was given unto them: and [I saw] the souls of them that were beheaded for the witness of Jesus, and for the word of God, and which had not worshipped the beast, neither his image, neither had received [his] mark upon their foreheads, or in their hands; and they lived and reigned with Christ a thousand years. But the rest of the dead lived not again until the thousand years were finished. This [is] the first resurrection. Blessed and holy [is] he that hath part in the first resurrection: on such the second death hath no power, but they shall be priests of God and of Christ, and shall reign with him a thousand years.

**Context:** Within the millennium narrative. The only beatitude with double predicate adjective: makarios KAI hagios. Links to the first resurrection (anastasis, G386), the second death (deuteros thanatos, G2288), and the kings-and-priests formula (hiereis tou Theou kai tou Christou, kai basileusousin met' autou). This is the convergence node where the beatitude series, the kings-and-priests thread, and the overcomer promises all intersect.

**Notes:** Unique features: (1) double predicate adjective (makarios kai hagios — no other beatitude adds hagios); (2) negative promise — second death has no exousia (authority/power) over them; (3) kings-and-priests fulfillment — hiereis and basileusousin echo 1:6 and 5:10; (4) convergence with overcomer promise of 2:11 ('shall not be hurt of the second death'). This is the lone unpaired beatitude in both the standard chiasm and the six-plus-one model.

### Graded numerical saying — three...four (X/X+1 pattern)

> The horseleach hath two daughters, [crying], Give, give. There are three [things that] are never satisfied, [yea], four [things] say not, [It is] enough: The grave; and the barren womb; the earth [that] is not filled with water; and the fire [that] saith not, [It is] enough.

**Context:** Agur's numerical proverbs. The X/X+1 pattern: 'three...four' — lists three then adds a fourth to signal completeness-plus-one. This literary device may illuminate Rev 17:10-11's 'seven...eighth' pattern.

**Notes:** The graded numerical saying uses consecutive numbers as a literary device indicating completeness + emphasis, not a strict count. If Rev 17's 'seven then eighth' follows this pattern, the eighth is not chronologically distinct but represents the apex/culmination of the seven.

**Notes:** The seventh trumpet announces the transfer of kingdom authority from the beast system's kings (Rev 17:10) to God and Christ. The heptadic trumpet climax parallels the heptadic head/king structure.

### Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen — doubled epesan formula (standalone)

> And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird.

**Context:** The detailed Babylon-fall announcement. Same doubled epesan as Rev 14:8 and Isa 21:9.

**Notes:** Third occurrence of the doubled epesan formula (Isa 21:9 → Rev 14:8 → Rev 18:2). Connects to Rev 17:10's epesan (five are fallen) through shared pipto vocabulary.

### Overcomer promise — second death has no power

> He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches; He that overcometh shall not be hurt of the second death.

**Context:** Letter to Smyrna. The overcomer promise: 'shall not be hurt of the second death.' This is fulfilled in beatitude 5 (Rev 20:6, 'on such the second death hath no power').

**Notes:** Beatitude 5 (20:6) fulfills this overcomer promise. The nikao (overcome) → makarios (blessed) connection makes beatitude 5 the convergence node of the overcomer thread and the beatitude series.

### Kings and priests — first occurrence of the formula

> And hath made us kings and priests unto God and his Father; to him [be] glory and dominion for ever and ever. Amen.

**Context:** Doxology in the prologue. First occurrence of the kings-and-priests formula in Revelation. Aorist tense (epoiēsen) — an accomplished act.

**Notes:** The kings-and-priests tense arc: epoiēsen (1:6, aorist accomplished) → basileusousin (5:10, future) → ebasileusan (20:4, ingressive aorist) → basileusousin (22:5, eternal future). Beatitude 5 (20:6) is the third link in this chain.

### Kings and priests — second occurrence, future reign

> And hast made us unto our God kings and priests: and we shall reign on the earth.

**Context:** The new song of the redeemed. Second occurrence of kings-and-priests. Future tense (basileusousin) — the reign is anticipated.

**Notes:** Second link in the kings-and-priests tense arc. Future reign anticipated; beatitude 5 (20:6) narrates its realization.

### Shall reign for ever and ever — final kings/priests formula

> And there shall be no night there; and they need no candle, neither light of the sun; for the Lord God giveth them light: and they shall reign for ever and ever.

**Context:** The New Jerusalem vision epilogue. Final occurrence of the reign promise. Eternal future (basileusousin eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn).

**Notes:** Fourth and final link in the kings-and-priests tense arc. The eternal future reign completes the trajectory that runs through beatitude 5 (20:6).

### Three...four transgressions of Gaza — graded numerical pattern repeated

> Thus saith the LORD; For three transgressions of Gaza, and for four, I will not turn away [the punishment] thereof; because they carried away captive the whole captivity, to deliver [them] up to Edom:

**Context:** Second oracle in Amos's judgment cycle. Same three/four formula as Amos 1:3. Shows the structural repetition of the X/X+1 pattern.

**Notes:** The repeated formula across multiple oracles (Damascus 1:3, Gaza 1:6, Tyre 1:9, etc.) shows the graded numerical saying is a structural device, not incidental. Supports Bauckham's reading of Rev 17:10-11.

### White garments — walk with me in white, clothed in white raiment

> Thou hast a few names even in Sardis which have not defiled their garments; and they shall walk with me in white: for they are worthy. He that overcometh, the same shall be clothed in white raiment; and I will not blot out his name out of the book of life, but I will confess his name before my Father, and before his angels.

**Context:** Letter to Sardis. Garment theme: undefiled garments → walking in white → clothed in white raiment. Connects to beatitude 3 (16:15, keeping garments) and beatitude 7 (22:14, washing robes).

**Notes:** The garment thread links three beatitudes: keeping garments (16:15), washing robes (22:14), and walking in white (3:4-5 provides the overcomer background). The white-garment motif also connects to 19:8 (bride's fine linen).

### Washed their robes in the blood of the Lamb — robe-washing concept

> And I said unto him, Sir, thou knowest. And he said to me, These are they which came out of great tribulation, and have washed their robes, and made them white in the blood of the Lamb.

**Context:** The great multitude before the throne. Their robes are washed (eplȳnan, aorist of plynō G4150) and made white in the Lamb's blood. This connects to beatitude 7 (22:14) where the critical text reads 'blessed are they that wash (plynontes) their robes.'

**Notes:** The same verb plynō (G4150) appears in Rev 7:14 (eplȳnan) and Rev 22:14 (plynontes). The robe-washing concept links the great tribulation multitude to the seventh beatitude.

### Fine linen, clean and white — garment/purity theme for beatitude 4

> And to her was granted that she should be arrayed in fine linen, clean and white: for the fine linen is the righteousness of saints.

**Context:** The bride's preparation for the marriage supper. The fine linen (byssinon) = righteousness of saints. Connects to beatitude 4 (19:9, called to the marriage supper) and the garment theme running through beatitudes 3 and 7.

**Notes:** The bride's garments (19:8) and the beatitude calling guests to the marriage supper (19:9) are adjacent — the garment preparation immediately precedes the blessing on the invited guests.

### Harlot introduction — great whore on many waters

> And there came one of the seven angels which had the seven vials, and talked with me, saying unto me, Come hither; I will shew unto thee the judgment of the great whore that sitteth upon many waters: With whom the kings of the earth have committed fornication, and the inhabitants of the earth have been made drunk with the wine of her fornication.

**Context:** The angel invites John to see the judgment of the great harlot. She sits on many waters (= peoples, 17:15). The kings of the earth commit fornication with her. This introduces the entire Rev 17 seven-heads passage.

**Notes:** The harlot introduction sets up the vision that contains the seven-heads interpretation (17:9-11). One of the seven bowl-angels serves as guide — connecting the bowl sequence to the harlot judgment.

### Modified divine formula — 'is to come' dropped

> Saying, We give thee thanks, O Lord God Almighty, which art, and wast, and art to come; because thou hast taken to thee thy great power, and hast reigned.

**Context:** The seventh trumpet worship. The divine formula appears but modified: ho ōn kai ho ēn. Some manuscripts omit ho erchomenos. The dropping of the 'coming' element may signal that at this point in the narrative, God has arrived — the future component is fulfilled.

**Notes:** If the 'is to come' element is absent (as in the best manuscripts), the seventh trumpet marks the point where God's future arrival becomes present reality. This is structurally significant for understanding the beast's temporal formula as an attempted counterfeit of this divine arrival.
