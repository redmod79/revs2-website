# Word Studies

## Question
What is the structural significance of the half-hour silence at the opening of the seventh seal (Rev 8:1)? How does this silence function as the transition between the seal sequence and the trumpet sequence? How does the incense prelude (Rev 8:2-5) — the angel with the golden censer, incense offered with the prayers of the saints, fire cast to earth — establish the sanctuary context for the trumpet judgments? Use Leviticus 16 and the Day of Atonement ritual to identify what sanctuary action Rev 8:3-5 depicts. Is the seventh seal the container for the trumpets (telescoping), or does it merely precede them (sequential)?

### G4602 — sige

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4602-sige.md`

**Occurrences:** 2

sige (silence) occurs only twice in the NT — Rev 8:1 and Acts 21:40. In both cases, silence precedes a significant communication. The word conveys imposed, active silence (hushing) rather than mere quiet.

**Semantic categories:** imposed silence before communication, reverent/active silence

**Key verses:** Rev 8:1, Acts 21:40

**Relevance:** Core word of the seventh seal. The rarity (only 2 NT occurrences) makes it a distinctive marker. Both uses share the pattern of silence preceding significant action, supporting the reading that the silence IS content (Beale) rather than merely a pause.

### G2256 — hemiorion

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2256-hemiorion.md`

**Occurrences:** 1

hemiorion (half an hour) is an NT hapax legomenon — the shortest time designation in Revelation. Compound of hemisu (half) + hora (hour). The precision of 'half an hour' rather than a round number underscores the deliberateness of the interval.

**Semantic categories:** temporal duration in apocalyptic vision

**Key verses:** Rev 8:1

**Relevance:** NT hapax — TM056. The deliberate precision of 'half an hour' resists both prophetic-time calculation (no evidence hemiorion was ever used prophetically) and casual dismissal. The brevity marks the silence as measured and bounded.

### G3031 — libanotos

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G3031-libanotos.md`

**Occurrences:** 2

libanotos (censer) occurs only at Rev 8:3 and 8:5 in the entire NT — TM067. The same vessel serves intercession (v.3, carrying incense with prayers) and judgment (v.5, filled with fire and cast to earth). Dual-function vessel marking the structural pivot.

**Semantic categories:** sanctuary vessel, dual-function instrument

**Key verses:** Rev 8:3, Rev 8:5

**Relevance:** The censer's dual function (intercession → judgment) is the structural pivot of the incense scene. Its exclusive occurrence at 8:3 and 8:5 binds the two actions together as aspects of a single scene. Part of the triple-attested vessel transformation arc (SP119).

### G2368 — thymiama

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2368-thymiama.md`

**Occurrences:** 6

thymiama (incense) occurs 6 times in the NT, with 4 occurrences in Revelation (5:8, 8:3, 8:4, 18:13). In 5:8 directly identified as prayers; in 8:3-4 offered WITH prayers. The phonetic near-identity with thymos (G2372, wrath) — thymiama/thymos — creates the vessel transformation arc from prayers to wrath.

**Semantic categories:** sanctuary incense, prayer symbolism, vessel content

**Key verses:** Rev 5:8, Rev 8:3, Rev 8:4, Rev 18:13

**Relevance:** Links the prayer-incense typology (Psa 141:2 → Rev 5:8, 8:3-4) to the vessel transformation arc. The thymiama/thymos phonetic connection (5:8 thymiamaton → 15:7 thymou) is one of the most distinctive structural features in Revelation.

### G4335 — proseuche

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4335-proseuchḗ.md`

**Occurrences:** 37

proseuche (prayer) occurs 37 times in the NT, with 3 Revelation occurrences (5:8, 8:3, 8:4). In Revelation, prayers of saints are depicted as incense offered before God's throne — a heavenly liturgical image. The dative tais proseuchais in 8:3-4 expresses accompaniment ('with the prayers').

**Semantic categories:** worship-address to God, heavenly/liturgical prayer, intercessory prayer

**Key verses:** Rev 5:8, Rev 8:3, Rev 8:4

**Relevance:** The prayers of saints in Rev 8:3-4 connect to the martyrs' cry at 6:9-10. The trumpet judgments that follow (8:6ff) are responses to these prayers — making the trumpet sequence the divine answer to the altar cry.

### G2379 — thysiastērion

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G2379-thysiastērion.md`

**Occurrences:** 23

thysiastērion (altar) tracks across all three judgment sequences in a vindication arc with increasing agency: passive blood-receiving (6:9) → mediating prayers (8:3-5) → issuing commands (9:13) → having authority (14:18) → personified speech (16:7) → vindication complete (19:2).

**Semantic categories:** sanctuary altar, vindication agent, judgment source

**Key verses:** Rev 6:9, Rev 8:3, Rev 9:13, Rev 14:18, Rev 16:7, Rev 19:2

**Relevance:** The altar vindication arc (SP037) is one of the strongest structural arguments for reading seals-trumpets-bowls as a unified narrative. The two altars question in 8:3 (unqualified vs. chrysoun) is diagnostic for the Stefanovic thesis.

### G4536 — salpinx

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G4536-salpinx.md`

**Occurrences:** 11

salpinx (trumpet) occurs 11 times in the NT, with 6 in Revelation (55% concentration). The word conflates both OT instruments: shophar (ram's horn, covenantal) and chatsotsrah (silver trumpet, priestly). In Revelation, the seven trumpets signal successive eschatological judgments.

**Semantic categories:** apocalyptic judgment signal, eschatological gathering, divine communication

**Key verses:** Rev 8:2, Rev 8:6, Rev 8:13, Rev 9:14

**Relevance:** Links the seventh seal directly to the trumpet sequence via 8:2 (trumpets given) and 8:6 (angels prepare to sound). The trumpet-as-judgment-signal function connects to the Feast of Trumpets (warning before DOA).

### G796 — astrape

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G796-astrapē.md`

astrape (lightning) occurs 9 times in the NT, with 4 in Revelation — EXCLUSIVELY in the four theophanic passages (4:5, 8:5, 11:19, 16:18). Consistently paired with bronte (thunder) and phone (voice) in the theophanic formula. Hebrew source: baraq (H1300).

**Key verses:** Rev 4:5, Rev 8:5, Rev 11:19, Rev 16:18

**Relevance:** The EXCLUSIVE distribution of astrape in Revelation's four theophanic passages is one of the strongest textual markers (TM) linking the three judgment sequences. The escalation pattern (3 → 4 → 5 → 5+ elements) never decreases.

### H2013 — hasah

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/H2013-hâçâh.md`

**Occurrences:** 10

hasah (hush/be silent) occurs 10 times in the OT. Piel imperative 'hush!' — commands silence. Three key prophetic uses: Hab 2:20, Zep 1:7, Zec 2:13 — all combining temple/sanctuary setting, divine presence, and imminent judgment. LXX renders with eulabeomai (reverent fear), revealing translators understood hasah as carrying fearful reverence, not merely acoustic quiet.

**Semantic categories:** reverent silence before God, command to silence a crowd, fearful silence in judgment

**Key verses:** Hab 2:20, Zep 1:7, Zec 2:13, Amo 6:10, Amo 8:3

**Relevance:** The OT background for Rev 8:1's silence. All three prophetic uses share: same root in Piel imperative, temple/sanctuary setting, divine presence as cause, imminent judgment context. This triad establishes silence as a sanctuary-judgment response.

### G1909 — epi

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G1909-epí.md`

**Occurrences:** 877

epi (preposition) occurs 877 times in the NT, with 139 in Revelation (highest per-100-verses rate). With genitive: contact/position (upon, at); with dative: basis/ground; with accusative: direction/motion toward. Rev 8:3 contains both epi + genitive (position at altar) and epi + accusative (direction of offering onto golden altar).

**Semantic categories:** spatial contact, direction/motion, authority/power

**Key verses:** Rev 8:3

**Relevance:** Critical for Stefanovic's two-altar thesis. epi + genitive with unqualified thysiastērion (8:3a) vs. epi + accusative with chrysoun thysiastērion (8:3b). Grammar reference confirms the genitive is compatible with either 'upon' or 'at/beside' — cannot resolve the altar identification by grammar alone.

### G5552 — chryseos

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G5552-chrýseos.md`

**Occurrences:** 18

chryseos (golden) occurs 18 times in the NT, with 15 in Revelation. Used for sanctuary furnishings (lampstands, altar, censer, bowls), royal imagery (crowns, girdles), and corruption (Babylon's cup). Distinguishes the golden altar (to thysiastērion to chrysoun) from the unqualified altar.

**Semantic categories:** sanctuary furnishings, royal/divine imagery

**Key verses:** Rev 8:3, Rev 9:13, Rev 1:12, Rev 5:8

**Relevance:** The qualifier chrysoun (golden) at Rev 8:3b and 9:13 explicitly identifies the golden/incense altar, distinguishing it from the unqualified altar at 6:9 and 8:3a.

### G906 — ballo

**Source:** `D:/bible/bible-studies/word-studies/G906-ballo.md`

**Occurrences:** 127

ballo (cast/throw) occurs 122 times in the NT (106 unique verses), with 23 in Revelation — the highest concentration per 100 verses. In Rev 8:5, ebalen (aorist) describes the angel casting the fire-filled censer to earth. Common apocalyptic collocation: ballo + pyr (fire), ballo + gē (earth).

**Semantic categories:** apocalyptic casting down, throwing/casting objects

**Key verses:** Rev 8:5, Rev 8:8

**Relevance:** The casting action (ebalen) in 8:5 marks the decisive judgment transition — the censer that held incense for prayer now casts fire for judgment. The verb's forceful connotation (throwing, not gently placing) underscores the violence of the transition.

### G1556 — ekdikeo

**Occurrences:** 6

ekdikeo (avenge/vindicate) — the vindication verb tracking across the altar arc. ekdikeis (present, 6:10) 'are you not avenging?' → exedikēsen (aorist, 19:2) 'hath avenged.' The tense shift from present demand to aorist completion spans the entire seal-trumpet-bowl sequence.

**Semantic categories:** vindication, divine justice

**Key verses:** Rev 6:10, Rev 19:2

**Relevance:** The grammatical arc from ekdikeis (present, 6:10) to exedikēsen (aorist, 19:2) provides one of the strongest structural arguments for reading the three judgment sequences as a unified narrative rather than parallel/recapitulating sequences.
