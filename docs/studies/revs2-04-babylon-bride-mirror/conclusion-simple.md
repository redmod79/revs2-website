# Babylon-Bride Mirror -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

Revelation contains two major city-woman visions: the harlot/Babylon (chapters 17-18) and the bride/New Jerusalem (chapters 21-22). This study examines how those two sections are deliberately constructed as a mirror image of each other — point by point, in the same sequence, using the same vocabulary to describe opposite things.

---

## The Two Visions Open with Nearly Identical Words

The angel introduction at the start of each vision is almost word-for-word the same. In Revelation 17:1 and 3, an angel comes, invites John to come and see, and carries him away in the Spirit to show him a woman. In Revelation 21:9-10, an angel comes, invites John to come and see, and carries him away in the Spirit to show him a woman. Six specific phrases are verbatim in the Greek original.

> *And there came one of the seven angels which had the seven vials, and talked with me, saying unto me, Come hither; I will shew unto thee the judgment of the great whore... So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast.* (Rev 17:1, 3)

> *And there came unto me one of the seven angels which had the seven vials... and talked with me, saying, Come hither, I will shew thee the bride, the Lamb's wife. And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain, and shewed me that great city, the holy Jerusalem.* (Rev 21:9-10)

The substitutions are the point. Where one says judgment, the other says bride. Where one says wilderness, the other says mountain. Both sections also close with the same scene: John falls to worship the angel and is told not to. These matching openings and closings function like brackets, marking both visions as a single paired unit.

---

## What Each City Is Made Of

The harlot is dressed in gold, precious stones, and pearls — everything applied from the outside. Babylon is gilded. The New Jerusalem is not decorated with these materials; it is composed of them. Its wall is jasper. Its streets are pure gold. Its foundations are each a different precious stone. Its gates are individual pearls.

> *And the woman was arrayed in purple and scarlet colour, and decked with gold and precious stones and pearls, having a golden cup in her hand full of abominations and filthiness of her fornication.* (Rev 17:4)

> *Having the glory of God: and her light was like unto a stone most precious, even like a jasper stone, clear as crystal.* (Rev 21:11)

The materials overlap — both cities feature gold, precious stones, and pearls — but their relationship to those materials is inverted. One city borrows its glory; the other is its glory.

---

## What Fills Each City

The harlot holds a golden cup full of abominations. The New Jerusalem is filled with the glory of God. The same Greek word for "abomination" appears three times across both sections: filling the harlot's cup (17:4), naming her identity (17:5), and then appearing again as precisely what is excluded from the New Jerusalem (21:27).

> *And there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth, neither whatsoever worketh abomination, or maketh a lie: but they which are written in the Lamb's book of life.* (Rev 21:27)

What fills one city is barred from the other. The same word does the work on both sides of the mirror.

---

## Demons, Tabernacle, Lamp, and Water

Each contrast follows the same pattern: the same vocabulary, opposite outcomes. Babylon becomes a dwelling place for demons (18:2). The New Jerusalem is the tabernacle of God with men (21:3). Babylon's lamp is extinguished forever (18:23). In the New Jerusalem, the Lamb is the lamp and God is the light, with no night ever (21:23; 22:5). Babylon sits on many waters that represent peoples under her control, and she is drunk with the blood of the saints (17:1, 6). The New Jerusalem has a river of the water of life flowing freely from God's throne (22:1), available to anyone who is thirsty at no cost (22:17).

> *And the tabernacle of God is with men, and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his people, and God himself shall be with them, and be their God. And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain.* (Rev 21:3-4)

---

## The Bride Who Was Silenced, Then Speaks

One of the most striking details crosses the mirror boundary. In Revelation 18:23, while describing Babylon's fall, the text says the voice of the bridegroom and the bride will be heard no more in her. The bride is silenced inside Babylon. Then, in Revelation 22:17, the bride speaks: "The Spirit and the bride say, Come." The same word for bride appears on both sides. She is silenced in the Babylon section and restored in the New Jerusalem section — a thread running through the entire mirror.

> *And the Spirit and the bride say, Come. And let him that heareth say, Come. And let him that is athirst come. And whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely.* (Rev 22:17)

And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain, and shewed me that great city, the holy Jerusalem, descending out of heaven from God.* (Rev 21:9-10)
