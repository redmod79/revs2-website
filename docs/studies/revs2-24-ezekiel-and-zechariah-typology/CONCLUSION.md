# CONCLUSION: Ezekiel and Zechariah as OT Templates for Revelation (revs2-24)

## Question

How does Ezekiel serve as the most extensive OT structural template for Revelation? Map: throne vision (Ezek 1 / Rev 4), scroll eating (Ezek 2:9-3:3 / Rev 10:8-10), temple measuring (Ezek 40-42 / Rev 11:1-2), Gog and Magog (Ezek 38-39 / Rev 20:7-10), river of life (Ezek 47:1-12 / Rev 22:1-2). What does Zechariah contribute? Map: horses (Zech 1:8, 6:1-8 / Rev 6:1-8), lampstand (Zech 4:2 / Rev 1:12, 4:5), olive trees (Zech 4:3,11-14 / Rev 11:4), stone with seven eyes (Zech 3:9 / Rev 5:6), measuring (Zech 2:1-2 / Rev 11:1). Where does Revelation transform rather than repeat its source?

---

## Summary Answer

Ezekiel is Revelation's primary Old Testament structural template. The two books share a parallel sequence of major scenes in the same order — throne vision, prophetic commissioning, protective marking, measuring, Gog-Magog, river of life — and the linguistic connections are precise, running through the LXX vocabulary chains rather than general thematic similarity. But Revelation does not reproduce Ezekiel. Every major parallel contains at least one deliberate transformation: faces are individualized, bitterness is internalized, the outer court is excluded, Gog-Magog is universalized, and the temple is replaced by the throne as the source of the river of life.

Zechariah supplies the symbolic vocabulary. The seven-lamps-to-seven-Spirits chain, the stone-to-Lamb transformation, the olive trees as prophetic witnesses, the horse colors — all come from Zechariah and are distributed through Revelation's throne-room and prophetic-witness passages. Together, Ezekiel provides the structural scaffold; Zechariah provides the symbolic vocabulary for many of the book's most important identifications.

In both cases the pattern is the same: Revelation receives the OT source, preserves its shape, and intensifies its meaning — Christologically, universally, and eschatologically.

## Key Verses

**Ezekiel 1:5-6 (KJV):** "Also out of the midst thereof came the likeness of four living creatures. And this was their appearance; they had the likeness of a man. And every one had four faces, and every one had four wings."

*The four-faces-per-creature baseline. Revelation transforms this to one face per creature (Rev 4:7), while adding six wings from Isaiah 6:2.*

**Ezekiel 3:1-3 (KJV):** "Moreover he said unto me, Son of man, eat that thou findest; eat this roll, and go speak unto the house of Israel. So I opened my mouth, and he caused me to eat that roll. And he said unto me, Son of man, cause thy belly to eat, and fill thy bowels with this roll that I give thee. Then did I eat it; and it was in my mouth as honey for sweetness."

*The scroll-eating source. Sweet only — no bitterness here. The bitterness comes after eating (Ezek 3:14) and as an emotional state, not a bodily one. Revelation relocates the bitterness inside the eating act and inside the body (Rev 10:10).*

**Ezekiel 9:4 (KJV):** "And the LORD said unto him, Go through the midst of the city, through the midst of Jerusalem, and set a mark upon the foreheads of the men that sigh and that cry for all the abominations that be done in the midst thereof."

*The protective forehead mark before judgment — the structural pattern preserved exactly in Rev 7:3.*

**Ezekiel 43:2 (KJV):** "And, behold, the glory of the God of Israel came from the way of the east: and his voice was like a noise of many waters: and the earth shined with his glory."

*The voice of the returning divine glory. The LXX is virtually word-for-word with Rev 1:15 — the returning kavod's voice is Christ's voice.*

**Ezekiel 47:1 (KJV):** "Afterward he brought me again unto the door of the house; and, behold, waters issued out from under the threshold of the house eastward: for the forefront of the house stood toward the east, and the waters came down from under from the right side of the house, at the south side of the altar."

*River source: the temple threshold. Rev 22:1 changes this to the throne of God and the Lamb — because Rev 21:22 has just declared there is no temple.*

**Ezekiel 48:35 (KJV):** "It was round about eighteen thousand measures: and the name of the city from that day shall be, The LORD is there."

*Ezekiel's last word: the LORD is there. Rev 21:3 fulfills this: "God himself shall be with them." Rev 21:22 transcends it: God himself is the temple.*

**Zechariah 3:9 (KJV):** "For behold the stone that I have laid before Joshua; upon one stone shall be seven eyes: behold, I will engrave the graving thereof, saith the LORD of hosts, and I will remove the iniquity of that land in one day."

*The stone with seven eyes in an atonement context. Rev 5:6 transforms the stone to the Lamb "as it had been slain" — same seven eyes, same atonement context, messianic identity now explicit.*

**Zechariah 4:10 (KJV):** "For who hath despised the day of small things? for they shall rejoice, and shall see the plummet in the hand of Zerubbabel with those seven; they are the eyes of the LORD, which run to and fro through the whole earth."

*Seven lamps = seven eyes = the Spirit running through all the earth. Rev 5:6 makes the equation explicit: the Lamb's seven eyes are "the seven Spirits of God sent forth into all the earth."*

**Revelation 21:22 (KJV):** "And I saw no temple therein: for the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it."

*Three words cancel Ezekiel's nine chapters of temple plans. This is not an oversight. It is the climactic statement of the entire Ezekiel-Revelation relationship.*

**Revelation 22:1-2 (KJV):** "And he shewed me a pure river of water of life, clear as crystal, proceeding out of the throne of God and of the Lamb. In the midst of the street of it, and on either side of the river, was there the tree of life, which bare twelve manner of fruits, and yielded her fruit every month: and the leaves of the tree were for the healing of the nations."

*Three OT sources compressed into two verses: Ezekiel 47 (river, monthly fruit, healing leaves), Genesis 2:9 (tree of life), and the throne sourcing everything because God and the Lamb are the temple.*

## Analysis

### I. The Case for Ezekiel as the Primary OT Template

Scholars sometimes speak loosely about Revelation borrowing from "the Old Testament," as though the allusions are scattered evenly across the Hebrew scriptures. The actual picture is more specific. Of the roughly 404 verses in Revelation, over half contain OT allusions. But the allusions are not evenly distributed: Ezekiel, Isaiah, Daniel, Psalms, and Zechariah supply the great majority. Among these, Ezekiel stands apart as the one book whose macro-structure runs parallel to Revelation's in sequence.

The parallel sequence is:

| Ezekiel | Revelation |
|---------|------------|
| Throne-chariot vision (Ezek 1) | Throne room (Rev 4) |
| Scroll eating and commission (Ezek 2:9-3:3) | Scroll eating and commission (Rev 10:8-11) |
| Forehead mark before judgment (Ezek 9:4) | Sealing before judgment (Rev 7:3) |
| Temple measuring (Ezek 40-42) | Temple measuring (Rev 11:1-2) |
| Gog and Magog (Ezek 38-39) | Gog and Magog (Rev 19:17-18; 20:7-10) |
| Glory voice of the returning LORD (Ezek 43:2) | Glory voice of Christ (Rev 1:15) |
| River from the sanctuary (Ezek 47:1-12) | River from the throne (Rev 22:1-2) |
| The LORD is there (Ezek 48:35) | God himself with them (Rev 21:3,22) |

These are not isolated allusions; they are a structural sequence. The throne vision begins both books' central vision cycles. The scroll eating commissions the prophet for his task. The forehead mark protects before judgment falls. The measuring scene establishes what belongs to God. The Gog-Magog scene climaxes the eschatological conflict. The river scene concludes the vision in life and healing. Kowalski (2004) documented seven such macro-structural correspondences. The textual evidence confirms the core of that finding.

The connections are confirmed by LXX vocabulary chains, not merely thematic similarity. Where Ezekiel uses H7070 qaneh (measuring rod), the LXX renders it G2563 (kalamos); the qaneh/kalamos co-pair appears 16 times in the LXX (PMI 9.09). Revelation uses kalamos at Rev 11:1. Where Ezekiel uses H5104 nahar (river), the LXX renders it G4215 (potamos); the nahar/potamos co-pair appears 101 times in the LXX (PMI 7.03). Revelation uses potamos for the river of life at Rev 22:1. Where Ezekiel describes the returning glory's voice as "noise of many waters," the LXX renders this phōnē hydatōn; Revelation 1:15 reproduces this phrase nearly verbatim and applies it to Christ. Thematic connections can be coincidental; lexical chains through the LXX are literary evidence of deliberate engagement.

The Sirach 49:8 testimony confirms what first-century readers already knew: Ezekiel's chariot vision (the merkavah) was the canonical throne-vision of Second Temple Judaism, "the glorious vision upon the chariot of the cherubims." When John wrote Rev 4, his audience would recognize every element — and every deviation. The deviations were meant to be noticed.

### II. The Ezekiel Parallels: Six Transformations

**The Throne Vision (Ezek 1 / Rev 4)**

Ezekiel's chariot vision and Revelation 4 share eight structural elements: four living creatures, crystal firmament/sea, divine throne, rainbow, fire and lightning, the creatures' worship, the creatures' appearance, and the transport-to-stationary setting. VP187 Strong documents these eight elements.

But six differences are equally deliberate:

1. *Face count.* Ezek 1:6,10 — four faces per creature. Rev 4:7 — one face per creature. The individualization is confirmed by the four homoion phrases, each with a single noun (lion / calf / man / eagle), distributed one per creature. What each creature shared collectively in Ezekiel, each now embodies individually in Revelation.

2. *Wing count.* Ezek 1:6 — four wings. Rev 4:8 — six wings. The source for six wings is Isaiah 6:2, not Ezekiel. Revelation composites both throne visions: Ezekiel for the overall structure; Isaiah for the wing count and the trisagion ("Holy, holy, holy" is in Isa 6:3 but absent from Ezek 1).

3. *Eye location.* Ezek 1:18 — eyes in the rims of the wheels (gabbot = wheel rims). Rev 4:6 — eyes on the creatures' bodies, front and back. The relocation moves divine omniscience from the transport mechanism to the living beings themselves.

4. *Crystal position.* Ezek 1:22 — crystal firmament above the creatures, forming the floor of the divine throne space. Rev 4:6 — sea of glass like crystal before and below the throne. The position shifts because the viewing angle shifts: Ezekiel looks up from earth into the chariot; John stands in the throne room looking at the sea below.

5. *Term choice.* Ezek 10:20 identifies the creatures as H3742 kerubim (cherubim). Revelation uses G2226 ζῶον (living creature), the LXX rendering of H2416 chay (the root of chayyot). This is a deliberate terminological choice: Rev 4 bypasses the cherub identity (with its Eden-guardian and fallen-cherub associations from Ezek 28) in favor of the life-creature identity.

6. *Transport to fixed.* Ezekiel's creatures are a chariot; they move. Revelation's creatures remain in the throne room throughout the book. The mobility of the divine presence in Ezekiel (the chariot could go anywhere) becomes in Revelation a permanent fixed throne, while the seven Spirits are "sent forth into all the earth" (Rev 5:6).

The overall transformation type is merger-and-intensification: Revelation merges Ezek 1 with Isa 6, intensifies the features it keeps, and selectively transforms the rest.

**Scroll Eating (Ezek 2:9-3:3,14 / Rev 10:8-11)**

Both scenes share the same sequence: vision of a scroll → command to eat the scroll → the eating → commission to speak to a people. Five elements are held in common (scroll, eat, sweet, honey, prophetic commission). AN037 Strong.

The transformation centers on bitterness. Ezekiel eats the scroll in Ezek 3:1-3; it is sweet as honey. The bitterness comes later — in Ezek 3:14, Ezekiel goes "in bitterness, in the heat of my spirit" to the captives at Tel-abib. The Hebrew mar is an adjective describing Ezekiel's emotional state, not a digestive event. Joseph Mede noted this accurately: "there is no reference, or but an obscure one, to bitterness in the belly" in Ezekiel.

In Revelation, the angel warns that the scroll "shall make thy belly bitter" (pikranei, future active indicative, Rev 10:9). After eating: "it was in my mouth sweet as honey: and as soon as I had eaten it, my belly was bitter" (epikranthē, aorist passive indicative; subject: hē koilia, "my belly," Rev 10:10). The belly is the grammatical subject undergoing bitterness as a completed past event.

The transformation: external emotional aftermath (Ezek 3:14) → intrinsic bodily immediacy (Rev 10:10). What was sequential and emotional in Ezekiel becomes simultaneous and physical in Revelation. The prophetic word itself contains both dimensions: it is sweet in the receiving and bitter in the bearing, because it includes both divine promise (sweet) and divine judgment (bitter). This reinterpretation deepens Ezekiel's original rather than changing its direction.

**Temple Measuring (Ezek 40-42 / Rev 11:1-2)**

Ezekiel's temple vision (Ezek 40-48) spans nine chapters with exhaustive measurements — inner court, outer court, gates, walls, chambers, altars. Nothing is excluded. The measuring is comprehensive.

Revelation inherits the measuring instrument (kalamos, from qaneh through the LXX, confirmed lexically) and the general idea of a commissioned measuring of the sanctuary. But the act is radically different: "rise, and measure the temple of God, and the altar, and them that worship therein. But the court which is without the temple leave out, and measure it not; for it is given unto the Gentiles" (Rev 11:1-2).

The transformation is selective exclusion. Where Ezekiel measures everything, Revelation measures only what God protects. The measuring act has become a declaration of ownership and protection: what is measured belongs to God; what is excluded is given over. The outer court (aulē, G833, its only occurrence in Revelation) is surrendered to 42 months of Gentile trampling. The naos (inner sanctuary), the altar, and the worshippers are measured and thereby claimed.

The contrast with Rev 21:15-17 completes the arc. In Rev 21, an angel measures the New Jerusalem with a golden reed (chrysoun kalamon — the same kalamos, now golden). There is no exclusion command. No outer court is surrendered. Everything is measured, and the specifications are glorious. The mortal reed of Rev 11 represents the church's measuring function under conditions of persecution and limitation; the golden reed of Rev 21 represents the completed eschatological reality where nothing is given over and all is claimed.

Moyise and Dulk have independently recognized this measuring arc. It is confirmed by the textual data.

**Gog and Magog (Ezek 38-39 / Rev 19:17-18 + Rev 20:7-10)**

This is the most structurally complex parallel because Revelation deploys the Ezekiel material in two places separated by the millennium.

Ezekiel 38-39 is a single oracle: a named coalition (Gog, prince of Meshech and Tubal, in the land of Magog, with Persia, Ethiopia, Libya, Gomer, Togarmah) comes against Israel from the north in the latter days. God defeats them with pestilence, blood, hailstones, fire and brimstone. The bodies of the fallen are eaten by birds (Ezek 39:17-20), and fire falls on Magog (Ezek 39:6). It is a single continuous narrative.

Revelation distributes this material:

- Bird feast (Ezek 39:17-20) → Rev 19:17-18: "Come and gather yourselves together unto the supper of the great God; that ye may eat the flesh of kings, and the flesh of captains, and the flesh of mighty men." This appears in Rev 19, at the return of Christ, before the millennium. VP166 Strong.

- Gog and Magog names + fire-from-heaven defeat (Ezek 38:2; 38:22; 39:6) → Rev 20:7-10: post-millennium rebellion; "Gog and Magog" appear as a collective appositive for all the nations of the four corners; fire from God devours them. VP190 Strong.

The Ezek 38-39 oracle's sequential elements are split across the millennium boundary. This is decisive evidence against the same-event reading: if Rev 20:7-10 is simply Ezek 38-39 at one point in time, why does the bird feast (Ezek 39:17-20, sequential with and following the military defeat of Gog) appear in Rev 19 rather than Rev 20?

The transformation within Rev 20:7-10 is universalization. Ezek 38:2-6 specifies a named ruler (Gog), a territory (land of Magog), a direction of origin (north, v.15), and an enumerated coalition (seven named peoples). Rev 20:8 dissolves all of this: Greek parser confirms Gōg kai Magōg appear in the accusative as appositive to ta ethnē, "the nations which are in the four quarters of the earth." Gog and Magog are not individuals here — they ARE the nations; they have become a collective typological label for all unredeemed humanity from all four compass directions. The geographic and personal specificity of Ezekiel is universalized into a cosmic statement about the totality of God's enemies.

The deception mechanism (planao, G4105, "to deceive," Rev 20:8) adds a new feature absent from Ezekiel's version. In Ezekiel, God himself brings Gog against Israel (Ezek 38:4,16: "I will bring thee forth"). In Revelation, Satan goes out to deceive the nations. The final assault is not divinely orchestrated through an enemy but directly initiated by Satan. This is consistent with Rev 20's trajectory: the dragon is the direct agent of the final rebellion, no longer working through intermediaries (beast, false prophet, Babylon), all of whom have already fallen.

**River of Life (Ezek 47:1-12 / Rev 22:1-2)**

The river-of-life parallel involves a three-part transformation that corresponds exactly to the three-part temple negation of Rev 21:22.

In Ezekiel, the river flows from under the threshold (miphtan) of the house (temple). The source is explicitly institutional: the physical sanctuary building. Trees on both banks are unspecified ("very many trees"). The healing is individual and localized: "the leaf thereof for medicine."

In Revelation, the river flows from "the throne of God and of the Lamb." The source is personal: the divine persons themselves, now declared to be the temple (Rev 21:22 — "I saw no temple therein: for the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it"). The trees are identified as "the tree of life" — the Gen 2:9 name imported from Eden, compositing two OT sources in a single scene (SP107). The healing is universal and national: "healing of the nations."

The logic is explicit: Rev 21:22 cancels the temple. Therefore the river (which in Ezekiel flows from the temple) must in Rev 22 flow from whatever has replaced the temple. What has replaced the temple? The throne of God and the Lamb. So the river flows from the throne. The transformation is required by the theological statement of Rev 21:22; it is not optional.

The monthly fruit detail (Rev 22:2, "yielded her fruit every month") is preserved from Ezek 47:12. This single preserved detail is the thread confirming the Ezekiel source while everything else around it transforms.

Three contributing voices feed the river-of-life tradition (Ezek 47; Joel 3:18; Zech 13:1), all maintaining the temple-source language. Revelation transforms all three by making God himself the source.

### III. The Zechariah Parallels: Five Connections

**Horse Colors (Zech 1:8, 6:1-8 / Rev 6:1-8)**

Zechariah has two horse-color passages. Zech 1:8 shows red, speckled, and white horses patrolling the earth — a three-horse patrol-and-report mission. Zech 6:1-8 shows four chariots with four color-categories (red, black, white, grisled/bay) sent as "the four spirits of the heavens, which go forth from standing before the Lord." Their mission is to execute God's purposes to the four compass directions and "quiet my spirit in the north country."

Revelation 6:1-8 has four horsemen in four colors: white (conquest), red (war), black (famine), pale (death). Three colors overlap (white, red, black); the fourth differs. Zech 6's fourth color is grisled/barod (LXX: psaroi kai poikiloi, "speckled and many-colored") — confirmed by the LXX text to use different vocabulary from Rev 6:8's chloros (G5515). G5515 chloros does not appear in LXX Zechariah for horse colors.

The fourth horse is Revelation's own addition: a death-pallor horse unique to this passage. G5515 chloros has a botanical sense (green, Mark 6:39; Rev 8:7; 9:4) and a death-pallor sense (Rev 6:8). The death-pallor sense creates a four-color set where white = conquest, red = war, black = famine, and pale-green = death.

The function transformation is equally significant. Zechariah's horses patrol the earth, observe it to be at rest, and report back (Zech 1:11). They go forth to execute God's purposes (Zech 6:5-8). Revelation's horsemen go to execute judgment sequentially: conquest, war removed, famine, death. The movement from observing agents to executing agents is a function transformation.

Crucially, the content of the pale horse's mission (sword, hunger, death, beasts of the earth) comes not from Zechariah but from Ezekiel 14:21 — the fourfold judgment formula (VP074 Strong). The four horsemen scene composites Zechariah's form (colored horses sent by God) with Ezekiel's content (the fourfold death mechanism). This is a clear example of Revelation taking the visual image from one OT source and the content from another.

**Lampstand (Zech 4:2 / Rev 1:12, 4:5)**

Zechariah sees a golden lampstand (menorah) with a bowl on top and seven lamps with seven pipes feeding them oil from two olive trees. The decoder comes in Zech 4:6: "Not by might, nor by power, but by my Spirit, saith the LORD."

Revelation uses the lampstand symbol at two points. At Rev 1:12-16, John sees seven golden lampstands (lychnia, G3087), and in Rev 1:20 John receives the decoder: "The seven candlesticks which thou sawest are the seven churches." At Rev 4:5, seven lamps of fire burn before the throne, decoded as "the seven Spirits of God."

G3087 lychnia has 11 NT occurrences. Six are in Revelation; the others are in Matthew 5:15 and Hebrews 9:2. Revelation is by far the dominant NT user of this term. The lampstand has become the primary symbol for the church's witness function (Rev 1:20) and for the sevenfold Spirit before the throne (Rev 4:5).

At Rev 4:5 the term shifts from G3087 lychnia (lampstand) to G2985 lampas (lamp). This mirrors the Zechariah source precisely: Zech 4:2 shows a lychnia (lampstand) with seven nerot (lamps/lights). Revelation has lychnia for the lampstands (the structures that hold the churches) and lampades for the seven burning lamps before the throne (the seven Spirits). The distinction between lampstand and lamp is preserved from Zechariah into Revelation.

**Olive Trees (Zech 4:3,11-14 / Rev 11:4)**

Zechariah's lampstand vision includes two olive trees flanking the lampstand. In Zech 4:14 they are decoded as "the two anointed ones, that stand by the Lord of the whole earth" — historically Joshua (high priest) and Zerubbabel (civil leader). The olive trees supply oil to the lampstand through golden pipes; they are distinct from the lampstand.

AN038 Explicit: Zech 4:3 has "two olive trees... one upon the right side of the bowl, and the other upon the left side thereof." Rev 11:4 has "these are the two olive trees, and the two candlesticks standing before the God of the earth" — nearly verbatim including the LXX vocabulary chain (H2132 zayith → G1636 elaiai, 23 co-occurrences, PMI 9.24). This is the strongest single lexical link between Zechariah and Revelation — PMI 9.24 for elaiai is one of the highest in this study's dataset.

The merger transformation in Rev 11:4 is critical: "These are the two olive trees, AND the two candlesticks." In Zechariah, olive trees and lampstand are separate. The olive trees supply the lampstand. They are not the same thing. In Revelation 11:4, the two witnesses ARE BOTH simultaneously — both olive trees AND lampstands. The witness-pair is the anointed ministers (olive trees = anointed ones, Zech 4:14) and the light-bearing community (lampstands = churches, Rev 1:20) at the same time.

The Greek constructio ad sensum in Rev 11:4 confirms the persons-not-objects reading: houtoi (masculine demonstrative) with hai duo elaiai kai hai duo lychniai (both feminine plural) + hestōtes (masculine perfect active participle). The masculine forms override the feminine grammatical gender of the nouns, indicating the referents are persons. TM214.

**Stone with Seven Eyes (Zech 3:9 / Rev 5:6)**

This is the most theologically precise connection between Zechariah and Revelation.

In Zech 3:1-9, Joshua the high priest stands in a divine courtroom. Satan accuses; God rebukes Satan; Joshua's filthy garments are removed; clean robes are given. Then in v.9: "upon one stone shall be seven eyes: behold, I will engrave the graving thereof, saith the LORD of hosts, and I will remove the iniquity of that land in one day."

The stone with seven eyes appears in an atonement context: the specific promise is that iniquity will be removed "in one day." Seven eyes appear on an object associated with cleansing from sin.

LXX Zech 3:9: ophthalmoi hepta. Rev 5:6: "a Lamb as it had been slain, having seven horns and seven eyes, which are the seven Spirits of God sent forth into all the earth" — ophthalmous hepta. The phrase is identical. The atonement context is preserved: the Lamb "as it had been slain" (esphagmenon, perfect passive participle — permanent ongoing sacrificial state). The "one day" of Zech 3:9 corresponds to the once-for-all sacrifice of Rev 5:9-10.

The object transforms: stone → Lamb. The context preserves: atonement/cleansing → slain sacrifice. The symbolism carries: seven eyes = seven Spirits (decoded in Rev 5:6 through the Zech 4:10 chain — the eyes of the LORD running through all the earth). This is VP099 Strong (5 shared elements: seven, eyes, sent, whole, earth).

The transformation type is Christological: Zechariah's messianic atonement-stone becomes Revelation's messianic slain Lamb. The seven eyes that represent divine omniscience in Zech 3-4 now belong to the Lamb, identifying the Lamb as the agent of that omniscience — "the seven Spirits of God sent forth into all the earth."

**Measuring (Zech 2:1-2 / Rev 11:1)**

This is Zechariah's weakest contribution to Revelation. A man with a measuring line (chevel, H2256, cord/rope) appears in Zech 2:1-2 to measure Jerusalem's breadth and length.

The measuring theme is shared with Rev 11:1-2, but the instrument differs: Zech 2:1 uses a cord (chevel), while Rev 11:1 uses a reed like a rod (kalamos homoios rabdō, G2563 kalamos). The primary lexical source for Rev 11:1's measuring instrument is Ezekiel 40:3 (qaneh → kalamos, confirmed LXX chain), not Zechariah 2:1 (which uses a different instrument with a different vocabulary chain). AN093 Probable (thematic only; no shared lexical chain).

The Zechariah connection to the measuring scene is therefore thematic: both involve a person approaching a holy city or space with a measuring instrument. But the instrument, the task, and the theological content (selective exclusion of the outer court) all derive from Ezekiel 40, not Zechariah 2.

### IV. Where Revelation Transforms Its Sources

The analysis yields eight transformation types across the two prophets:

**Individualization:** Four faces per creature (Ezek 1:6,10) → one face per creature (Rev 4:7). A feature shared collectively by all four creatures in Ezekiel is distributed individually in Revelation.

**Merger (OT source):** Separate OT passages combined into a single scene. Rev 4 = Ezek 1 + Isa 6. Rev 22:1-2 = Ezek 47 + Gen 2:9. Rev 6:8 = Zech 6 (form) + Ezek 14:21 (content). Rev 11:4 = Zech 4:3 (olive trees) + Zech 4:14 (anointed ones) + Rev 1:20 decoder (lampstands = churches).

**Merger (Zechariah symbols):** Separate symbolic objects (olive trees ≠ lampstand in Zech 4) become the same persons in Rev 11:4. Witnesses = both olive trees and lampstands simultaneously.

**Relocation:** Eyes move from wheel rims (Ezek 1:18) to creature bodies (Rev 4:6). Bitterness moves from external emotional state (Ezek 3:14) to intrinsic bodily content (Rev 10:10). Crystal firmament moves from above creatures (Ezek 1:22) to sea below throne (Rev 4:6).

**Selective exclusion:** Ezekiel measures everything (Ezek 40-42). Revelation measures only naos, altar, worshippers; the outer court is excluded and given over (Rev 11:1-2).

**Universalization:** Named coalition from the north (Ezek 38:2-6,15) → nations from all four corners of the earth (Rev 20:8). Local Jerusalem judgment protected by forehead mark (Ezek 9:4) → cosmic judgment, with sealing of all God's servants (Rev 7:3).

**Source transcendence:** River flows from temple threshold (Ezek 47:1) → river flows from throne of God and Lamb (Rev 22:1). The source transforms because the institutional structure (temple) has been replaced by the divine persons (Rev 21:22).

**Christological:** The returning kavod's voice (Ezek 43:2) = Christ's voice (Rev 1:15). The atonement stone with seven eyes (Zech 3:9) = the slain Lamb with seven eyes (Rev 5:6). The river from the temple = the river from the throne of God AND the Lamb (Rev 22:1). Every major parallel, when made Christological, places Christ in the position previously occupied by the divine glory, the atoning symbol, or the source of life.

The cumulative direction of all eight transformation types is consistent: Revelation intensifies, universalizes, and makes Christological everything it receives from Ezekiel and Zechariah. The pattern is not random modification. It is a systematic reinterpretation that preserves the shape of the OT sources while radically elevating their content.

### V. The Gog-Magog Split and the Typological Method

The typological split of the Gog-Magog material deserves separate treatment because it reveals Revelation's compositional method most clearly.

Ezekiel 38-39 is a sequential narrative: named coalition gathers → comes against Israel → fire and brimstone from heaven → bird feast on the fallen. It is a single event described continuously.

Revelation distributes this material across the millennium boundary:
- Rev 19:17-18: bird feast (from Ezek 39:17-20)
- Rev 20:7-10: Gog-Magog rebellion with fire-from-heaven defeat (from Ezek 38-39)

If Revelation treats Ezekiel's Gog-Magog as a single literal future event, we would expect the bird feast to appear in Rev 20, following the battle of Rev 20:7-10 in sequence. Instead, the bird feast appears in Rev 19, applied to the fall of Babylon's military power at the return of Christ. Then the Gog-Magog names and fire-from-heaven defeat appear in Rev 20, applied to the post-millennial final rebellion.

The most natural explanation is typological use. Revelation treats the Ezekiel Gog-Magog complex as a pattern — a template for how God defeats his enemies — and applies that pattern twice: once to the pre-millennial climax and once to the post-millennial climax. This is what OT typology does: a single historical or prophetic event becomes the pattern that subsequent events fulfill and surpass.

This finding also clarifies the universalization of Rev 20:8. In Ezekiel, Gog is a historical figure and Magog is a territory. In Rev 20:8, "Gog and Magog" are applied as a collective label to all nations from all four compass directions. The named figures have become a typological designation for every opponent of God's people at the end of history. The universalization is the sign of typological application: when a specific name becomes a label for a universal category, the text is using the original typologically rather than historically.

EGW's identification of Gog and Magog as "Satan and armies of the damned" (JTLD 383.2) is consistent with the post-millennial setting of Rev 20:7-10: at this point the dead wicked have been raised and Satan has been released; the "armies" in the Rev 20 scene are the resurrected wicked gathered for the final assault. The typological label "Gog and Magog" covers this entire scenario.

### VII. The Coherence of the Transformation Pattern

Looking across all eleven parallels, the transformation types are not random. They move in a consistent direction:

- *Every geographic limit is removed.* Jerusalem (Ezek 9:4) → all of God's servants (Rev 7:3). Northern coalition (Ezek 38:15) → four corners of the earth (Rev 20:8). Localized healing (Ezek 47:12) → healing of the nations (Rev 22:2).

- *Every institutional structure is transcended.* The temple (Ezek 40-48) → "I saw no temple" and God himself is the temple (Rev 21:22). The river from the temple threshold (Ezek 47:1) → the river from the throne (Rev 22:1).

- *Every symbol is Christologically focused.* The returning kavod's voice → Christ's voice (Rev 1:15). The messianic atonement stone → the slain Lamb (Rev 5:6). The river from the divine dwelling → the river from the throne of God and the Lamb (Rev 22:1).

- *Every transformation deepens rather than discards.* The scroll is still sweet — and now also bears the weight of judgment (Rev 10:10). The creatures still have all four face-types — now embodied individually rather than collectively (Rev 4:7). The temple is still present as a concept — now realized in the persons of God and the Lamb (Rev 21:22). The measuring still happens — but now the criterion is divine protection rather than architectural completeness (Rev 11:1-2).

Moyise describes this as "radical reinterpretation." That phrase is accurate in the sense that Revelation goes to the root of what Ezekiel promises and reconstructs the fulfillment at a higher level. But it is not arbitrary — the reconstruction is principled and consistent.

The principle appears to be this: everything that Ezekiel locates in a physical institution (temple, city, land, people-group, direction) Revelation relocates in a person — the Lamb who was slain, the throne of God and of the Lamb, the God who IS the temple. The transformation is Christological and personal at its core. The institution was always pointing to the person. Revelation, drawing on Ezekiel and Zechariah, shows that the person has now arrived.

## Word Studies

**G2226 ζῶον / H2416 chay (living creature):** The deliberate choice of ζῶον over cheroubim in Rev 4 preserves the life-creature identity (from H2416 chayyot) while bypassing the sanctuary-guardian and fallen-cherub associations of H3742 kerub. 17 of 23 NT occurrences are in Revelation.

**G2563 kalamos / H7070 qaneh (measuring reed):** LXX chain confirmed (16 co-occurrences, PMI 9.09). The three Revelation uses (11:1; 21:15; 21:16) form the measuring arc: mortal reed under persecution → golden reed in eschatological glory.

**G4215 potamos / H5104 nahar (river):** LXX chain confirmed; nahar/potamos co-pair appears 101 times in the LXX (PMI 7.03). Eight of 16 NT occurrences in Revelation. The river chain connects Ezek 47 to Rev 22 through an unbroken lexical thread.

**G1636 elaia / H2132 zayith (olive tree):** LXX chain confirmed (23 co-occurrences, PMI 9.24). The only symbolic use for persons in the NT is Rev 11:4. The high PMI score confirms a tight LXX rendering relationship.

**G4087 πικραίνω (to embitter):** Four of six NT occurrences in Revelation; two in Rev 10:9,10 (the scroll-eating transformation). The verb makes explicit what Ezekiel left as an emotional adjective: the digested prophetic word itself is embitterment-inducing.

**G5515 chloros (pale/green):** Not in LXX Zechariah for horse colors. The fourth horseman's color is Revelation's own invention. The death-pallor sense at Rev 6:8 is distinct from the botanical-green sense used elsewhere.

**G4450 pyrrhos (fiery red):** Two NT occurrences only — Rev 6:4 (red horse) and Rev 12:3 (red dragon). TM212 exclusive vocabulary creates a structural link between the war horseman and the dragon who sponsors him.

**G4105 planao (to deceive):** Nine Revelation occurrences (highest per-book NT density). The Gog-Magog gathering (Rev 20:8) is the culmination of the deception chain: Satan deceives → nations deceived → final assault. Absent from Ezekiel's version of the Gog-Magog oracle.

**G3354 metreo (to measure):** Five of 10 NT occurrences in Revelation. The measuring verb carries opposite theological valences in Rev 11 (measure = protect; do not measure = surrender) and Rev 21 (full glorious measurement, nothing surrendered).

## Difficult Passages

**The same-event reading of Gog and Magog (Riddlebarger):** The amillennial reading argues Ezek 38-39 and Rev 20:7-10 describe a single event. The bird-feast split refutes this without requiring a specific millennial view. If Rev 20 is the Ezek 38-39 event, the bird feast (Ezek 39:17-20) should appear in Rev 20, following the battle described there. It appears in Rev 19 instead. The simplest explanation is typological: Revelation applies the Gog-Magog pattern to two separate occasions, not one. This is how typology works and it is what the text shows.

**Bohr's OT/NT lampstand-doubling claim:** The multiplication from one lampstand (Zech 4) to seven lampstands (Rev 1:12-16) to two lampstands (Rev 11:4) does not support an OT/NT distinction. Rev 1:20 decodes lampstands as churches, not as OT or NT eras. The two lampstands of Rev 11:4 most naturally represent the two witnesses as embodying the church's prophetic witness function — two as the minimum for legal testimony (Deut 17:6; Matt 18:16). The text supplies no OT/NT rationale for the lampstand count.

**The face-type assignment problem:** Various commentators assign symbolic meanings to the four face-types (Haskell: lion=kingly, ox=patient, man=rational, eagle=swift; Miller: four historical church periods). Neither Ezekiel, Isaiah, nor Revelation provides an internal key for the face-types. The four face-types likely represent the four categories of earthly creatures — wild predator, domestic work animal, human, flying creature — showing that the full scope of creation stands in worship before the throne. Any more specific assignment is external interpretation, not textual decoding.

**The mercy-seat connection (EGW):** EGW states the river of life flows from the mercy-seat. Rev 22:1 says it flows from the throne of God and the Lamb. The throne of grace (Heb 4:16) has mercy-seat connotations, and the connection is theologically coherent. But the text of Rev 22:1 specifies the throne, not the mercy seat, as the source. The mercy-seat connection is a permitted theological inference, not a textual statement.

## Conclusion

Ezekiel is Revelation's primary structural OT template. The evidence is both quantitative (eight matching macro-structural parallels in sequence) and qualitative (confirmed LXX vocabulary chains, not merely thematic similarity). Zechariah functions as the secondary symbolic vocabulary source, supplying the key identifications for the throne room (seven lamps = seven Spirits), the Lamb (stone with seven eyes → Lamb with seven eyes), the two witnesses (olive trees and lampstands), and the colored horsemen (horse-color imagery with function transformed).

In every case, Revelation does not simply reproduce its sources. It receives them, preserves their shape, and transforms their content in three consistent directions: universalization (local → cosmic), source transcendence (institution → person), and Christological identification (divine glory → Christ, atoning symbol → Lamb, life-source → throne of God and the Lamb).

The climactic statement is Rev 21:22 — three Greek words ("I saw no temple") canceling Ezekiel's nine chapters of temple specifications. This is not a rejection of Ezekiel. It is the declaration that what Ezekiel's temple was pointing to has arrived. When God himself is the temple, no building is needed. When the throne of God and the Lamb is the source, no threshold is needed. When the Lamb who was slain stands in the heavenly court, no atonement stone is needed — the reality the stone represented has come.

Revelation receives Ezekiel and Zechariah as its most extensive OT structural and symbolic sources, then transforms them into something that exceeds its sources — not by discarding them, but by showing what they were always pointing toward.
