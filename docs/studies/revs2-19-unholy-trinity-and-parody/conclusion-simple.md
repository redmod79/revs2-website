# Unholy Trinity & Parody -- Plain-English Summary

## A Plain-English Summary

Revelation 12–13 does not just introduce a villain. It constructs a three-member counterfeit of the divine Trinity — dragon, beast, and false prophet — using the same specific words, grammatical forms, and structural patterns as the divine original. This study traced every verbal and structural link between the parody system and its counterpart, showing that the imitation is precise enough to be deliberate at every level of the text.

---

## The Three Members and Their Roles

The dragon functions as the Father-figure in the parody. He holds a throne (Rev 2:13 notes "Satan's throne"), transfers authority to the beast, and receives worship alongside him:

> And they worshipped the dragon which gave power unto the beast: and they worshipped the beast, saying, Who is like unto the beast? who is able to make war with him? (Rev 13:4)

The beast mirrors the Son — he receives a formal authority transfer, suffers a wound that is healed, and draws universal worship. The false prophet mirrors the Holy Spirit — appearing lamb-like while speaking for another, directing worship toward the beast just as the Spirit glorifies Christ, and dispensing breath to animate the beast's image.

---

## The Grammar Exposes the Limit of the Parody

One of the study's central findings is hidden in translation. In Revelation 13, the dragon gives the beast his power and throne using an active verb — he presents himself as a sovereign giver. But every mandate the beast actually exercises comes through passive constructions: it was *given* to him to make war, power was *given* over all nations, it was *given* to him to speak blasphemies.

> And it was given unto him to make war with the saints, and to overcome them: and power was given unto him over all kindreds, and tongues, and nations. (Rev 13:7)

In Greek, this passive construction signals divine permission without naming God directly. God never appears as a character in chapter 13, but the grammar makes him present at every step as the unnamed authority who bounds the beast's activity. The parody presents itself as independent sovereignty. The grammar discloses it is not.

## The Incomparability Claim

After Israel crossed the Red Sea, they sang:

> Who is like unto thee, O LORD, among the gods? who is like thee, glorious in holiness, fearful in praises, doing wonders? (Exod 15:11)

Revelation 13:4 gives the identical rhetorical structure to the beast's worshippers: "Who is like unto the beast?" The Greek construction is exact — the same interrogative formula Israel used to declare God's uniqueness, now placed in the mouths of those who wonder at a counterfeit resurrection. The audacity is the point: a hymn that belongs only to God is transferred to a creature.

The second question in the same verse — "who is able to make war with him?" — challenges God's role as the almighty one. Revelation 19 answers it directly: Christ comes as the divine warrior who "in righteousness doth judge and make war."

---

## The Lamb's Word Used Once for a Deceiver

The Greek word for "lamb" used throughout Revelation as a title for Christ (*arnion*) appears 30 times in the New Testament. Twenty-nine of those occurrences refer to Jesus. The one exception is Revelation 13:11:

> And I beheld another beast coming up out of the earth; and he had two horns like a lamb, and he spake as a dragon. (Rev 13:11)

The false prophet looks like the Lamb — using the Lamb's own specific word — but speaks like the dragon. The single verse is the clearest description of religious deception in the book: not a figure that looks obviously evil, but one that looks like Jesus while representing the enemy.

---

## The Beast's Formula Cannot Hold the Divine Present

God's identifying formula in Revelation is "who is, and who was, and who is to come" — expressing eternal and essential being. The beast's formula in Revelation 17:8 reaches for the same structure: he *was*, he *is not*, he *shall ascend*. The critical difference is the middle term. God essentially *is*. The beast *is not* — he cannot sustain the divine present.

> The beast that thou sawest was, and is not; and shall ascend out of the bottomless pit, and go into perdition. (Rev 17:8)

His formula also adds a fourth element absent from all divine versions: he goes into perdition. The parody reachesfor resurrection but arrives at destruction. The imitation includes its own ending.

---

## What the Parody Reveals

The study concludes that the imitation proves the original. A parody requires something genuine to imitate. The beast can receive a throne; the Lamb's throne is the divine throne shared with the Father. The false prophet can animate an idol with breath; the Spirit gives eternal life. Three unclean spirits go out from the unholy trinity's mouths; the seven Spirits of God go out into all the earth. The parody is, by its nature, derivative and bounded. The grammar of Revelation makes clear that the original is sovereign.
