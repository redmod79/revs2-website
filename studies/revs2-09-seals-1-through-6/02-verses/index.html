    Verses¶

    Question¶

    What is the internal pattern of the four horsemen (seals 1-4)? Document the formula each horseman follows (living creature says 'Come,' horse appears, rider described, effect stated). How does the fifth seal's altar cry ('How long, O Lord?') introduce the theodicy question that drives the rest of the book? What role does the interlude between seals 6 and 7 (the 144,000 and the great multitude, Rev 7) play — how does it answer the question of 6:17 ('who shall be able to stand?')? What is the structural relationship between the seals and the Olivet Discourse (Matt 24)?

    First seal: white horse with bow and stephanos crown, conquering and to conquer¶

    And I saw when the Lamb opened one of the seals, and I heard, as it were the noise of thunder, one of the four beasts saying, Come and see. And I saw, and behold a white horse: and he that sat on him had a bow; and a crown was given unto him: and he went forth conquering, and to conquer.

    Context: The Lamb who was found worthy to open the sealed scroll (Rev 5) now opens the first seal. The first living creature (lion, per Rev 4:7) summons the horseman with 'Come' (Erchou). The formula opens with unique thunder element absent from subsequent seals.

The stephanos crown (G4735) is a victor's wreath, not a royal diadema (G1238). VP073 documents 4 contrastive differences with Rev 19:11.

    Second seal: red horse, power to take peace, given great sword¶

    And when he had opened the second seal, I heard the second beast say, Come and see. And there went out another horse [that was] red: and [power] was given to him that sat thereon to take peace from the earth, and that they should kill one another: and there was given unto him a great sword.

    Context: The second living creature (calf/ox, per Rev 4:7) summons the second horseman. The thunder element is absent. Two divine passives (edothe x2): power given to take peace, and a great sword given.

    Notes: Formula elements: (1) Lamb opens seal, (2) second living creature says 'Come,' (3) red horse (hippos pyrrhos — pyrrhos G4450 only 2x NT: here and Rev 12:3 red dragon), (4) rider given authority (double edothe), (5) effect — peace removed from earth, mutual slaughter (sphazo G4969 future active: sphaxousin). The great sword is machaira megale (G3162), distinct from the rhomphaia (G4501) of Christ's mouth in Rev 19:15.

    Third seal: black horse with balances, famine pricing, oil and wine spared¶

    And when he had opened the third seal, I heard the third beast say, Come and see. And I beheld, and lo a black horse; and he that sat on him had a pair of balances in his hand. And I heard a voice in the midst of the four beasts say, A measure of wheat for a penny, and three measures of barley for a penny; and [see] thou hurt not the oil and the wine.

    Context: The third living creature (human face, per Rev 4:7) summons the third horseman. Unique element: a voice from the midst of the four living creatures announces famine pricing. The divine passive edothe is ABSENT from this seal.

    Notes: Formula elements: (1) Lamb opens seal, (2) third living creature says 'Come,' (3) black horse (hippos melas — melas G3189, 3x NT: Rev 6:5, 6:12, Matt 5:36), (4) rider has balances/scales (zygos G2218, normally 'yoke,' here 'balance'), (5) unique element — voice announces scarcity prices but protects oil and wine. The edothe pattern is absent — the rider's authority is implied by the pricing voice rather than stated via divine passive.

    Fourth seal: pale horse named Death, Hades follows, authority over 1/4 of earth¶

    And when he had opened the fourth seal, I heard the voice of the fourth beast say, Come and see. And I looked, and behold a pale horse: and his name that sat on him was Death, and Hell followed with him. And power was given unto them over the fourth part of the earth, to kill with sword, and with hunger, and with death, and with the beasts of the earth.

    Context: The fourth living creature (eagle, per Rev 4:7) summons the fourth horseman. This is the only seal where the rider is named (Thanatos/Death, G2288) and has a companion (Hades, G86). The fourfold killing means match Ezekiel 14:21 (AN024 Strong).

    Notes: Formula elements: (1) Lamb opens seal, (2) fourth living creature says 'Come,' (3) pale horse (hippos chloros — chloros G5515, normally 'green/verdant' but here 'sickly pallor'), (4) rider NAMED (onoma auto ho Thanatos, TM065), companion Hades follows (akoloutheo imperfect — continuous following), (5) edothe to them (plural — both Death and Hades) authority over 1/4 of earth. Killing means: en rhomphaia (sword, note: Greek uses rhomphaia G4501 here, not machaira), en limo (famine), en thanato (death/pestilence), hypo ton therion (by beasts). This matches Ezek 14:21: sword, famine, noisome beast, pestilence (AN024). Fraction 1/4 begins the escalation pattern SP036 (1/4 seals → 1/3 trumpets → total bowls).

    Fifth seal: altar scene, martyrs' cry, white robes, little season¶

    And when he had opened the fifth seal, I saw under the altar the souls of them that were slain for the word of God, and for the testimony which they held: And they cried with a loud voice, saying, How long, O Lord, holy and true, dost thou not judge and avenge our blood on them that dwell on the earth? And white robes were given unto every one of them; and it was said unto them, that they should rest yet for a little season, until their fellowservants also and their brethren, that should be killed as they [were], should be fulfilled.

    Context: Clear 4+3 structural break (SP025 Strong). Every element of the horseman formula is abandoned: no living creature command, no horse, no rider. Instead: altar scene in heavenly sanctuary. The martyrs are described with the same sphazo vocabulary as the Lamb (esphagmenon, perfect passive participle). The 'how long' cry echoes OT lament tradition (Ps 6:3, 13:1, 79:5, 94:3, Hab 1:2, Zech 1:12). Three temporal markers: (a) esphagmenon — perfect participle, past completed martyrdom; (b) chronon mikron — accusative of duration, future waiting period; (c) mellontes apoktennesthai — present participle + present passive infinitive, future martyrdom. The vindication arc (VP076 Strong) begins here with ekdikeis (present active indicative) and resolves at Rev 19:2 with exedikesen (aorist active indicative).

    Great earthquake in trumpets — structural comparison with sixth seal earthquake¶

    Context: Supporting verse. Structural comparison: seismos megas appears at the sixth seal (6:12), the trumpet interlude (11:13), and the seventh bowl (16:18). The earthquakes escalate across sequences — the sixth seal's earthquake is the first in the series.

    'Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book' — reverses Dan 12:4 sealing¶

    And he saith unto me, Seal not the sayings of the prophecy of this book: for the time is at hand.

    Context: Supporting verse. Reversal of Dan 12:4 ('shut up the words, and seal the book'). Dan 12:4 seals → Rev 5:1 sealed scroll → Lamb opens seals (Rev 6-8:1) → Rev 22:10 commands NOT to seal. The sealed-to-unsealed arc spans both testaments and the entire book of Revelation.

    Notes: sphragises (aorist subjunctive of sphragizo G4972) — 'do not seal.' The prohibition reverses Daniel's command. The reason: 'the time is at hand' (ho kairos engys) — the delay is over, the content must be accessible.

    Feast of Tabernacles: palm branches required — establishes Rev 7:9 Tabernacles allusion¶

    Context: Supporting verse. Tabernacles is the only OT feast explicitly requiring palm branches (kappot temarim). Combined with the skenosei verb in 7:15, this places the interlude in a Tabernacles (post-harvest, post-judgment celebration) context.

    Notes: Palm branches (phoinix G5404) appear only twice in the NT: John 12:13 (triumphal entry) and Rev 7:9 (great multitude). Both are celebration/processional contexts. The Tabernacles requirement establishes the feast-specific allusion in Rev 7.

    Abel slain by Cain — uses sphazo (G4969), linking to Rev 5:6 and 6:9 sphazo chain¶

    Not as Cain, [who] was of that wicked one, and slew his brother. And wherefore slew he him? Because his own works were evil, and his brother's righteous.

    Context: Supporting verse. The sphazo verb (esphaxen, aorist active of G4969) connects Abel's death to the Lamb's (Rev 5:6, esphagmenon) and the martyrs' (Rev 6:9, esphagmenon). Same verb family throughout — establishing a vocabulary chain from the first murder to the eschatological martyrdom.

    Notes: The sphazo chain: 1 John 3:12 (esphaxen, aorist — Abel slain) → Rev 5:6 (esphagmenon, perfect passive — Lamb slain) → Rev 6:9 (esphagmenon, perfect passive — martyrs slain) → Rev 18:24 (esphagmenon — all slain). The verb specifically denotes sacrificial/ritual slaughter, elevating each death into sacrificial register.
