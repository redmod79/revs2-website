    Structural Markers of Revelation (revs2-01)¶

    Study Question¶

    What are the primary structural markers in the book of Revelation? Trace the four "in the Spirit" (en pneumati) markers at Rev 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, and 21:10 — how do they divide the book into four major visions? Trace the "and I saw" (kai eidon) transitions — where do they occur, what do they introduce, and how do they subdivide the visions into scenes? Trace the "after these things" (meta tauta) indicators. How do these three marker systems work together to create Revelation's structural skeleton? For each marker, document the Greek form, every occurrence, and what structural boundary it signals. Note where the markers' structural signals converge and where they create ambiguity.

    Summary Answer¶

    Revelation's structural architecture is built on multiple interlocking marker systems operating at different levels. The four en pneumati markers (Rev 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, 21:10) create the highest-level fourfold division, each paired with a unique location and divided into two verb-pairs (ginomai in Visions 1-2, apophero in Visions 3-4). The kai eidon formula (approximately 35 structural occurrences) operates at the micro-level, subdividing visions into scenes without inherently signaling chronological sequence. The meta tauta eidon formula (five to nine occurrences depending on classification) creates intermediate-level sub-divisions that sometimes cut across the en pneumati boundaries. A fourth system — the Sinai-theophany formula (four occurrences with progressive intensification from three to five-plus elements) — marks structural transitions that none of the three primary markers signals. These systems converge at Rev 4:1-2 (the strongest boundary, where meta tauta eidon and en pneumati co-occur) and diverge at Rev 11:19, 15:5, and 21:1-8, where different marker systems point to different boundaries. The structural evidence is consistent with multiple scholarly models and does not, by itself, resolve the chronological question.

    Key Verses¶

    First en pneumati — Vision 1 begins (Rev 1:10) I was in the Spirit on the Lord's day, and heard behind me a great voice, as of a trumpet,

    Second en pneumati — Vision 2 begins (Rev 4:1-2) After this I looked, and, behold, a door was opened in heaven: and the first voice which I heard was as it were of a trumpet talking with me; which said, Come up hither, and I will shew thee things which must be hereafter. And immediately I was in the spirit: and, behold, a throne was set in heaven, and one sat on the throne.

    Third en pneumati — Vision 3 begins (Rev 17:3) So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast, full of names of blasphemy, having seven heads and ten horns.

    Fourth en pneumati — Vision 4 begins (Rev 21:10) And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain, and shewed me that great city, the holy Jerusalem, descending out of heaven from God,

    Sinai-theophany formula — climactic occurrence (Rev 11:19) And the temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament: and there were lightnings, and voices, and thunderings, and an earthquake, and great hail.

    Chronological neutrality test case (Rev 13:11) And I beheld another beast coming up out of the earth; and he had two horns like a lamb, and he spake as a dragon.

    Prologue-Epilogue inclusio anchor (Rev 22:6) And he said unto me, These sayings are faithful and true: and the Lord God of the holy prophets sent his angel to shew unto his servants the things which must shortly be done.

    Progressive intensification — fraction escalation (Rev 6:8) And I looked, and behold a pale horse: and his name that sat on him was Death, and Hell followed with him. And power was given unto them over the fourth part of the earth, to kill with sword, and with hunger, and with death, and with the beasts of the earth.

    Ezekiel Spirit-transport — OT model (Ezek 40:2) In the visions of God brought he me into the land of Israel, and set me upon a very high mountain, by which was as the frame of a city on the south.

    Analysis¶

    The Four En Pneumati Markers: Revelation's Primary Structural Skeleton¶

    The phrase en pneumati occurs exactly four times in Revelation as a visionary-state formula: at Rev 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, and 21:10. All four use pneuma (G4151, 385 NT occurrences) in the dative singular neuter without the definite article — anarthrous pneumati throughout. This consistency marks a deliberate authorial pattern. Every scholarly model, regardless of its other structural commitments, accepts these four markers as genuine division points. They constitute the least disputed structural observation in the book.

    Each marker is paired with a distinct location. Vision 1 (1:10) is set on Patmos. Vision 2 (4:2) opens in heaven at the throne room. Vision 3 (17:3) opens in a wilderness. Vision 4 (21:10) opens on a great and high mountain. The location pairing is not accidental — it creates a spatial framework for the four visions that mirrors the thematic content of each section.

    The Greek grammar reveals a further structural distinction within the four markers. Visions 1 and 2 use the verb ginomai (G1096, 702 NT occurrences): egenomen en Pneumati — literally "I became in Spirit," with John as the grammatical subject experiencing the visionary state. Visions 3 and 4 shift to the verb apophero (G667, 7 NT occurrences): apenengken me en Pneumati — "he carried me away in Spirit," with the angel as the grammatical subject and John as the object being transported. This verb shift divides the four visions into two pairs: a ginomai pair (Visions 1-2) and an apophero pair (Visions 3-4). The apophero + en Pneumati combination occurs only at Rev 17:3 and 21:10 in the entire NT — a unique verbal pair that links these two passages and separates them from the ginomai pair.

    The Visions 3-4 pair shares an additional structural feature: the angel-introduction formula. At both 17:1-3 and 21:9-10, one of the seven angels who had the seven bowls approaches John, says "Come hither, I will shew thee," and then carries him away in the Spirit to a specific location where he sees a female figure (the harlot in the wilderness at 17:3; the bride on the mountain at 21:10) associated with a great city (Babylon at 17:18; New Jerusalem at 21:10). Six or more identical or near-identical Greek phrases are shared between these two passages, creating what the methodology classifies as a Strong verbal parallel. The deliberate mirroring — harlot vs. bride, wilderness vs. mountain, judgment vs. reward — structures Visions 3 and 4 as contrasting counterparts.

    The grammatical contrast between the en pneumati formula and the "Spirit saith unto the churches" formula within Vision 1 further refines the structural picture. The seven occurrences of "He that hath an ear, let him hear what the Spirit saith unto the churches" (Rev 2:7, 2:11, 2:17, 2:29, 3:6, 3:13, 3:22) use to Pneuma — articular (with the definite article), nominative — where the Spirit is the personal subject who speaks. The en pneumati formula uses pneumati — anarthrous, dative — where pneuma describes the domain or means of John's visionary experience. Same word, different grammatical construction, different structural function. The "Spirit saith" formula is confined entirely to Vision 1; after Rev 3:22 it never appears again. This confinement marks Vision 1 as structurally distinct — a section of direct communication to the churches, unlike the predominantly visionary narrative of Visions 2-4. The word ekklesia (G1577, "church," 118 NT occurrences) reinforces this: it appears 19 times in chs. 1-3 and once in the Epilogue (22:16), but is completely absent from Visions 2, 3, and 4.

    The Ezekiel Spirit-transport passages provide the intertextual model for Revelation's en pneumati/apophero pattern. Ezekiel's visions involve the Spirit lifting or carrying the prophet to specific locations: Jerusalem's temple (Ezek 8:3), Chaldea (11:24), a valley of dry bones (37:1), a very high mountain where a city is visible (40:2). Ezek 37:1 is particularly close to Revelation's Visions 3-4 formula: "carried me out in the spirit of the LORD" — a carrying verb + "in the spirit" construction that parallels apenengken me en Pneumati. Ezek 40:2 directly parallels Rev 21:10: both place the prophet on a high mountain where he sees a city, and both involve Spirit-transport to the location. This Ezekiel parallel operates at what Bandy's three-layer model calls the intertextual structural layer — Revelation inherits and adapts Ezekiel's literary architecture.

    The Kai Eidon System: Scene Subdivision Within Visions¶

    The formula kai eidon ("and I saw") uses the 2nd aorist active indicative 1st person singular of horao (G3708, 415 NT occurrences) — the form eidon. Only this specific form qualifies as a technical structural marker; other seeing verbs (blepo G991, theoreo G2334, deiknymi G1166) serve descriptive functions. The count of kai eidon occurrences is disputed among scholars: Korner counts 44 total (including kai idou forms), of which 40 function as independent structural markers; the prior study revs-02 counted approximately 25 verse-initial occurrences. The discrepancy arises from how broadly one defines the formula — whether to include idou without preceding eidon, whether to count eidon after temporal clauses (like hote enoixen at the seal openings), and whether to count eidon within relative clauses.

    Korner identifies five occurrences where intervening words between kai and eidon signal concurrent description rather than new structural transitions: Rev 1:17 (kai hote eidon auton — "when I saw him"), 9:17 (kai houtos eidon — "thus I saw"), 10:5 (ho angelos hon eidon — "the angel whom I saw"), 13:2 (to therion ho eidon — "the beast which I saw"), and 21:22 (naon ouk eidon — "a temple I did not see"). In each case, the intervening words transform the structural formula into a descriptive reference — the eidon points backward to an already-introduced subject rather than forward to a new scene.

    The kai eidon kai idou double formula (where eidon is followed by idou, G2400, 213 NT occurrences) marks elevated structural significance above standard kai eidon. It occurs at Rev 4:1; 5:6; 6:2; 6:5; 6:8; 7:9; 14:1; 14:14; and 19:11 — nine passages introducing the most significant new elements in each section: the opened door in heaven, the Lamb as slain, the four horsemen, the great multitude, the Lamb on Mount Zion, the Son of Man on a white cloud, and heaven opened for the rider on the white horse.

    The most structurally significant observation about kai eidon is its chronological neutrality. Two test cases establish this beyond dispute. First, at Rev 13:1 kai eidon introduces the beast from the sea, and at 13:11 kai eidon introduces the beast from the earth — but 13:12 states that the earth beast "exerciseth all the power of the first beast before him," meaning the two beasts operate simultaneously, not sequentially. The kai eidon at 13:11 introduces a contemporaneous figure, not a subsequent event. Second, after the seventh trumpet's kingdom declaration at 11:15 — an eschatological climax — Rev 12:1-5 narrates Christ's birth, a chronological regression of centuries. No meta tauta eidon or en pneumati marker appears at the boundary between 11:19 and 12:1. The narrative simply moves backward in time after a climactic formula. These two cases establish that kai eidon marks what John saw next in his visionary experience, not what happens next in historical chronology. The conjunction kai (G2532, 9101 NT occurrences) is copulative ("and"), not inherently temporal ("then"). The aorist tense of eidon describes completed action without specifying temporal relationship between events.

    The densest concentration of kai eidon in the book occurs at Rev 19:11-21:1, where seven instances appear in rapid succession: 19:11 (heaven opened, white horse), 19:17 (angel in the sun), 19:19 (beast and kings gathered), 20:1 (angel with key and chain), 20:4 (thrones and judgment), 20:11-12 (great white throne, two kai eidon in rapid succession), and 21:1 (new heaven and new earth). Whether this dense chain represents chronological progression (Second Coming -> millennium -> final judgment -> new creation) or whether some elements could describe the same events from different angles remains a disputed interpretive question that the kai eidon marker itself cannot resolve. The marker's demonstrated chronological neutrality at 13:1/13:11 and 12:1-5 means the rapid succession alone does not prove sequential chronology. At the same time, the density of the cluster — seven in twelve chapters — creates a narrative momentum that is consistent with forward progression.

    The Meta Tauta System: Macro-Level Sub-Divisions¶

    The meta tauta eidon formula ("after these things I saw/looked") uses meta (G3326, 472 NT occurrences) with the accusative plural demonstrative pronoun tauta (G5023, 243 NT occurrences). The count of meta tauta occurrences depends on how one classifies disputed instances:

    Undisputed structural meta tauta eidon occurrences (4-5): - Rev 4:1 — Meta tauta eidon kai idou — transition from Vision 1 to Vision 2 - Rev 7:9 — Meta tauta eidon kai idou — great multitude (within Vision 2) - Rev 15:5 — Meta tauta eidon — temple of tabernacle opened (within Vision 2) - Rev 18:1 — Meta tauta eidon — angel with great power (within Vision 3) - Rev 7:1 — Meta touto eidon — uses singular touto (D-ASN) instead of plural tauta (D-APN); Korner includes it, Bandy excludes it

    Disputed occurrences (4): - Rev 1:19 — Meta tauta as temporal framework ("things which shall be hereafter"), not a vision-transition formula; embedded within a clause governed by mellei genesthai - Rev 9:12 — Meta tauta as temporal adverb ("two woes more hereafter"), not a structural transition; no eidon follows - Rev 19:1 — Meta tauta ekousa ("after these things I heard") — uses ekousa (akouo G191) instead of eidon; auditory rather than visual - Rev 20:3 — Meta tauta dei luthenai ("after these things he must be loosed") — refers to a future narrative event, not a vision transition

    Korner counts five meta tauta eidon occurrences (4:1; 7:1; 7:9; 15:5; 18:1) creating six vision blocks. Bandy counts four (4:1; 7:9; 15:5; 18:1). Larsen counts nine total meta tauta including all disputed instances. The variation depends on whether one restricts the count to the strict meta tauta eidon formula or includes all meta tauta occurrences regardless of what follows.

    The grammatical variation at Rev 7:1 is structurally noteworthy. Where all other structural occurrences use the plural tauta (D-APN, "these things"), Rev 7:1 uses the singular touto (D-ASN, "this thing"). The plural references a series of preceding events; the singular may reference a single preceding event (the seal sequence just concluded). Whether this variant qualifies as the same structural formula is disputed but the distinction is observable in the Greek text.

    The key structural observation about meta tauta is that it creates sub-divisions that the en pneumati system does not mark. At Rev 7:1, 7:9, 15:5, 18:1, and (if counted) 19:1, meta tauta creates internal boundaries within what en pneumati treats as a single vision. Rev 7:1 and 7:9 fall within Vision 2 (4:2-16:21). Rev 15:5 also falls within Vision 2. Rev 18:1 and 19:1 fall within Vision 3 (17:3-21:8). This means en pneumati and meta tauta operate at different structural levels — en pneumati at the macro level (four major visions), meta tauta at an intermediate level (sub-divisions within visions). The two systems do not compete; they complement each other.

    The one point where both systems converge is Rev 4:1-2, where meta tauta eidon (4:1) and en pneumati (4:2) co-occur within two verses. This is the strongest structural boundary in the entire book — every scholar, regardless of model, recognizes the transition from Vision 1 to Vision 2 here. The convergence of both markers at a single boundary, unique in the book, underscores its structural weight.

    The Sinai-Theophany Formula: A Fourth Structural Marker System¶

    The Sinai-theophany formula consists of a cluster of vocabulary drawn from the Sinai theophany of Exo 19:16-18: astrapai (G796, "lightnings," 9 NT occurrences), brontai (G1027, "thunderings," 12 NT occurrences), phonai (G5456, "voices," 141 NT occurrences), seismos (G4578, "earthquake," 14 NT occurrences), and chalaza (G5464, "hail," 4 NT occurrences). This cluster appears at exactly four points in Revelation with progressive intensification:

    Passage Elements Count Added Element
    Rev 4:5 lightnings, voices, thunderings 3 (baseline)
    Rev 8:5 voices, thunderings, lightnings, earthquake 4 +earthquake
    Rev 11:19 lightnings, voices, thunderings, earthquake, great hail 5 +hail
    Rev 16:18-21 voices, thunders, lightnings, great earthquake, great hail (with superlatives) 5+ +superlatives

    The progression is measurable: three elements at the throne room introduction (4:5), four at the transition between seals/censer scene and trumpets (8:5), five at the seventh trumpet climax where the ark of the covenant is revealed (11:19), and five-plus with superlatives at the seventh bowl climax (16:18-21). The superlatives at 16:18 are emphatic: "such as was not since men were upon the earth, so mighty an earthquake, and so great."

    This formula operates independently of the three primary marker systems. None of the four theophany occurrences coincides with an en pneumati marker. Only one (8:5) falls near a potential meta tauta boundary (meta tauta at 7:1 and 7:9 bracket it). The theophany formula marks transitions that the primary markers do not signal — particularly at Rev 11:19, where the five-element formula and the visible ark (sanctuary progression reaching the Most Holy Place) create a major structural pivot that neither en pneumati nor meta tauta marks. This divergence is a key finding: relying on any single marker system produces an incomplete structural picture.

    The Sinai source text at Exo 19:16-18 provides thunders, lightnings, voice of trumpet, thick cloud, smoke, fire, and earthquake. Revelation's formula draws on this vocabulary but adds hail (chalaza) as a fifth element not present in the Exodus account. The allusion is classified as Strong: three or more shared vocabulary items (thunders, lightnings, earthquake) plus shared theophanic context.

    Additional Marker Systems¶

    The semeion ophthe markers constitute a minor but distinct system. At Rev 12:1 and 12:3, the text uses ophthe (V-API-3S, aorist passive of horao) — "a great sign appeared/was seen" — rather than the active "I saw." The passive voice shifts agency: the sign presents itself rather than John actively seeing it. At 15:1, the formula becomes hybrid: kai eidon allo semeion ("and I saw another sign"), combining active eidon with the semeion vocabulary. The three sign markers cluster at the structural center of Revelation (chs. 12-15), and the word semeion (G4592, 77 NT occurrences) connects to the book's opening: "he sent and signified it" (1:1, using semaino G4591, the verbal form of the same root).

    The Prologue-Epilogue inclusio (Rev 1:1-8 and 22:6-21) shares six or more distinctive verbal elements: angel sent to show servants (1:1/22:6, 16), words of this prophecy (1:3/22:7, 10, 18), blessed pronouncement (1:3/22:7, 14), Alpha and Omega (1:8/22:13), "I come quickly" (1:7/22:7, 12, 20), seven churches (1:4, 11/22:16), testimony of Jesus (1:2/22:16, 20). This Strong inclusio frames the entire vision report (1:9-22:5) as a single literary unit. This framing means that the en pneumati at 1:10 inaugurates a visionary experience that continues through 22:5, with the subsequent en pneumati markers (4:2, 17:3, 21:10) marking transitions within a single visionary episode rather than separate visions from separate occasions.

    The progressive intensity fractions (1/4 at seals, Rev 6:8; 1/3 at trumpets, Rev 8:7-12; total at bowls, Rev 16:3-4) create a measurable escalation pattern. The domain correspondence between trumpets and bowls — earth, sea, rivers, sun, in identical sequential order — is a structural observation verifiable from the text. This escalation is consistent with both progressive sequence (each judgment series is more intense than the last) and telescoping (each series is nested within the final element of the previous one).

    Convergence and Divergence: Where Marker Systems Agree and Disagree¶

    The marker systems converge at their strongest when they co-occur at the same structural boundary. Rev 4:1-2 is the only passage where both meta tauta eidon and en pneumati appear together, making it the most unambiguous structural boundary in the book. The trumpet voice (salpinx G4536) at both 1:10 and 4:1 provides an additional auditory link between Visions 1 and 2.

    The marker systems diverge at several critical points:

    Rev 11:19 — The Sinai-theophany formula (5 elements) and the sanctuary progression (ark visible = Most Holy Place opened) both place a major transition here. But neither en pneumati nor meta tauta marks this as a division. The en pneumati model treats 11:19 as interior to Vision 2 (4:2-16:21). Paulien's sanctuary-introduction model and Strand's eight-section model both treat 11:19 as a primary boundary. This divergence shows that different structural systems can legitimately produce different boundary signals — they operate at different levels of the text's architecture.

    Rev 15:5 — Meta tauta eidon marks a boundary here (temple of tabernacle opened), but the en pneumati system treats this as mid-Vision 2. Korner's six-block model makes 15:5 a primary division point. The four-vision model does not. Both readings are consistent with the textual evidence because the two marker systems operate at different structural levels.

    Rev 21:1-8 — The en pneumati model places the Vision 3/4 boundary at 21:10, but the new creation theme begins at 21:1 and the New Jerusalem appears at 21:2 — before the fourth en pneumati marker. This transition zone resists precise boundary placement by any single marker. Some scholars place the boundary at 21:1; others at 21:9; others at 21:10. The structural markers create a zone of transition rather than a sharp line.

    Rev 7:1 and 7:9 — Two meta tauta occurrences within a single chapter (7:1 meta touto, singular; 7:9 meta tauta, plural) create sub-divisions within Vision 2 that the en pneumati system does not mark. These function as internal section breaks within the seal narrative, separating the seal sequence (ch. 6) from the sealing interlude (7:1-8) and the great multitude vision (7:9-17).

    Rev 18:1 and 19:1 — Two meta tauta occurrences within Vision 3 (17:3-21:8) create internal divisions that the en pneumati system does not signal. The meta tauta at 18:1 separates the Babylon description (ch. 17) from the Babylon judgment (ch. 18), and the meta tauta at 19:1 (using ekousa rather than eidon) separates the judgment from the celebration.

    Competing Structural Models and What the Markers Support¶

    The four-vision model (Bauckham, Hicks, Bandy) privileges en pneumati as the primary organizer: four divisions at 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, and 21:10. This model must explain why meta tauta creates sub-divisions within Vision 2 (at 7:1, 7:9, 15:5) that other models treat as primary divisions. The model is supported by the uniqueness of the en pneumati formula and its pairing with distinct locations.

    The six-block model (Korner) privileges meta tauta eidon: five occurrences creating six blocks. This model must explain why en pneumati creates only four divisions that do not align with the six blocks. The model is supported by the structural-transition function of meta tauta eidon.

    The eight-vision model (Strand) privileges victorious-introduction scenes and chiastic pairing, creating a historical/eschatological divide at chs. 14-15. The en pneumati markers do not support this division point — no en pneumati marker falls between chs. 14 and 15. The model is supported by the chiastic verbal parallels between Revelation's sections (e.g., churches/New Jerusalem: tree of life at 2:7/22:2, book of life at 3:5/21:27, New Jerusalem at 3:12/21:10).

    The seven-section model (Paulien) privileges sanctuary introductions: lampstands (1:12-20), throne room (4:1-5:14), altar of incense (8:2-6), heavenly temple (15:5-8). This model must explain why en pneumati does not mark the sanctuary transitions. The model is supported by verifiable sanctuary vocabulary at each proposed introduction scene.

    Ian Paul's methodological observation is directly applicable: "Looking for one 'structure' cannot be done... If you consider literary markers, there is one structure. If you consider theological themes, there is another." Bandy's three-layer model — surface structure (discourse markers like en pneumati and kai eidon), intertextual layer (OT patterns, especially Ezekiel), and intratextual layer (repeated phrases linking distant passages in Revelation) — offers a framework for understanding why different markers point to different boundaries: they operate at different structural levels. The en pneumati markers are surface-structure phenomena. The Ezekiel Spirit-transport parallels are intertextual. The "word of God and testimony" chain (1:2, 1:9, 6:9, 12:11, 12:17, 20:4) is intratextual. All three contribute to Revelation's structural architecture, but they do not all mark the same boundaries because they operate at different levels of the text.

    Word Studies¶

    pneuma (G4151) — 385 NT occurrences. In the en pneumati structural formula, pneuma is anarthrous dative singular neuter, functioning as the domain/means of visionary experience. In the "Spirit saith to the churches" formula (2:7-3:22), it is articular nominative (to Pneuma), functioning as the personal Holy Spirit who speaks. The grammatical distinction separates two structural functions of the same word.

    horao (G3708) — 415 NT occurrences. The 2nd aorist active indicative 1st person singular form eidon is the key structural marker form in Revelation. The suppletive aorist stem (eid-) replaces the present stem (hora-). Only this specific morphological form functions as a technical structural marker.

    ginomai (G1096) — 702 NT occurrences. Verb in the Visions 1-2 en pneumati formula: egenomen en Pneumati (aorist middle indicative 1st singular). John "became in Spirit." The shift from ginomai to apophero at Vision 3 creates the 2+2 structural pairing.

    apophero (G667) — 7 NT occurrences. Verb in the Visions 3-4 en pneumati formula: apenengken me en Pneumati (aorist active indicative 3rd singular). The apophero + en Pneumati combination is unique to Rev 17:3 and 21:10 in the entire NT.

    meta (G3326) — 472 NT occurrences. Preposition in the meta tauta formula, governing the accusative case to indicate temporal succession. Highest per-verse density in Revelation (49 occurrences, 12.1 per 100 verses).

    kai (G2532) — 9101 NT occurrences. In kai eidon, kai functions as a copulative conjunction linking scenes, not as a temporal adverb. The chronological neutrality of kai eidon is confirmed by Rev 13:11 (contemporaneous beast) and Rev 12:1-5 (chronological regression).

    semeion (G4592) — 77 NT occurrences. Sign-marker vocabulary at 12:1, 12:3, 15:1. The passive ophthe at 12:1 and 12:3 shifts agency from seer to sign. The root connects to semaino (G4591) at 1:1 ("signified").

    astrape (G796) — 9 NT occurrences, 4 in Revelation. Constant element in all four Sinai-theophany formula occurrences (4:5, 8:5, 11:19, 16:18).

    seismos (G4578) — 14 NT occurrences. Escalation element in the Sinai-theophany formula: absent at 4:5, present at 8:5, 11:19, 16:18. Demonstrates progressive intensification.

    chalaza (G5464) — 4 NT occurrences. Final escalation element: absent at 4:5 and 8:5, present at 11:19 and 16:21. Added to the formula only at its two final occurrences.

    Difficult Passages¶

    Rev 11:19 — A major transition without a primary marker¶

    The Sinai-theophany formula (five elements) and the sanctuary progression (ark visible = Most Holy Place opened) both place a major transition at Rev 11:19. But neither en pneumati nor meta tauta marks this boundary. The en pneumati model treats it as interior to Vision 2. Paulien's model and Strand's model treat it as a primary boundary. This passage illustrates that relying on any single marker system produces an incomplete structural picture. The divergence is not a defect in the text but a reflection of multiple structural systems operating at different levels.

    Rev 21:1-8 — Ambiguous transition zone¶

    The en pneumati model places the Vision 3/4 boundary at 21:10, but the new creation theme begins at 21:1 and New Jerusalem appears at 21:2 — before the fourth en pneumati marker. Rev 21:2 uses the distinctive formula kai ... eidon with "I John" (ego Ioannes) intervening, a construction found only here and at 1:9. The result is a transition zone rather than a sharp boundary. The structural markers do not resolve whether 21:1-8 belongs to Vision 3 or constitutes a bridge passage.

    Rev 19:11-21:1 — Dense kai eidon chain and the millennium question¶

    The seven kai eidon statements in rapid succession create the densest cluster in the book. Whether Rev 20:1-6 (millennium) follows 19:21 (destruction of the beast and false prophet) in strict chronological sequence or recapitulates earlier material is the single most debated structural question in Revelation scholarship. The kai eidon marker itself cannot resolve this, since its chronological neutrality is established elsewhere (13:1/13:11, 12:1-5). The competing claims (IC005 for linear sequence, IC006 for recapitulation) both have supporting structural evidence and the markers do not adjudicate between them.

    Rev 7:1 — Singular touto vs. plural tauta¶

    Rev 7:1 uses meta touto (singular, D-ASN) while all other structural meta tauta occurrences use the plural (D-APN). Whether this variant counts as the same structural formula is disputed. Korner includes it among five meta tauta eidon occurrences; Bandy excludes it, counting only four. The grammatical distinction is verifiable but its structural significance is interpretive.

    Rev 19:1 — Meta tauta with "heard" instead of "saw"¶

    Rev 19:1 uses meta tauta ekousa ("after these things I heard") rather than meta tauta eidon ("I saw"). Whether the auditory variant functions identically to the visual formula as a structural-transition marker is unsettled. The shift from seeing to hearing may indicate a different type of transition or it may be a stylistic variation without structural significance.

    Conclusion¶

    The structural analysis catalogs the following markers in Revelation:

    En pneumati (4 occurrences): Rev 1:10, 4:2, 17:3, 21:10. Verb shift: ginomai (Visions 1-2) to apophero (Visions 3-4). Location pairing: Patmos, heaven, wilderness, mountain. Validation: Strong — all four are verifiable, the verb shift is grammatically observable, the location pairing is textually explicit.

    Kai eidon (approximately 35 structural occurrences): The most frequent structural device. Five intervening-word exceptions provide concurrent description rather than new structural transitions (1:17, 9:17, 10:5, 13:2, 21:22). Nine kai eidon kai idou double-formula occurrences mark elevated structural significance. Dense seven-member cluster at 19:11-21:1. Chronological neutrality established by 13:1/13:11 and 12:1-5. Validation: Strong for the formula's existence and distribution; Neutral on chronological implications.

    Meta tauta eidon (5 undisputed, up to 9 total): Rev 4:1, 7:1 (singular variant), 7:9, 15:5, 18:1. Four additional disputed occurrences at 1:19, 9:12, 19:1, 20:3. Creates sub-divisions within the en pneumati macro-structure. Operates at an intermediate structural level between en pneumati (macro) and kai eidon (micro). Validation: Strong for the undisputed occurrences; Moderate for the disputed ones.

    Sinai-theophany formula (4 occurrences): Rev 4:5 (3 elements), 8:5 (4 elements), 11:19 (5 elements), 16:18-21 (5+ with superlatives). Progressive intensification is measurable and verifiable. Operates independently of the three primary markers. Marks transitions that no other system signals. Validation: Strong — the element counts and escalation pattern are objectively verifiable.

    Additional systems: Semeion ophthe (3 occurrences: 12:1, 12:3, 15:1), Prologue-Epilogue inclusio (6+ shared elements, Strong validation), progressive intensity fractions (1/4 -> 1/3 -> total), sanctuary vocabulary progression (lampstands -> altar -> ark -> no temple), "Spirit saith to the churches" formula (7 occurrences, confined to Vision 1).

    The competing structural models (four-vision, six-block, eight-vision, seven-section) each privilege different marker systems and produce different structural outlines. The textual markers themselves do not resolve which model is correct, because the models operate at different structural levels. Bandy's three-layer framework (surface, intertextual, intratextual) offers the most productive approach: the different markers do not compete at the same level — they complement each other across multiple layers of Revelation's literary architecture. The en pneumati markers provide the surface-level macro-structure. The Ezekiel parallels provide the intertextual framework. The verbal chains ("word of God and testimony," fraction escalation, Sinai-theophany formula) provide intratextual connections that span the entire book.

    The chronological question — whether Revelation's sequences are parallel (recapitulation), sequential (linear), or nested (telescoping) — is supported by structural observations on multiple sides. The identical elthen he orge formula at Rev 6:17 and 11:18, the mountains and islands at 6:14 and 16:20, and the chronological regression at 12:1-5 are consistent with overlapping time coverage. The fraction escalation (1/4 -> 1/3 -> total), the 7th seal/trumpet telescoping transitions (8:1-2, 11:15-19), and the dense kai eidon chain at 19:11-21:1 are consistent with progressive sequence or nesting. These observations are not mutually exclusive — recapitulation and telescoping can coexist within the same structural architecture. The structural markers catalog what the text contains; the chronological model that emerges from this evidence is the subject of subsequent studies.
