    Verses¶

    Question¶

    How do the Babylon/harlot (Rev 17-18) and New Jerusalem/bride (Rev 21-22) sections form a deliberate structural mirror? Document every verbal parallel between Rev 17:1-3 and Rev 21:9-10 (the angel introduction formula). Trace the antithetical contrasts: harlot vs. bride, wilderness vs. mountain, jewels adorning each, what fills each (abominations vs. glory), the fate of each. How does this structural mirror connect to the en pneumati markers (17:3 and 21:10 are visions 3 and 4)?

    Angel introduction formula — vision 3: harlot shown in wilderness¶

    And there came one of the seven angels which had the seven vials, and talked with me, saying unto me, Come hither; I will shew unto thee the judgment of the great whore that sitteth upon many waters: With whom the kings of the earth have committed fornication, and the inhabitants of the earth have been made drunk with the wine of her fornication. So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast, full of names of blasphemy, having seven heads and ten horns.

    Context: One of the seven bowl angels approaches John to show him the judgment of the great harlot. This is the third en Pneumati marker (17:3), using apophero (carry away) instead of ginomai (become). John is transported to a wilderness setting where he sees the woman-beast vision.

    Notes: Near-verbatim parallel opening with Rev 21:9-10. Shared elements: kai elthen heis ek ton hepta angelon ton echonton tas hepta phialas, kai elalesen met emou legon Deuro, deixo soi. Divergent elements: 17:1 has to krima tes pornes tes megales (judgment of the great harlot); 21:9 has ten nymphen ten gynaika tou arniou (the bride, the Lamb's wife). 17:3 has eis eremon (into wilderness); 21:10 has epi oros mega kai hypselon (to great high mountain). Both use apenengken me en Pneumati. The grammar reference at rev-17-1-6.md documents all parsing details.

    Angel introduction formula — vision 4: bride shown on high mountain¶

    And there came unto me one of the seven angels which had the seven vials full of the seven last plagues, and talked with me, saying, Come hither, I will shew thee the bride, the Lamb's wife. And he carried me away in the spirit to a great and high mountain, and shewed me that great city, the holy Jerusalem, descending out of heaven from God,

    Context: The same type of bowl angel approaches John (the fourth en Pneumati marker, 21:10) to show him the bride-city. 21:9 adds ton gemonton ton hepta plegon ton eschaton (full of the seven last plagues) — this addition explicitly links the showing-angel to the bowl sequence. The mountain location echoes Ezek 40:2.

    Notes: The verbal parallel with 17:1-3 is extraordinary. Word-by-word shared elements: kai elthen heis ek ton hepta angelon ton echonton tas hepta phialas / kai elalesen met emou legon / Deuro deixo soi / apenengken me en Pneumati. 21:9 adds the plague-bowl description (ton gemonton ton hepta plegon ton eschaton). The object of showing shifts from krima (judgment) to nymphen (bride). The destination shifts from eremon (wilderness) to oros mega kai hypselon (great high mountain). 21:10 adds edeixen moi (showed me) — using deiknymi which was promised in the formula.

    Harlot's external adornment and golden cup of abominations¶

    And the woman was arrayed in purple and scarlet colour, and decked with gold and precious stones and pearls, having a golden cup in her hand full of abominations and filthiness of her fornication:

    Context: The harlot's appearance: three perfect passive participles (peribeblēmenē, kechryōsmenē, gegrammmenon in v.5) describe her settled state. The adornment vocabulary (purple, scarlet, gold, precious stones, pearls) matches the high priest's garments in Exod 28:5-6 except for the absence of blue (tekeleth/hyakinthos). The verb chrysoo (G5558, 'to gild') appears only here and 18:16 in the NT — external plating, not intrinsic composition.

    Notes: The grammar reference at rev-17-1-6.md documents the perfect passive participle stack (peribeblēmenē, kechryōmenē) and the genitive chain for cup contents (gemon bdelygmatōn kai ta akatharta tēs porneias autēs). Key contrast with NJ: the harlot is 'gilded' (external decoration) while NJ IS pure gold (intrinsic composition, 21:18). The cup (potērion chrysoun) alludes to Jer 51:7 (Babylon a golden cup in the LORD's hand).

    Forehead inscription: MYSTERY BABYLON THE GREAT¶

    And upon her forehead [was] a name written, MYSTERY, BABYLON THE GREAT, THE MOTHER OF HARLOTS AND ABOMINATIONS OF THE EARTH.

    Context: The forehead inscription on the harlot. The anarthrous mystērion (G3466) functions either as part of the name or as an adverbial gloss ('symbolically') — the grammar reference documents this ambiguity. MĒTĒR TŌN PORNŌN (mother of harlots) indicates reproductive capacity — generating daughter-harlots.

    Notes: Direct contrast with Rev 22:4 where God's name (onoma autou) is on the foreheads of the redeemed. The 3am-10 study documented that this inverts the high priest's gold plate: HOLINESS TO THE LORD (Exod 28:36) becomes MYSTERY BABYLON THE GREAT MOTHER OF HARLOTS.

    Harlot drunk with blood of saints¶

    And I saw the woman drunken with the blood of the saints, and with the blood of the martyrs of Jesus: and when I saw her, I wondered with great admiration.

    Context: The harlot's defining characteristic: methyousan ek tou haimatos (being drunk from blood). The present participle depicts ongoing, habitual drunkenness. This is the antithesis of NJ's river of life (22:1).

    Notes: Blood of saints and blood of martyrs are two groups or two descriptions of the same group. This verse sets up the blood-guilt climax at 18:24.

    Angel interprets the waters: peoples, multitudes, nations, tongues¶

    And he saith unto me, The waters which thou sawest, where the whore sitteth, are peoples, and multitudes, and nations, and tongues.

    Context: The angel decodes the 'many waters' of 17:1. The fourfold list (laoi, ochloi, ethnē, glōssai) indicates universal scope. Contrast with NJ's single divine river (22:1).

    Notes: The waters of Babylon are peoples under her control; the water of NJ is the river of life proceeding from God's throne. The harlot sits ON the waters (domination); the river of NJ flows FROM the throne (provision).

    Fate of harlot: burned with fire by ten horns¶

    And the ten horns which thou sawest upon the beast, these shall hate the whore, and shall make her desolate and naked, and shall eat her flesh, and burn her with fire. For God hath put in their hearts to fulfil his will, and to agree, and give their kingdom unto the beast, until the words of God shall be fulfilled.

    Context: The harlot's destruction by her own allies — the ten horns that supported her now destroy her. The fourfold destruction (desolate, naked, eat flesh, burn with fire) represents total annihilation.

    Notes: Fire judgment of Babylon contrasts with divine glory illuminating NJ (21:23). God's sovereignty controls even this destruction (17:17 — God put it in their hearts).

    Woman identified as the great city — city-woman identification principle¶

    And the woman which thou sawest is that great city, which reigneth over the kings of the earth.

    Context: Explicit identification: the woman IS the city. This city-woman identification principle applies symmetrically to the bride (21:2, 9-10) — the bride IS the New Jerusalem. hē polis hē megalē (the great city) contrasts with hē polis hē hagia (the holy city) of 21:2.

    Notes: The structural reciprocity principle: if the harlot = the great city, and the bride = the holy city, then the text uses the same symbolic logic for both. This is the foundation of the Guinness observation.

    Babylon becomes habitation of devils — antithesis to NJ as tabernacle of God¶

    And he cried mightily with a strong voice, saying, Babylon the great is fallen, is fallen, and is become the habitation of devils, and the hold of every foul spirit, and a cage of every unclean and hateful bird.

    Context: The doubled epesen (pipto, G4098, aorist active) quotes Isa 21:9 LXX. Babylon 'has become' (egeneto, aorist of ginomai) the katoikētērion daimonion (habitation of demons). Triple desolation vocabulary: katoikētērion (habitation), phylakē (hold/prison), phylakē (cage).

    Notes: Direct antithesis to 21:3 — the skēnē tou theou meta tōn anthrōpōn (tabernacle of God with men). Demons inhabit one city; God inhabits the other. The egeneto signals progressive deterioration — Babylon was not always demon-haunted.

    Come out of her my people — separation call¶

    And I heard another voice from heaven, saying, Come out of her, my people, that ye be not partakers of her sins, and that ye receive not of her plagues.

    Context: The pastoral center of the Babylon oracle. exelthate ex autēs ho laos mou — imperative to flee. Direct allusion to Jer 51:6 (Flee out of the midst of Babylon) and Jer 51:45 (My people, go ye out of the midst of her).

    Notes: Antithesis to Rev 22:17 where the Spirit and bride say 'Come' (erchou) — an invitation to enter. 18:4 commands departure from the condemned city; 22:17 invites entrance to the life-giving city.

    Babylon's self-glorification and sudden plagues¶

    How much she hath glorified herself, and lived deliciously, so much torment and sorrow give her: for she saith in her heart, I sit a queen, and am no widow, and shall see no sorrow. Therefore shall her plagues come in one day, death, and mourning, and famine; and she shall be utterly burned with fire: for strong [is] the Lord God who judgeth her.

    Context: Babylon claims queenly status (basilissa kathēmai) — self-glorification. Plagues come 'in one day' (en mia hēmera) — sudden, total judgment.

    Notes: The self-glorification contrasts with the bride who receives her adornment from God (19:8 — edothē autē, divine passive 'it was given to her'). Babylon adorns herself; the bride is adorned by God.

    Cargo list ending with bodies and souls of men¶

    And the merchants of the earth shall weep and mourn over her; for no man buyeth their merchandise any more: The merchandise of gold, and silver, and precious stones, and of pearls, and fine linen, and purple, and silk, and scarlet, and all thyine wood, and all manner vessels of ivory, and all manner vessels of most precious wood, and of brass, and iron, and marble, And cinnamon, and odours, and ointments, and frankincense, and wine, and oil, and fine flour, and wheat, and beasts, and sheep, and horses, and chariots, and slaves, and souls of men.

    Context: The 28-item merchandise catalog descends from precious metals to human beings (sōmatōn kai psychas anthrōpōn — bodies and souls of men). Alludes to Ezekiel 27:12-22 (Tyre's trade catalog). NJ's contrast: free access to the water of life (21:6, 22:17).

    Notes: The cargo list shares vocabulary with the harlot's adornment (gold, precious stones, pearls, fine linen, purple, scarlet) — Babylon's wealth is her adornment.

    Sevenfold 'no more' — life-elements removed from Babylon¶

    And a mighty angel took up a stone like a great millstone, and cast [it] into the sea, saying, Thus with violence shall that great city Babylon be thrown down, and shall be found no more at all. And the voice of harpers, and musicians, and of pipers, and trumpeters, shall be heard no more at all in thee; and no craftsman, of whatsoever craft [he be], shall be found any more in thee; and the sound of a millstone shall be heard no more at all in thee; And the light of a candle shall shine no more at all in thee; and the voice of the bridegroom and of the bride shall be heard no more at all in thee: for thy merchants were the great men of the earth; for by thy sorceries were all nations deceived.

    Context: The sevenfold 'no more' (ou mē ... eti) construction: (1) Babylon not found, (2) voice of musicians, (3) craftsmen, (4) millstone sound, (5) lamplight, (6) bridegroom voice, (7) bride voice. The double negative ou mē + aorist subjunctive is the strongest Greek negation — emphatic permanent denial.

    Notes: These are LIFE-elements removed from Babylon. In NJ, SUFFERING-elements are removed (21:4 — death, sorrow, crying, pain; 22:3 — curse; 22:5 — night). The lamp contrast: 'light of a candle shall shine (phanē, G5316) no more at all in thee' (18:23) vs. 'the Lamb is the light thereof' (21:23) and 'they need no candle' (22:5). The bride/bridegroom voice silenced in 18:23 (nymphē, G3565) reappears in 22:17 (Spirit and bride say Come).

    Blood of prophets and saints found in Babylon¶

    And in her was found the blood of prophets, and of saints, and of all that were slain upon the earth.

    Context: The blood-guilt climax of the Babylon section. Echoes 17:6 (drunk with blood). The passive heurethē (was found) implies judicial discovery — forensic language.

    Notes: This connects to the altar vindication arc — the martyrs' cry of Rev 6:10 is answered here.

    Four hallelujahs responding to Babylon's fall¶

    And after these things I heard a great voice of much people in heaven, saying, Alleluia; Salvation, and glory, and honour, and power, unto the Lord our God: For true and righteous [are] his judgments: for he hath judged the great whore, which did corrupt the earth with her fornication, and hath avenged the blood of his servants at her hand. And again they said, Alleluia. And her smoke rose up for ever and ever. And the four and twenty elders and the four beasts fell down and worshipped God that sat on the throne, saying, Amen; Alleluia. And a voice came out of the throne, saying, Praise our God, all ye his servants, and ye that fear him, both small and great. And I heard as it were the voice of a great multitude, and as the voice of many waters, and as the voice of mighty thunderings, saying, Alleluia: for the Lord God omnipotent reigneth.

    Context: All four NT occurrences of allēlouia (G239) concentrated here — the heavenly response to Babylon's fall. This passage forms the structural conclusion of the Babylon section before the transition to the marriage supper.

    Notes: The four hallelujahs conclude the Babylon section. The marriage supper announcement immediately follows (19:7-9), linking the fall of the harlot to the presentation of the bride.

    Marriage of the Lamb — bride made herself ready¶

    Let us be glad and rejoice, and give honour to him: for the marriage of the Lamb is come, and his wife hath made herself ready. And to her was granted that she should be arrayed in fine linen, clean and white: for the fine linen is the righteousness of saints. And he saith unto me, Write, Blessed [are] they which are called unto the marriage supper of the Lamb. And he saith unto me, These are the true sayings of God.

    Context: The marriage announcement. The bride (gynē, wife) has prepared herself (hētoimasen heautēn), yet her garment is 'granted' (edothē, divine passive) — both human preparation and divine provision. The fine linen (byssinon, G1039) is explicitly decoded: ta dikaiōmata tōn hagiōn (the righteous acts of the saints).

    Notes: Contrast with harlot's garments (17:4): harlot wears purple, scarlet, gold (priestly garments minus blue); bride wears fine linen, clean and white. The harlot's adornment is self-acquired luxury; the bride's is divinely granted righteousness. The Bohr/EGW distinction: bride-as-city (19:7 gynē) vs. called-to-supper guests (19:9 keklēmenoi) may indicate separate entities.

    Angel-worship refusal — first occurrence of VP022 bracket¶

    And I fell at his feet to worship him. And he said unto me, See [thou do it] not: I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren that have the testimony of Jesus: worship God: for the testimony of Jesus is the spirit of prophecy.

    Context: First angel-worship refusal, closing the Babylon section. John falls (epesa, pipto G4098) to worship (proskunēsai, G4352). Angel responds: Hora mē (See [thou do it] not), syndoulos sou eimi (I am thy fellowservant), tō Theō proskynēson (worship God). This pairs with 22:8-9 as VP022.

    Notes: The shared vocabulary with 22:8-9: epesa proskunēsai, emprosthen tōn podōn, hora mē, syndoulos sou eimi, tōn adelphōn sou, tō Theō proskynēson. Differences: 19:10 has 'the testimony of Jesus' clause; 22:9 has 'the prophets and them which keep the sayings of this book.'

    Angel-worship refusal — second occurrence of VP022 bracket¶

    And he said unto me, These sayings [are] faithful and true: and the Lord God of the holy prophets sent his angel to shew unto his servants the things which must shortly be done. Behold, I come quickly: blessed [is] he that keepeth the sayings of the prophecy of this book. And I John saw these things, and heard [them]. And when I had heard and seen, I fell down to worship before the feet of the angel which shewed me these things. Then saith he unto me, See [thou do it] not: for I am thy fellowservant, and of thy brethren the prophets, and of them which keep the sayings of this book: worship God.

    Context: Second angel-worship refusal, closing the NJ section. Near-identical vocabulary to 19:10. The double-bracket (VP022, SP012) frames both city-woman visions with the same worship-God command.

    Notes: 22:8 adds deiknuontos moi tauta (the one showing me these things) — using deiknymi, the same verb from the introduction formula (17:1 deixō soi; 21:9 deixō soi). This creates a showing-verb inclusio within each section. 22:6 echoes the prologue (1:1) with 'sent his angel to shew unto his servants' — connecting to the prologue-epilogue inclusio.

    Spirit and bride say Come — invitation to enter¶

    And the Spirit and the bride say, Come. And let him that heareth say, Come. And let him that is athirst come. And whosoever will, let him take the water of life freely.

    Context: The bride's voice restored. The nymphē who was silenced in Babylon (18:23 'voice of the bride shall be heard no more') here speaks the climactic invitation. The threefold 'Come' (erchou, G2064) inverts 18:4's 'Come out' (exelthate).

    Notes: The Proverbs two-women topos: Lady Wisdom invites guests to her feast (Pro 9:1-6 'Come, eat of my bread'); the foolish woman's guests are in Sheol (Pro 9:18). The bride's 'Come' echoes Wisdom's invitation. The water of life freely (dorean, G1432) contrasts with Babylon's commerce in human souls (18:13).

    New Jerusalem descending as bride adorned for husband¶

    And I John saw the holy city, new Jerusalem, coming down from God out of heaven, prepared as a bride adorned for her husband.

    Context: First identification of NJ as bride. The city-bride identification: polin hagian Ierousalēm kainēn katabainousan... hōs nymphēn kekosmēmenēn tō andri autēs. The perfect passive participle kekosmēmenēn (having been adorned) parallels the harlot's perfect passives (peribeblēmenē, kechryōmenē) but with divine agency implied.

    Notes: NJ descends 'from God out of heaven' (apo tou theou ek tou ouranou) — divine origin contrasting with Babylon's terrestrial source (from the beast/dragon, 17:3). Zerbe's observation: the source of wealth/beauty distinguishes the two cities.

    Tabernacle of God with men — no more death, sorrow, crying, pain¶

    And I heard a great voice out of heaven saying, Behold, the tabernacle of God [is] with men, and he will dwell with them, and they shall be his people, and God himself shall be with them, [and be] their God. And God shall wipe away all tears from their eyes; and there shall be no more death, neither sorrow, nor crying, neither shall there be any more pain: for the former things are passed away.

    Context: The tabernacle declaration (hē skēnē tou theou meta tōn anthrōpōn, G4633) fulfills Ezek 37:27 and Lev 26:11-12. God will skenōsei (G4637, dwell/tabernacle) with them. The fourfold removal of suffering-elements (death, sorrow, crying, pain) inverts Babylon's sevenfold removal of life-elements.

    Notes: Direct antithesis to 18:2 (katoikētērion daimonion, habitation of demons). skēnē (tabernacle) vs. katoikētērion (habitation); theou (of God) vs. daimonion (of demons). The covenant formula 'they shall be his people, and God himself shall be with them' echoes Lev 26:12, Jer 31:33, Ezek 37:27.

    NJ has the glory of God — light like jasper, clear as crystal¶

    Having the glory of God: and her light [was] like unto a stone most precious, even like a jasper stone, clear as crystal;

    Context: The NJ's defining characteristic: echousan tēn doxan tou theou (G1391, having the glory of God). The jasper (iaspis, G2393) connects NJ to the throne vision (4:3 — he who sat was like jasper). The phōstēr (luminary/light-bearer) is likened to jasper krystallizzonti (crystallizing/clear as crystal).

    Notes: Antithesis to the harlot's counterfeit adornment (17:4). The harlot is gilded with gold and adorned with precious stones externally; NJ HAS the glory of God intrinsically. Jasper (G2393, 4 occurrences — all in Revelation: 4:3, 21:11, 21:18, 21:19) links the city to the divine throne.

    NJ composed of precious stones — wall of jasper, city of pure gold, 12 foundations, pearl gates¶

    And the building of the wall of it was [of] jasper: and the city [was] pure gold, like unto clear glass. And the foundations of the wall of the city [were] garnished with all manner of precious stones. The first foundation [was] jasper; the second, sapphire; the third, a chalcedony; the fourth, an emerald; The fifth, sardonyx; the sixth, sardius; the seventh, chrysolite; the eighth, beryl; the ninth, a topaz; the tenth, a chrysoprasus; the eleventh, a jacinth; the twelfth, an amethyst. And the twelve gates [were] twelve pearls; every several gate was of one pearl: and the street of the city [was] pure gold, as it were transparent glass.

    Context: NJ's composition: wall IS jasper (21:18), city IS pure gold (21:18), 12 foundations ARE precious stones (21:19-20), 12 gates ARE pearls (21:21), street IS pure gold (21:21). The city IS these materials — intrinsic composition, not external decoration.

    Notes: Key contrast with harlot: kechryōmenē chrysiō (gilded with gold, 17:4 — external plating via chrysoo G5558) vs. chrysion katharon (pure gold, 21:18 — solid composition). The 12 foundation stones overlap with but do not match the 12 breastplate stones of Exod 28:17-20 — some correspondence, some variation. Ezek 28:13 lists 9 of the same stones for the covering cherub. Isa 54:11-12 describes Jerusalem rebuilt with sapphires, agates, carbuncles.

    No temple — God and Lamb are temple and light¶

    And I saw no temple therein: for the Lord God Almighty and the Lamb are the temple of it. And the city had no need of the sun, neither of the moon, to shine in it: for the glory of God did lighten it, and the Lamb [is] the light thereof.

    Context: No naos (temple) in NJ because God and the Lamb ARE the temple. No sun or moon needed because the doxa tou theou (glory of God) illuminates and the arnion (Lamb) is the lychnos (lamp). This fulfills Isa 60:19-20.

    Notes: Antithesis to Babylon's extinguished lamplight (18:23 — phōs lychnou ou mē phanē en soi eti). Babylon's lamp goes out; NJ's lamp is the Lamb himself. The light vocabulary connects: phaino (G5316) is used for Babylon's extinguished lamp; doxa (G1391) and phōtizo (G5461) for NJ's divine illumination.

    Kings of earth bring glory into NJ — nations walk in its light¶

    And the nations of them which are saved shall walk in the light of it: and the kings of the earth do bring their glory and honour into it. And the gates of it shall not be shut at all by day: for there shall be no night there. And they shall bring the glory and honour of the nations into it.

    Context: The kings of the earth (basileis tēs gēs, G935) bring their glory (doxan, G1391) into NJ. Same title as 17:2 and 18:3,9 where the kings committed fornication with the harlot. The actors are the same; the relationship is reversed.

    Notes: In 17:2, basileis tēs gēs + porneuō (committed fornication). In 21:24, basileis tēs gēs + pherousin tēn doxan (bring their glory). The gates never shut (21:25) contrasts with the gates of Babylon that are breached (Isa 45:1-2, Jer 51:58). Fulfills Isa 60:3 (kings to the brightness of thy rising) and Isa 60:11 (gates open continually, kings bring wealth).

    Triple exclusion: nothing unclean, no abomination, no lie enters NJ¶

    And there shall in no wise enter into it any thing that defileth, neither [whatsoever] worketh abomination, or [maketh] a lie: but they which are written in the Lamb's book of life.

    Context: The exclusion formula: ou mē eiselthē eis autēn pan koinon kai ho poiōn bdelygma kai pseudos. Three excluded categories: koinon (common/defiled, G2839), bdelygma (abomination, G946), pseudos (lie, G5579). The vocabulary directly references the harlot's cup contents (17:4 — bdelygmatōn, akatharta).

    Notes: bdelygma (G946) appears in both 17:4 (fills the harlot's cup) and 21:27 (excluded from NJ). akathartos (G169) describes the harlot's cup contents (17:4 ta akatharta) and koinon (G2839, defiled/common) is the adjective excluding the unclean from NJ. What fills one city is excluded from the other.

    River of water of life and tree of life¶

    And he shewed me a pure river of water of life, clear as crystal, proceeding out of the throne of God and of the Lamb. In the midst of the street of it, and on either side of the river, [was there] the tree of life, which bare twelve [manner of] fruits, [and] yielded her fruit every month: and the leaves of the tree [were] for the healing of the nations.

    Context: NJ's defining water feature: potamon hydatos zōēs lampron hōs krystallon (river of water of life, bright as crystal). The source is the throne of God and the Lamb (ek tou thronou tou theou kai tou arniou). The tree of life (xulon zōēs) bears twelve fruits monthly — provision for healing.

    Notes: Antithesis to Babylon's waters: the harlot sits on 'many waters' (17:1, hydatōn pollōn) decoded as peoples (17:15); NJ features a single divine river. Babylon's waters are blood-stained (17:6, 18:24); NJ's water is 'of life' (zōēs, G2222). potamos (G4215, 16 occurrences) appears 8 times in Revelation; here it is the river of life. edeixen moi (he showed me) uses deiknymi (G1166) — the same verb from the introduction formula.

    No more curse — God's name on foreheads — eternal reign¶

    And there shall be no more curse: but the throne of God and of the Lamb shall be in it; and his servants shall serve him: And they shall see his face; and his name [shall be] in their foreheads. And there shall be no night there; and they need no candle, neither light of the sun; for the Lord God giveth them light: and they shall reign for ever and ever.

    Context: The comprehensive positive antithesis to the harlot's fate. No more katathema (curse, G2652). God's name (onoma autou) on their foreheads (metōpōn) — direct contrast with 17:5 (harlot's forehead inscription). No night (nyx ouk estai eti) — contrast with Babylon's extinguished lamp. They reign (basileusousin) forever — contrast with Babylon thrown down permanently.

    Notes: The forehead contrast is explicit: 17:5 has 'MYSTERY BABYLON THE GREAT MOTHER OF HARLOTS' inscribed; 22:4 has 'his name' (God's name). The eternal reign (eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn) contrasts with Babylon's permanent destruction (ou mē heurethē eti, 18:21).

    First en Pneumati marker — vision 1 at Patmos¶

    I was in the Spirit on the Lord's day, and heard behind me a great voice, as of a trumpet,

    Context: First of four en Pneumati markers. egenomēn en Pneumati (I became/was in [the] Spirit). Uses ginomai (G1096) — state-entry verb. Located on Patmos (earthly).

    Notes: Visions 1-2 use ginomai en Pneumati; visions 3-4 use apophero en Pneumati. This verb shift divides the four visions into two pairs.

    Second en Pneumati marker — vision 2 in heaven¶

    And immediately I was in the spirit: and, behold, a throne was set in heaven, and [one] sat on the throne.

    Context: Second en Pneumati marker. eutheos egenomēn en Pneumati (immediately I was in [the] Spirit). Same ginomai verb as 1:10, with added adverb eutheōs (immediately). Located in heaven (heavenly throne room).

    Notes: Same kernel as 1:10 but with transport to a new locus. The destination contrast across the four markers: Patmos (earthly) → heaven → wilderness → mountain.

    OT source for Rev 21:10 — mountain transport to see city¶

    In the visions of God brought he me into the land of Israel, and set me upon a very high mountain, by which [was] as the frame of a city on the south.

    Context: Ezekiel transported to a very high mountain (har gavoah me'od) where he sees a city-structure. This is the direct OT template for Rev 21:10 (epi oros mega kai hypselon — upon a great and high mountain). Both transports lead to a vision of the ideal city.

    Notes: The Hebrew har gavoah me'od corresponds to Greek oros mega kai hypselon. Ezek 40:2 uses bemarot elohim (in visions of God) instead of beruah (in the Spirit), but the functional slot is identical — a prepositional phrase qualifying visionary transport.

    Jerusalem as bride adorned by God¶

    Now when I passed by thee, and looked upon thee, behold, thy time [was] the time of love; and I spread my skirt over thee, and covered thy nakedness: yea, I sware unto thee, and entered into a covenant with thee, saith the Lord GOD, and thou becamest mine. Then washed I thee with water; yea, I throughly washed away thy blood from thee, and I anointed thee with oil. I clothed thee also with broidered work, and shod thee with badgers' skin, and I girded thee about with fine linen, and I covered thee with silk. I decked thee also with ornaments, and I put bracelets upon thy hands, and a chain on thy neck. And I put a jewel on thy forehead, and earrings in thine ears, and a beautiful crown upon thine head. Thus wast thou decked with gold and silver; and thy raiment [was of] fine linen, and silk, and broidered work; thou didst eat fine flour, and honey, and oil: and thou wast exceeding beautiful, and thou didst prosper into a kingdom. And thy renown went forth among the heathen for thy beauty: for it [was] perfect through my comeliness, which I had put upon thee, saith the Lord GOD.

    Context: God adorns Jerusalem as a bride with fine linen, silk, ornaments, gold, silver, crown. The beauty is explicitly divine gift: 'my comeliness, which I had put upon thee.' This parallels the NJ bride whose adornment is 'granted' (edothē, 19:8).

    Notes: OT template for the bride-adornment. The garments listed overlap with the harlot's (gold, fine linen) but originate from God rather than self-acquisition. Ezek 16:15-16 shows the same woman turning these gifts into harlotry.

    OT source: Babylon a golden cup making nations drunk¶

    Babylon [hath been] a golden cup in the LORD'S hand, that made all the earth drunken: the nations have drunken of her wine; therefore the nations are mad.

    Context: Direct OT source for Rev 17:4 (golden cup) and 18:3 (nations drunk). The cup in Jeremiah is 'in the LORD's hand' — Babylon is God's instrument of judgment. Revelation's harlot holds the cup herself.

    Notes: The key verbal parallels: golden cup (kos zahav / potērion chrysoun), nations drunk (shatu goyim / pepōkan panta ta ethnē), wine making mad. This is one of 12 documented verbal parallels between Jer 50-51 and Rev 17-18 (3am-12 study).

    High priest's garments: gold, blue, purple, scarlet, fine linen¶

    And they shall take gold, and blue, and purple, and scarlet, and fine linen. And they shall make the ephod [of] gold, [of] blue, and [of] purple, [of] scarlet, and fine twined linen, with cunning work.

    Context: The five materials of the high priest's garments. The harlot's garments (Rev 17:4) match four of these five: gold (chrysion), purple (porphyra), scarlet (kokkinos), and implied fine linen. The missing element is BLUE (tekeleth in Hebrew, hyakinthos in LXX) — identified by the 3am-10 study as the color of the law.

    Notes: The priestly-garment parody: the harlot wears a corrupted version of the high priest's garments. The missing blue represents the absent law/obedience dimension.

    High priest's breastplate: 12 precious stones¶

    And thou shalt set in it settings of stones, [even] four rows of stones: [the first] row [shall be] a sardius, a topaz, and a carbuncle: [this shall be] the first row. And the second row [shall be] an emerald, a sapphire, and a diamond. And the third row a ligure, an agate, and an amethyst. And the fourth row a beryl, and an onyx, and a jasper: they shall be set in gold in their inclosings.

    Context: The 12 breastplate stones for comparison with NJ's 12 foundation stones (Rev 21:19-20). Several stones overlap (jasper, sapphire, emerald, beryl, topaz, amethyst) but the order differs and some names vary.

    Notes: The correspondence between breastplate and NJ foundations is partial, not exact. This is consistent with allusion rather than quotation.

    High priest's forehead plate: HOLINESS TO THE LORD¶

    And thou shalt make a plate [of] pure gold, and grave upon it, [like] the engravings of a signet, HOLINESS TO THE LORD. And thou shalt put it on a blue lace, that it may be upon the mitre; upon the forefront of the mitre it shall be. And it shall be upon Aaron's forehead, that Aaron may bear the iniquity of the holy things, which the children of Israel shall hallow in all their holy gifts; and it shall be always upon his forehead, that they may be accepted before the LORD.

    Context: The high priest's forehead gold plate inscribed HOLINESS TO THE LORD (qodesh laYHWH). This is the direct antithesis to the harlot's forehead inscription (Rev 17:5): MYSTERY BABYLON THE GREAT MOTHER OF HARLOTS.

    Notes: The inversion: a pure gold plate on the high priest's forehead declaring holiness to God → the harlot's forehead inscription declaring her identity as the mother of harlots and abominations. The 3am-10 study documented this as a priestly-garment parody.

    Lady Wisdom builds her house and invites guests¶

    Wisdom hath builded her house, she hath hewn out her seven pillars: She hath killed her beasts; she hath mingled her wine; she hath also furnished her table. She hath sent forth her maidens: she crieth upon the highest places of the city, Whoso [is] simple, let him turn in hither: [as for] him that wanteth understanding, she saith to him, Come, eat of my bread, and drink of the wine [which] I have mingled. Forsake the foolish, and live; and go in the way of understanding.

    Context: Lady Wisdom's invitation — the OT template for the bride's 'Come' in Rev 22:17. Wisdom builds her house, prepares a feast, and invites all to come. Contrast with the foolish woman (9:13-18) whose guests end in Sheol.

    Notes: Rossing's two-women topos: Lady Wisdom = bride/NJ template; Foolish Woman/Strange Woman = harlot/Babylon template. The invitation vocabulary connects: 'Come, eat of my bread, and drink of the wine' (Pro 9:5) parallels 'let him that is athirst come... take the water of life freely' (Rev 22:17).

    Foolish woman sits at her door — guests in depths of Sheol¶

    A foolish woman [is] clamorous: [she is] simple, and knoweth nothing. For she sitteth at the door of her house, on a seat in the high places of the city, To call passengers who go right on their ways: Whoso [is] simple, let him turn in hither: and [as for] him that wanteth understanding, she saith to him, Stolen waters are sweet, and bread [eaten] in secret is pleasant. But he knoweth not that the dead [are] there; [and that] her guests [are] in the depths of hell.

    Context: The foolish woman's invitation is near-verbatim with Wisdom's (compare 9:4 and 9:16) — the difference is the destination. Wisdom leads to life; the foolish woman leads to death/Sheol.

    Notes: This mirrors the Babylon/NJ contrast: Babylon leads to blood and death (17:6, 18:24); NJ offers the water of life (22:1, 22:17). The near-identical invitation formulas in Proverbs 9 parallel the near-identical angel-introduction formulas in Revelation 17:1/21:9.

    Strange woman with attire of a harlot¶

    And, behold, there met him a woman [with] the attire of an harlot, and subtil of heart. (She [is] loud and stubborn; her feet abide not in her house: Now [is she] without, now in the streets, and lieth in wait at every corner.)

    Context: The Strange Woman of Proverbs 7 — dressed as a harlot, active in the streets. Part of the OT two-women topos that Rossing identifies as the template for Revelation's harlot/bride contrast.

    Notes: The harlot's attire (shith zonah) parallels the harlot's adornment in Rev 17:4. The street-dwelling contrasts with Lady Wisdom who builds a house (9:1).

    OT source: glory of the LORD, nations come to light, kings to brightness¶

    Arise, shine; for thy light is come, and the glory of the LORD is risen upon thee. For, behold, the darkness shall cover the earth, and gross darkness the people: but the LORD shall arise upon thee, and his glory shall be seen upon thee. And the Gentiles shall come to thy light, and kings to the brightness of thy rising.

    Context: Direct OT source for Rev 21:23-24. The glory of the LORD illuminates Jerusalem; nations and kings come to its light. Fulfilled in NJ where the glory of God lightens the city and kings bring their glory.

    Notes: The verbal parallels: 'glory of the LORD' (kavod YHWH / doxa tou theou), 'kings to thy brightness' / 'kings of the earth bring their glory', 'Gentiles shall come to thy light' / 'nations shall walk in the light of it.'

    OT source: Jerusalem rebuilt with precious stones¶

    O thou afflicted, tossed with tempest, [and] not comforted, behold, I will lay thy stones with fair colours, and lay thy foundations with sapphires. And I will make thy windows of agates, and thy gates of carbuncles, and all thy borders of pleasant stones.

    Context: OT background for NJ's jewel composition. Foundations of sapphires, windows of agates, gates of carbuncles, borders of pleasant stones.

    Notes: The stone vocabulary overlaps with Rev 21:19-20 (sapphire appears in both). The precious-stone city is an established OT eschatological motif.

    OT source: My tabernacle shall be with them¶

    My tabernacle also shall be with them: yea, I will be their God, and they shall be my people.

    Context: Direct OT source for Rev 21:3 (the tabernacle of God is with men, he will dwell with them, they shall be his people, God shall be with them). The covenant formula is quoted almost verbatim.

    Notes: The tabernacle promise connects the wilderness tabernacle → Solomon's temple → eschatological NJ. The same covenant formula appears in Lev 26:11-12, Jer 31:33, Ezek 37:27, and Rev 21:3.

    OT source: I will set my tabernacle among you and walk among you¶

    And I will set my tabernacle among you: and my soul shall not abhor you. And I will walk among you, and will be your God, and ye shall be my people.

    Context: The earliest form of the tabernacle-dwelling covenant formula. Rev 21:3 fulfills this promise: the skēnē tou theou meta tōn anthrōpōn (tabernacle of God with men), and skenōsei met autōn (he will dwell/tabernacle with them).

    Notes: The verb skenoo (G4637, to tabernacle/dwell) in Rev 21:3 deliberately echoes the tabernacle (mishkan) of Lev 26:11. God's dwelling was in the tabernacle → temple → incarnation (John 1:14 eskenosen) → eschatological NJ.

    OT source: Flee out of the midst of Babylon¶

    Flee out of the midst of Babylon, and deliver every man his soul: be not cut off in her iniquity; for this [is] the time of the LORD'S vengeance; he will render unto her a recompence.

    Context: OT source for Rev 18:4 'Come out of her, my people.' The command to flee Babylon is a deliverance imperative.

    Notes: Combined with Jer 51:45 (My people, go ye out of the midst of her) and Isa 48:20 (Go ye forth of Babylon, flee ye from the Chaldeans) as the OT come-out-of-Babylon tradition.

    OT source: My people, go ye out of the midst of her¶

    My people, go ye out of the midst of her, and deliver ye every man his soul from the fierce anger of the LORD.

    Context: Second OT source for Rev 18:4. Uses 'my people' (ammi) — the same address as Rev 18:4 (ho laos mou).

    Notes: The 'my people' address in both the OT source and Rev 18:4 is significant — it implies that God's people are INSIDE Babylon and must separate.

    First announcement: Babylon is fallen¶

    And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

    Context: The second angel's message introduces the Babylon theme. The doubled epesen (pipto, G4098) quotes Isa 21:9. This verse structurally prepares for the Rev 17-18 expansion.

    Notes: Campbell's claim that the antithetical parallelism is 'carefully engineered from chapter 14 onwards' — this verse introduces the Babylon vocabulary (fallen, great city, wine, fornication) that structures Rev 17-18.

    Seventh bowl: great Babylon remembered¶

    And the great city was divided into three parts, and the cities of the nations fell: and great Babylon came in remembrance before God, to give unto her the cup of the wine of the fierceness of his wrath.

    Context: The seventh bowl leads directly into the Rev 17-18 Babylon expansion. The bowl-angel of 16:17ff is identified as the guide-angel of 17:1 — the same angel who later shows the bride (21:9).

    Notes: The bowl context establishes that Rev 17-18 expands on the seventh bowl's announcement, not a separate parallel sequence.

    Thyatira: Jezebel teaches fornication — chapter 14 preparation¶

    Notwithstanding I have a few things against thee, because thou sufferest that woman Jezebel, which calleth herself a prophetess, to teach and to seduce my servants to commit fornication, and to eat things sacrificed unto idols. And I gave her space to repent of her fornication; and she repented not. Behold, I will cast her into a bed, and them that commit adultery with her into great tribulation, except they repent of their deeds.

    Context: Campbell's claim: the Thyatira letter connects the seven churches to the later city visions. The vocabulary links are: gynē (woman, cf. 17:3), porneia/porneuo (fornication, cf. 17:2,4), bdelygma is absent but eidōlothyta (things sacrificed to idols) is conceptually related.

    Notes: The woman Jezebel teaches fornication (porneuō G4203) and idol-food — both Babylon-vocabulary items. Campbell sees this as preparation for the harlot-city vision.

    OT source: Babylon is fallen, is fallen¶

    And, behold, here cometh a chariot of men, [with] a couple of horsemen. And he answered and said, Babylon is fallen, is fallen; and all the graven images of her gods he hath broken unto the ground.

    Context: The OT source for the doubled 'is fallen, is fallen' (naphelah naphelah) in Rev 14:8 and 18:2 (epesen epesen). The doubling emphasizes certainty and completeness.

    Notes: The verbal allusion is strong: both texts use the doubled fall-verb with Babylon as subject. The 3am-12 study documented this as a direct verbal allusion.

    OT Spirit-transport: lifted between earth and heaven to Jerusalem¶

    And he put forth the form of an hand, and took me by a lock of mine head; and the spirit lifted me up between the earth and the heaven, and brought me in the visions of God to Jerusalem, to the door of the inner gate that looketh toward the north; where [was] the seat of the image of jealousy, which provoketh to jealousy.

    Context: Spirit-transport to see judgment in Jerusalem — parallel to Rev 17:3 (Spirit-transport to see the harlot in wilderness). Both involve transport to see a corrupt/judged entity.

    Notes: The destination in Ezek 8 is the temple in Jerusalem where abominations are committed — thematically parallel to seeing the harlot (who represents a corrupt religious system).     OT Spirit-transport: carried out in the Spirit to valley of bones¶

    The hand of the LORD was upon me, and carried me out in the spirit of the LORD, and set me down in the midst of the valley which [was] full of bones,

    Context: Spirit-transport to a wilderness-type setting (valley of dry bones). Uses the explicit beruah YHWH (in/by the Spirit of the LORD) — the closest lexical match to Rev 17:3/21:10's en Pneumati.

    Notes: The grammar reference entry documents this: Ezek 37:1 has the same anarthrous beruah (= en pneumati) + carrying-verb + destination formula that Revelation inherits.

    OT source: no need for sun — LORD is everlasting light¶

    The sun shall be no more thy light by day; neither for brightness shall the moon give light unto thee: but the LORD shall be unto thee an everlasting light, and thy God thy glory. Thy sun shall no more go down; neither shall thy moon withdraw itself: for the LORD shall be thine everlasting light, and the days of thy mourning shall be ended.

    Context: Direct OT source for Rev 21:23 (no need of sun or moon, God's glory is the light) and 22:5 (no night, God gives them light).

    Notes: Verbal correspondence: 'the LORD shall be unto thee an everlasting light' → 'the glory of God did lighten it, and the Lamb is the light thereof.' The 'days of thy mourning shall be ended' → 'no more death, neither sorrow' (21:4).

    OT bride imagery: Jerusalem as bride with new name¶

    For Zion's sake will I not hold my peace, and for Jerusalem's sake I will not rest, until the righteousness thereof go forth as brightness, and the salvation thereof as a lamp [that] burneth. And the Gentiles shall see thy righteousness, and all kings thy glory: and thou shalt be called by a new name, which the mouth of the LORD shall name. Thou shalt also be a crown of glory in the hand of the LORD, and a royal diadem in the hand of thy God. Thou shalt no more be termed Forsaken; neither shall thy land any more be termed Desolate: but thou shalt be called Hephzibah, and thy land Beulah: for the LORD delighteth in thee, and thy land shall be married. For [as] a young man marrieth a virgin, [so] shall thy sons marry thee: and [as] the bridegroom rejoiceth over the bride, [so] shall thy God rejoice over thee.

    Context: OT Jerusalem-as-bride imagery: new name, crown of glory, land 'married.' The bridegroom/bride vocabulary connects to Rev 21:2,9.

    Notes: The 'new name' (shem chadash) echoes Rev 3:12 (the name of the city of my God). The 'crown of glory' echoes the jewel imagery of NJ. 'Kings thy glory' parallels Rev 21:24 (kings bring their glory).

    Oholah and Oholibah — OT two-women harlot template¶

    And the names of them [were] Aholah the elder, and Aholibah her sister: and they were mine, and they bare sons and daughters. Thus [were] their names; Samaria [is] Aholah, and Jerusalem Aholibah. And Aholah played the harlot when she was mine; and she doted on her lovers, on the Assyrians [her] neighbours,

    Context: Ezekiel's two-sisters allegory: both are God's covenant partners who become harlots. They are 'mine' — covenant relationship precedes harlotry. This supports the zanah/porneia vocabulary analysis (3am-10): harlotry implies prior covenant relationship.

    Notes: The two-harlot-sisters template in Ezekiel 23 provides an OT precedent for Revelation's two-women motif. Both Ezekiel and Revelation use the harlot metaphor for a corrupted covenant community.

    Jerusalem turned harlot — used God-given beauty for fornication¶

    But thou didst trust in thine own beauty, and playedst the harlot because of thy renown, and pouredst out thy fornications on every one that passed by; his it was. And of thy garments thou didst take, and deckedst thy high places with divers colours, and playedst the harlot thereupon: [the like things] shall not come, neither shall it be [so].

    Context: Continuation of Ezek 16:8-14 — Jerusalem takes the God-given adornment and uses it for harlotry. The garments are repurposed for idolatrous worship.

    Notes: The pattern: God adorns Jerusalem (16:8-14) → she misuses the adornment for harlotry (16:15-16). This mirrors the harlot of Rev 17 who wears priestly garments for her own purposes.

    Covering cherub: 9 precious stones¶

    Thou hast been in Eden the garden of God; every precious stone [was] thy covering, the sardius, topaz, and the diamond, the beryl, the onyx, and the jasper, the sapphire, the emerald, and the carbuncle, and gold: the workmanship of thy tabrets and of thy pipes was prepared in thee in the day that thou wast created.

    Context: The covering cherub in Eden had 9 precious stones. These overlap with both the breastplate stones (Exod 28) and NJ's foundation stones (Rev 21). Jasper appears in all three lists.

    Notes: The threefold precious-stone correspondence: (1) breastplate of high priest, (2) covering cherub in Eden, (3) NJ foundations. The stone vocabulary connects priestly service, Edenic perfection, and eschatological restoration.

    Gates open continually — OT source for Rev 21:25¶

    Notes: OT source for Rev 21:25 (gates not shut by day, no night there). The open-gate motif connects Isaiah New Jerusalem to Revelation New Jerusalem. Kings bringing wealth parallels Rev 21:24,26.

    Tyre's trade/cargo list — OT template for Rev 18:11-13¶

    Notes: Ezekiel's cargo list for Tyre is the OT template for Rev 18:11-13 Babylon cargo list. Both enumerate luxury trade goods (gold, silver, precious stones, fine linen, purple, silk, ivory, spices, slaves/bodies of men). The verbal parallel confirms Babylon = end-time Tyre figure.
