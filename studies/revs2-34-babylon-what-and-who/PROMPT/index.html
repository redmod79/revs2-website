    Primary Verses (28 — full analysis required)¶

    Old Testament: - Isa 21:9 — OT source: 'Babylon is fallen, is fallen' — naphal doubled; LXX epesen epesen quoted verbatim at Rev 14:8 and 18:2 (AN085) - Jer 51:6-7 — Come-out call + golden cup: 'Babylon hath been a golden cup in the LORD's hand, that made all the earth drunken' — Rev 17:4 quarry text - Jer 51:45 — 'My people, go ye out of the midst of her' — direct source for Rev 18:4 come-out call - Isa 47:7-9 — Queen boast and sudden widowhood: 'I shall be a lady for ever... I am, and none else beside me; I shall not sit as a widow' — Rev 18:7 direct allusion - Ezek 16:15-16 — Jerusalem as foundling bride turned harlot — zanah (H2181) covenant unfaithfulness; template for Rev 17 harlot imagery - Gen 11:1-9 — Tower of Babel: balal (H1101) = to mix/confuse; etymological origin of 'Babylon' as deliberate mixture

    New Testament: - Rev 17:1-3 — Angel introduction formula + en pneumati transport to wilderness — architecturally paired with 21:9-10; apophero (G667) exclusive to these two passages - Rev 17:4-5 — Harlot's garments (gold, purple, scarlet, precious stones but NO blue) + golden cup full of abominations + forehead inscription MYSTERY BABYLON THE GREAT MOTHER OF HARLOTS — priestly-garment parody and title analysis - Rev 17:6 — Drunk with the blood of saints and martyrs of Jesus — persecuting agency identification; triggers John's thaumazo (G2296) wonder/recognition - Rev 17:7-8 — Beast existence parody: was, is not, yet is — inverts divine existence formula (1:4,8; 4:8); beast goes to apoleia (G684), same term for Judas (John 17:12) and man of sin (2 Thess 2:3) - Rev 17:9-10 — Seven mountains/seven kings interpretive crux — oros (G3735) vs bounos; mountains = kingdoms in OT prophetic imagery (Jer 51:25; Dan 2:35,44) - Rev 17:11 — The beast that was and is not is the eighth and of the seven — arithmetic composite of Daniel's empires; goes to apoleia - Rev 17:15 — Many waters = peoples, multitudes, nations, tongues — angel-interpreted symbol; echoes Jer 51:13 - Rev 17:16 — Ten horns hate the harlot, make her desolate, naked, eat her flesh, burn her with fire — beast destroys the religious system; parallels Ezek 16:37-41 - Rev 17:18 — Explicit identification: the woman is 'that great city' (he polis he megale) which reigns over the kings of the earth — same phrase as Rev 11:8 - Rev 18:2-3 — Expanded fall announcement: egeneto (became) indicates progressive moral deterioration; wine/fornication vocabulary; katoiketerion daimonion - Rev 18:4-5 — Come-out call: 'my people' (ho laos mou) inside Babylon — God claims people within the system; dual hina-me aorist subjunctive clause; OT come-out tradition - Rev 18:7 — Queen boast: 'I sit a queen and am no widow' — near-verbatim quotation of Isa 47:7-9 LXX - Rev 18:12-13 — 28-item cargo list ending with somaton kai psychas anthropon — comparison with Ezek 27 Tyre oracle cargo list; human trafficking climax - Rev 18:20 — Heaven rejoices over Babylon's judgment — echoes Jer 51:48; vindication of prophets and saints - Rev 18:21-23 — Sevenfold ouketi ou me (no more at all): millstone, music, craft, lamp, bridegroom voice — total cessation formula using strongest Greek negation - Rev 18:24 — Blood of prophets, saints, and all slain on earth — echoes Matt 23:34-37 (Jerusalem accusation) but extends scope universally - Rev 14:8 — First announcement of Babylon's fall: epesen epesen — doubled aorist quoting Isa 21:9 LXX verbatim; wine of the wrath (thumos G2372) of her fornication (porneia G4202) - Rev 16:19 — Great city divided into three parts; Babylon comes in remembrance before God — tripartite division of Babylon - Rev 21:9-10 — Mirror angel introduction for bride/New Jerusalem — same formula as 17:1-3 with mountain instead of wilderness; apophero (G667) exclusive pairing - Rev 11:8 — The great city where also our Lord was crucified — same phrase he polis he megale; identifies 'great city' as Jerusalem here; must map all occurrences - Rev 19:1-2 — Four NT allelouia celebrating Babylon's judgment — vindication of blood of God's servants; IC103 convergence zone - Rev 19:10 — Angel-worship refusal closing Vision 3 (Babylon vision) — brackets with 22:8-9 closing Vision 4

    Focus Areas¶

    FA01. Babylon-Bride Mirror (IC182): Revelation's literary architecture places Babylon and New Jerusalem in the most elaborate structural mirror in the entire book. The same bowl-angel introduces both visions using the same formula; the same verb (apophero) transports John in both cases — a verb used nowhere else in Revelation or the NT for Spirit-transport. The destinations are antithetical: wilderness (judgment, desolation) versus great high mountain (divine presence, revelation). Every element of the harlot finds its inverted counterpart in the bride: gilded gold versus pure gold, demon-habitation versus divine tabernacle, extinguished lamp versus eternal light, blood versus water of life. The mirror closes with identical angel-worship refusal scenes, forming a structural bracket. The interpretive force is decisive: whatever the bride IS as a category of thing, the harlot must be the same category of thing in its corrupted form. Verses: Rev 17:1-3, Rev 21:9-10, Rev 17:4, Rev 21:18, Rev 18:2, Rev 21:3, Rev 18:23, Rev 21:23, Rev 17:6, Rev 22:1, Rev 19:10, Rev 22:8-9 Strong's: G667, G5558, G4151, G2048, G3735

    FA02. OT Babylon Allusions: Isa 21:9, Jer 50-51, Isa 47: Jeremiah 50-51 is the primary quarry for Revelation's Babylon material, with at least thirteen documented thematic parallels spanning identity, punishment, lamentation, divine command to flee, golden cup, intoxicated nations, sudden destruction, many waters, fire, heavenly rejoicing, persecution of saints, and the sinking stone. Isaiah contributes the doubled fall formula (21:9) and the queen boast (47:7-9). But John does not simply reproduce these oracles — he transforms them. The golden cup moves from the LORD's hand to Babylon's hand, shifting agency. The 'many waters' of Jer 51:13 (the Euphrates canal system) becomes 'peoples, multitudes, nations, and tongues' (Rev 17:15), universalizing a geographic reference into a global symbol. The come-out call of Jer 51:6,45 becomes Rev 18:4's urgent separation command addressed to God's own people inside the system. These transformations show Revelation reading its OT sources typologically. Verses: Isa 21:9, Jer 51:6-7, Jer 51:13, Jer 51:45, Jer 51:48, Jer 51:63-64, Isa 47:7-9, Jer 50:29, Jer 50:39-40, Rev 14:8, Rev 17:1, Rev 17:4, Rev 18:2, Rev 18:4, Rev 18:7, Rev 18:20, Rev 18:21 Strong's: G4098, G4204, G897, H894, H5307

    FA03. Ezek 27 Tyre Oracle and Rev 18 Cargo List: Revelation 18 borrows heavily from Ezekiel 27's Tyre oracle, but the borrowing is not mechanical reproduction — it is creative transformation. Both lists catalog luxury goods and end with human trafficking, but Revelation adds 'bodies' (somata) to Ezekiel's 'souls of men,' creating a chilling doublet that encompasses total human commodification. The threefold lament in Revelation (kings weeping at a distance, merchants mourning lost markets, shipmasters casting dust on their heads) echoes Ezekiel 27's concentric lament but reorganizes it for apocalyptic maximum impact. The composite nature of Revelation's cargo list — drawing from Ezekiel's Tyre rather than from any Babylon-specific oracle — demonstrates that John is building a symbolic type, not describing a single geographic entity. Ancient Tyre was a commercial empire; by grafting Tyre's commercial profile onto Babylon's religious-political profile, Revelation creates a figure that encompasses both economic exploitation and spiritual corruption. Verses: Ezek 27:12-25, Ezek 27:2-4, Rev 18:9-10, Rev 18:11-17, Rev 18:17-19, Rev 18:12-13, Rev 18:21-23 Strong's: G4983, G5590, G3173

    FA04. Ezek 16 / Hosea Harlotry = Covenant Unfaithfulness: The OT does not call every wicked city a 'harlot.' It reserves this language almost exclusively for entities that were once in covenant relationship with God and departed. Ezekiel 16 depicts Jerusalem as a foundling whom YHWH rescued from the roadside, washed, clothed in royal garments, and married — who then took those very garments and wedding gifts and used them in harlotry. The metaphor requires a prior relationship. Hosea 1-2 develops the same pattern for northern Israel: God is the husband, Israel the unfaithful wife. The punishment in Ezek 16:37-41 — lovers stripping the harlot naked, burning her with fire — is precisely what happens to Babylon in Rev 17:16. There are exceptions: Tyre (Isa 23:15-17) and Nineveh (Nah 3:4) receive harlot language despite being pagan cities. But these exceptions are illuminating: Tyre's 'harlotry' is commercial seduction, and Nineveh's is political domination through sorcery — secondary uses that prove the primary usage domain is covenant infidelity. When Revelation calls Babylon 'the great harlot,' it invokes this entire tradition and its covenant presupposition. Verses: Ezek 16:15-16, Ezek 16:37-41, Ezek 23:2-4, Hos 1:2, Hos 2:2-5, Isa 1:21, Isa 23:15-17, Nah 3:4, Rev 17:1, Rev 17:5, Rev 17:16 Strong's: H2181, G4204, G4202, G4203, H8457

    FA05. Rev 17:5 MYSTERY Title and Mysterion Vocabulary: The word mysterion on the harlot's forehead functions as an interpretive signal. At Rev 1:20, the only other Revelation occurrence, mysterion explicitly introduces symbolic interpretation: 'The mystery of the seven stars... the seven stars ARE the angels.' This sets a precedent: when Revelation labels something a mysterion, it signals that what follows is symbolic, not literal. The forehead inscription also inverts the high priest's golden plate 'HOLINESS TO THE LORD' (Exod 28:36). Where the high priest bore God's character on his forehead, the harlot bears the mystery of iniquity on hers — a priestly-garment parody that points to a corrupted religious system wearing the vestments of true worship. The mother-daughters language (meter ton pornon) uses the productive genitive: this is not calling Babylon the worst harlot but the source/generator of other harlots. This means no single historical institution IS Babylon — they can only be manifestations of the mother system that produces them. Verses: Rev 17:5, Rev 1:20, Rev 17:4, Exod 28:36, Exod 28:5-6, 2 Thess 2:7 Strong's: G3466, G3359, G4204

    FA06. Rev 17:9-11 Seven Mountains/Kings: The standard identification of Rev 17:9's 'seven mountains' with Rome's seven hills faces a lexical problem. John uses oros (G3735), which means 'mountain' in every other Revelation occurrence (6:14,15,16; 8:8; 14:1; 16:20; 21:10) and throughout the NT. It never means 'hill' in Revelation. The text itself does not pause on the geographic reference but immediately transitions: 'they are also seven kings' — suggesting the mountains ARE the kings, functioning as a symbolic transfer chain rather than a geographic clue. The OT supports this: Jeremiah calls Babylon itself a 'destroying mountain' (51:25); Daniel's stone becomes a mountain filling the earth (2:35) representing God's kingdom. Mountains are kingdoms in prophetic vocabulary. The seven-hills argument also faces the circularity problem: if 'seven hills' is meant as an obvious code for Rome, why encode it at all when Rome's identity as the seven-hills city was universally known? A coded reference only works if the code is not immediately transparent. Verses: Rev 17:9-10, Rev 17:11, Jer 51:25, Dan 2:35, Dan 2:44, Zech 4:7, Isa 2:2, Rev 6:14-16, Rev 8:8, Rev 16:20, Rev 21:10 Strong's: G3735, G935, G2776

    FA07. Rev 17:18 'That Great City' — Every 'Great City' in Revelation: The phrase 'the great city' (he polis he megale) appears approximately ten times in Revelation, and its referent is debated at every occurrence. At Rev 11:8, the great city is explicitly identified by the qualifying clause 'where also our Lord was crucified' — unambiguously Jerusalem. But the same verse says this city 'spiritually is called Sodom and Egypt,' using pneumatikos to signal that the city names are symbolic, not geographic. If Jerusalem can 'spiritually' be called Sodom and Egypt — cities with which it shares no geography — then the name 'Babylon' could function the same way: a spiritual designation applied to a city/system that shares Babylon's character. At Rev 16:19, 'the great city' is divided into three parts and distinguished from 'the cities of the nations,' suggesting a single entity with internal divisions. At Rev 17:18, the angel explicitly identifies the woman as 'that great city which reigneth over the kings of the earth' — a description that fits Rome at the time of writing but also fits any religious system that has exercised political dominance. Verses: Rev 17:18, Rev 11:8, Rev 16:19, Rev 18:10, Rev 18:16, Rev 18:18, Rev 18:19, Rev 18:21, Rev 14:8 Strong's: G4172, G3173, G4153

    FA08. Rev 18:4 'Come Out of Her My People' — OT Come-Out Tradition: The come-out call is ancient and cumulative. God called Abraham out of Ur (Gen 12:1), Lot out of Sodom (Gen 19:17), Israel out of Egypt (Exodus), the faithful out of Korah's rebellion (Num 16:26). Isaiah intensified the call with six imperatives (52:11): 'Depart ye, depart ye, go ye out from thence, touch no unclean thing; go ye out of the midst of her; be ye clean, that bear the vessels of the LORD.' Jeremiah directed it specifically at Babylon (51:6,45): 'Flee out of the midst of Babylon... My people, go ye out of the midst of her.' Revelation 18:4 stands as the climax of this tradition. God addresses 'my people' — not potential converts but existing covenant community members — inside a system that has become corrupt. The dual hina-me clause with aorist subjunctive verbs reveals urgency: these people have not yet fully shared in Babylon's sins, but the window for separation is closing. The call presupposes genuine believers sincerely but mistakenly worshiping within a corrupted system, which fits only a religious entity, not a political empire. Verses: Rev 18:4-5, Gen 12:1, Gen 19:17, Exod 8:23, Num 16:26, Isa 48:20, Isa 52:11, Jer 51:6, Jer 51:45, John 10:16, Rev 14:8 Strong's: G2992, G2842, G266

    FA09. Mother/Daughters Relationship (Rev 17:5): The title 'Mother of Harlots' is not a superlative but a productive designation. In Greek, meter + genitive plural pornon means 'source that generates other harlots' — a mother who births daughters carrying her character. This language parallels Ezekiel 23's Oholah and Oholibah, two sisters who both engage in harlotry, demonstrating that unfaithfulness is reproductive rather than singular. The implication is profound: Babylon cannot be identified with any single historical institution because a mother is not the same as her daughters. The system generates offspring. The addition of 'abominations of the earth' (bdelygmaton tes ges) connects to the OT's consistent use of bdelygma for idolatrous worship objects (1 Kings 11:5,7; Dan 9:27; 12:11), confirming that the 'abominations' in question are religious, not merely political or commercial. Babylon is the source-system from which apostate worship practices originate and propagate. Verses: Rev 17:5, Ezek 23:2-4, Ezek 23:19-21, 1 Kings 11:5-7, Dan 9:27, Rev 17:4 Strong's: G3384, G4204, G946, H2181, H8441

    FA10. Evidence For/Against Each Identification Model: Every identification model has genuine textual support. Rome was the city of seven hills, ruled over the kings of the earth, and persecuted the early church — and 1 Peter 5:13 already uses 'Babylon' as a cipher for Rome. Jerusalem killed the prophets, is explicitly called 'the great city' in Rev 11:8, and is the primary OT referent of harlot language. The apostate-religion reading accounts for the harlot-bride mirror (symbolic community), the zanah covenant presupposition, the mother-daughter productive language, and the come-out call to God's people inside the system. The literal-Babylon reading appeals to geographic consistency and unfulfilled OT prophecies. The trans-historical reading explains why Revelation's Babylon is a composite of multiple OT city-judgment oracles. But each model also has weaknesses that must be honestly faced. The question is not which model can amass the most parallels but which model best accounts for the text's own internal logic, structural architecture, and OT source transformations. Verses: Rev 17:9, Rev 17:18, Rev 17:1-5, Rev 11:8, Rev 18:4, Rev 18:24, Rev 17:16, 1 Pet 5:13, Matt 23:34-37 Strong's: G3735, G4172, G4204, G897

    FA11. Blood of Prophets and Saints (Rev 17:6, 18:24): Babylon is drunk — not merely stained — with the blood of saints and martyrs of Jesus. The image is grotesque excess: this is not incidental persecution but systematic, sustained, intoxicating violence against God's people. The accusation connects to the altar vindication arc that spans the entire book: the souls under the altar at 6:9-10 cry for judgment; their vindication is finally pronounced at 19:2 when God judges the great harlot and avenges the blood of his servants at her hand. The persecutor is identified. Jesus' accusation in Matt 23:34-37 assigns prophet-killing specifically to Jerusalem, and this tradition is invoked. But Rev 18:24 extends the scope to 'all that were slain upon the earth' — a scope that transcends any single city. This extension may indicate that Babylon's bloodguilt is systemic rather than geographic: every system that kills truth-tellers participates in the same spirit of persecution, and Babylon as the 'mother' bears responsibility for all her daughters' violence as well as her own. Verses: Rev 17:6, Rev 18:24, Rev 6:9-10, Rev 19:2, Rev 16:5-7, Matt 23:34-37, 1 Thess 2:14-16, Jer 2:30, Neh 9:26 Strong's: G129, G3144, G4396, G40

    FA12. Wine/Fornication Vocabulary Network: Revelation weaves wine, wrath, and fornication into an inseparable vocabulary complex around Babylon. The nations are drunk on 'the wine of her fornication' (14:8; 17:2; 18:3) — her false teaching intoxicates them into spiritual unfaithfulness. But the same wine that intoxicates also becomes the instrument of judgment: 'the wine of the wrath of God, poured out without mixture (akratos)' (14:10). The word akratos is a hapax legomenon in the NT — used only here to describe God's undiluted judgment. The Greek thumos (passion/fury) appears 10 times in Revelation, creating a sustained drumbeat of divine wrath that culminates in the winepress image at 14:19-20 and 19:15. The vocabulary network functions at two levels simultaneously: Babylon's wine is her corrupting influence (false doctrines, spiritual seduction); God's wine is his judgment on those who drink hers. The two wines mirror each other. The harlot herself is 'drunk' — but with the blood of the saints (17:6), not with wine. She intoxicates others while being intoxicated herself on violence. Verses: Rev 14:8, Rev 14:10, Rev 17:2, Rev 18:3, Rev 19:15, Rev 16:19, Rev 17:6, Jer 25:15-16, Jer 51:7 Strong's: G3631, G2372, G4202, G194, G3184, G4204
