    Analysis: Revelation's Already/Not-Yet Temporal Framework¶

    Verse-by-Verse Analysis¶

    Rev 1:1 — En Tachei: The Book's Opening Temporal Claim¶

    Context: The prologue frames the entire book's mandate — "things which must shortly come to pass" — using en tachei (dative of G5034 tachos). This single clause sets the temporal expectation for all 404 verses that follow.

    Direct statement: The contents of Revelation carry an urgency marker from the first sentence. Whatever happens within the book, it is introduced as something that "must" (dei — present indicative of necessity) "shortly" (en tachei) "come to pass" (genesthai — aorist infinitive).

    Original language: τάχει [N-DSN] G5034 — dative of tachos, "in swiftness/in a short time." BDAG gives "soon, in a short time" as the primary gloss for this verse. The infinitive genesthai (aorist of ginomai G1096) links to the entire ginomai arc running through the book. Critically, the phrase "ha dei genesthai" is verbatim from LXX Dan 2:28, which reads "ha dei genesthai ep' eschatōn tōn hēmerōn" — "what must come to pass in the last days." John substitutes "en tachei" for Daniel's "in the last days," converting a distance marker to a nearness marker (AN083, already registered).

    Cross-references: The verbatim repetition at Rev 22:6 creates the prologue-epilogue frame (TM015, already registered). Dan 2:28 is the explicit source text (AN083). The three-camp debate about en tachei — chronological imminence (Farrar, BDAG, Aland), prophetic imminence across all ages (Mounce, Thomas), and manner of execution (Walvoord, Rogers) — is documented in TM188 and IC071/IC072 from revs-39.

    Relationship to other evidence: This verse is the anchor for the entire temporal debate. The structural observation that hypomonē calls (TM190, SP089) throughout the book presuppose a temporal extension argues against strict immediate fulfillment. But the BDAG lexical support for "soon, in a short time" argues against reducing the phrase to mere manner-of-action. Both senses appear to be present.

    Rev 1:3 — Ho Kairos Engus: First Inclusio Marker¶

    Context: First beatitude of Revelation. The blessing is given to those who read, hear, and keep the prophecy — grounded in the temporal declaration "for the time is at hand."

    Direct statement: καιρὸς ἐγγύς — the appointed prophetic season is near. This is the book's opening temporal frame statement alongside the en tachei of v.1.

    Original language: καιρὸς [N-NSM] G2540 + ἐγγύς [ADV] G1451. The choice of kairos over chronos is significant — kairos marks appointed prophetic seasons rather than measured elapsed time (a distinction established in revs2-18 and confirmed in the G2540 word study). G1451 engus: 30 NT occurrences; the Revelation pair (1:3, 22:10) creates the book-spanning inclusio (TM017, already registered). Hebrew source H7138 with PMI 7.99.

    Cross-references: The identical formula "ho kairos engus" closes the book at Rev 22:10, forming the inclusio. Phil 4:5 uses engus of the Lord's nearness in a manner that could be temporal, relational, or both — the word does not restrict itself to one semantic range.

    Relationship to other evidence: The kairos (appointed season) rather than chronos (elapsed duration) reinforces that the book declares a prophetically appointed time has arrived, not merely that clock-time is short. This aligns with the already-inaugurated-but-not-consummated framework.

    Rev 1:4 — Three-Element Divine Formula: Full Form¶

    Context: Epistolary prescript. The divine self-description uses three elements: "who is and who was and who is to come."

    Direct statement: God is described across all three temporal dimensions simultaneously — present being (ho ōn, present participle), past being (ho ēn, imperfect indicative), and anticipated coming (ho erchomenos, present participle of erchomai G2064).

    Original language: ὢν [V-PAP-NSM] G1510 Pres Act Ptcp — "which is." ἦν [V-IAI-3S] G1510 Impf Act Ind — "which was." ἐρχόμενος [V-PNP-NSM] G2064 Pres M/P Ptcp — "which is to come." The erchomenos element is a present participle meaning "the coming one" — an ongoing approach, not merely a future fact. This form appears again at 1:8 and 4:8.

    Cross-references: The dropping of ho erchomenos at Rev 11:17 and Rev 16:5 is the key structural moment in the divine formula reduction arc. When the coming element drops, the text signals that the anticipated arrival has been realized in that context.

    Direct statement: סְתֹ֣ם (satam H5640) Verb.Qal.Impv.2ms — "shut up" (imperative). Time marker: לְיָמִ֥ים רַבִּֽים = "for many days."

    Original language: Same satam root as Dan 12:4. The distance marker "many days" is the clearest contrast with Rev 22:10's engus ("near"). The sealed vision of Dan 8:26 is related to the sealed vision of Dan 12:4 (the 2,300-day and 3.5-time prophecies).

    Relationship to other evidence: The progression: "many days" (Dan 8:26) → "time of end" (Dan 12:4,9) → "time is near" (Rev 22:10) is the canonical arc of the already/not-yet at the macro level of biblical revelation. The sealing-for-distance becomes the unsealing-because-near.

    John 12:31-33 — Prince of World Cast Out (Future Indicative)¶

    Context: Jesus' declaration at his triumphal entry. "Now the prince of this world shall be cast out." The cross is the moment of cosmic judgment.

    Direct statement: ἐκβληθήσεται [V-FPI-3S] G1544 ekballō — Fut Pass Ind 3S — "shall be cast out" (FUTURE — not aorist). The cross event is announced as impending.

    Original language: CRITICAL: G1544 ekballō, NOT G906 ballō (used in Rev 12:9 eblēthē). Cognate but distinct roots. The cross event is announced in future tense from Jesus' pre-cross perspective; Rev 12:9 looks back on the completed casting with aorist. The same event viewed from different temporal vantages, using cognate vocabulary.

    Cross-references: The parallels file confirms: John 12:31 = future passive of G1544 (Satan not yet cast at that moment); Rev 12:9 = aorist passive of G906 (Satan cast, looking back). Both describe the same cosmic displacement, but the roots are distinct: G1544 (throw out) vs G906 (throw/cast generally).

    Relationship to other evidence: John 12:31-33 establishes that Jesus himself understood the cross as the decisive moment of cosmic displacement — the prince of this world is cast out at Calvary. Rev 12:9's aorist then looks back on what Jesus anticipated. The cross = the first stage of the ballo arc.

    John 16:11 — Prince of World Judged: Perfect State¶

    Context: Paraclete discourse. The Spirit convicts of judgment because Satan is judged.

    Direct statement: κέκριται [perfect indicative] — "is judged" (perfect = completed with ongoing state). The judgment is already accomplished with continuing validity.

    Original language: The perfect tense marks the judgment as a completed event whose results stand — the "already" of cosmic judgment in perfect grammatical form.

    Relationship to other evidence: John 16:11's perfect κέκριται is the companion to John 12:31's future ekblēthēsetai — together they frame the cross event as both anticipated (12:31) and accomplished (16:11). The already of Rev 12:9-10 connects to this Johannine cross-theology.

    John 16:33 — Nenikēka: Christ's Perfect Victory Declaration¶

    Context: Farewell discourse. Christ's victory declaration in the PERFECT tense.

    Direct statement: νενίκηκα [V-RAI-1S] G3528 Perf Act Ind Sg 1S — "I have overcome" (PERFECT — completed with ongoing relevance).

    Original language: The perfect tense here emphasizes the ongoing relevance of the completed victory — the disciples' peace (present) is grounded in the settled state of Christ's victory. Contrast Rev 3:21 enikesan (aorist — punctiliar historical act) and Rev 5:5 enikēsen (aorist). The Johannine perfect and the Revelation aorists describe the same reality from different emphatic angles: the perfect at Jn 16:33 foregrounds the ongoing resultant state; the aorists in Revelation foreground the completed historical act.

    Relationship to other evidence: The revs2-05 finding (John 16:33 parallel, already noted in DB) — the perfect vs. aorist comparison shows John and Revelation use different forms of the same verb to describe Christ's victory, each emphasizing a different aspect.

    Isa 21:9 — Naphal Naphal: Hebrew Prophetic Perfect Doublet Source¶

    Context: Isaiah's Babylon oracle. "Babylon is fallen, is fallen."

    Direct statement: Hebrew: naphal naphal Babel (qatal doublet — prophetic perfect). This is the direct OT source for Rev 14:8 and 18:2's Greek epesen epesen (AN085, already registered).

    Original language: Hebrew qatal (perfect) used for future certainty — the prophetic perfect. The LXX renders naphal with epesen (G4098), transferring the doublet structure from Hebrew to Greek. The repetition in the Hebrew marks the certainty of the announcement.

    Relationship to other evidence: The prophetic perfect in Hebrew (qatal for future certainty) is the OT grammatical precedent for the proleptic aorist in Greek. Rev 14:8 and 18:2 are deliberately echoing this OT convention.

    Isa 53:4-5 — Prophetic Perfect in Isaiah: OT Precedent¶

    Context: Isaiah's fourth Servant Song. All Hebrew verbs are past-tense qatal forms describing the Servant's future suffering.

    Direct statement: Hebrew qatal (perfect) throughout vv.4-5 describes future events as completed — the prophetic perfect in its most concentrated OT form.

    Original language: The LXX translates with Greek aorists — the same aorist tense that Rev 14:8 and 18:2 use. The OT → LXX → NT chain is: Hebrew prophetic perfect (qatal) → LXX Greek aorist → Revelation's proleptic aorist. The convention is inherited and transformed.

    Relationship to other evidence: This is the deepest OT precedent for Revelation's proleptic aorist. John is not inventing a new grammatical device — he is using a pattern established in Isaiah's Servant Songs and inherited through the LXX.

    Dan 7:25 — Time Times and Half: Parallel to Rev 12:14¶

    Context: Daniel's fourth beast vision. The little horn's 3.5-year period.

    Direct statement: Aramaic: "a time and times and the dividing of time" = 3.5 years. The saints "given into his hand" echoes Rev 13:7's edothē passives.

    Original language: The "given into his hand" formula parallels exactly the divine passive edothē at Rev 13:7 — both are instances of permitted adversarial authority with built-in limits.

    Relationship to other evidence: The Daniel 7:25 / Rev 12:14 connection links the OT beast-period and the NT beast-period as the same structure of permitted adversarial authority within divine sovereignty.

    Rom 8:30 — Pauline Prophetic Aorist: Glorified as Past¶

    Context: Romans 8 golden chain. "Glorified" is aorist — future glorification stated as already accomplished.

    Direct statement: ἐδόξασεν [V-AAI-3S] G1392 Aor Act Ind 3S — "glorified" (aorist for future believers). This is the Pauline proleptic aorist precedent.

    Original language: The proleptic aorist use in Romans 8:30 demonstrates that the device is not unique to Revelation — it is a recognized Koine/LXX rhetorical-grammatical convention across NT authors.

    Relationship to other evidence: The Pauline precedent supports reading Revelation's aorists as proleptic rather than as chronological errors. What God declares is treated as accomplished regardless of temporal sequence.

    Rom 16:20 — En Tachei in Paul: Temporal Imminence¶

    Context: Romans closing. "God shall bruise Satan under your feet shortly."

    Direct statement: ἐν τάχει [G5034, N-DSN] — "shortly" (en tachei). Clear temporal-imminence context.

    Original language: The Pauline use of en tachei here is in a straightforward temporal-imminence context — the Satan-bruising is promised as temporally near. This supports the temporal-imminence reading of en tachei at Rev 1:1.

    Cross-references: Gen 3:15 ("seed bruising serpent's head") is the OT allusion. The Satan-bruising is "coming shortly" in Paul's expectation.

    Relationship to other evidence: The Pauline precedent for en tachei as temporal imminence (not merely manner-of-action) is the strongest contextual argument against the pure manner-reading (Walvoord) at Rev 1:1.

    Luke 10:18 — Satan Fall as Lightning: Pre-Cross Declaration¶

    Context: Jesus' response to the 70 returning from ministry. He sees Satan's defeat in the disciples' successful exorcisms.

    Direct statement: ἐθεώρουν [G2334, imperfect] — "I was beholding." Σατανᾶν πεσόντα [G4098, aorist participle] — "Satan having fallen."

    Original language: Imperfect of viewing + aorist participle = Jesus watched as Satan fell. The aorist participle "having fallen" describes a completed action observed in ongoing vision. The highest NT parallel for Rev 12:9 (0.349 score).

    Relationship to other evidence: Jesus' pre-cross statement about Satan's defeat correlates with John 12:31 (cross = casting out), Rev 12:9 (aorist casting), and Rev 12:10 (arti — NOW — realized). The four texts together trace the cross-event as the already of Satan's defeat: anticipated (Lk 10:18), announced as impending (Jhn 12:31), declared accomplished (Rev 12:9-10).

    Jude 1:9 — Michael and the Devil¶

    Context: Jude's citation of Michael's contention with the devil over Moses' body.

    Direct statement: Michael appears as agent in cosmic conflict — parallel to his role in Rev 12:7.

    Original language: The devil is the "accuser" (katēgorōn at Rev 12:10) — his role as prosecutor/accuser spans both Jude and Revelation.

    Relationship to other evidence: The Michael-devil background in Jude illuminates Rev 12:7-9's war in heaven as part of a longer pattern of cosmic legal and military contest. The "accuser of the brethren" (Rev 12:10) is the same adversary Michael confronted — the legal-court metaphor of Satan's access to heaven (Job 1-2 background) grounds the "cast down from accusation" interpretation.

    1 John 3:8 — Destroy Works of Devil: Cross-Event Already¶

    Context: 1 John 3. The Son of God's purpose: to destroy the devil's works.

    Direct statement: ἐφανερώθη [V-API-3S] G5319 Aor Pass Ind — "was manifested" (completed event). λύσῃ [V-AAS-3S] G3089 Aor Act Subj — "might destroy" (purpose).

    Original language: The cross-event "already" of 1 John 3:8 parallels John 16:33 and John 12:31 — the purpose of the incarnation was the destruction of the devil's works, expressed in aorist and aorist subjunctive forms.

    Relationship to other evidence: The consistent cross-event already in John, 1 John, and Revelation (Rev 12:9-10) forms a coherent theological unity: the cross accomplished the decisive defeat, while the ongoing activity of the dragon (12:13-17) constitutes the not-yet.

    Hebrews 2:14 — Destroy Devil Through Death: Cross Already¶

    Context: Hebrews 2. Christ's incarnation to destroy the one holding the power of death.

    Direct statement: καταργήσῃ [V-AAS-3S] G2673 Aor Act Subj — "might destroy/render inoperative." The devil "had" (past) the power of death — Christ's death changed that.

    Original language: The perfect understanding: Christ's death has already accomplished the defeat of the power of death. The aorist subjunctive marks the purpose-and-result of the incarnation.

    Relationship to other evidence: The same cross-event already that John expresses in Rev 12:9-10 is expressed in Hebrews as the defeat of the power of death. The "already" of the cross spans multiple NT authors with consistent vocabulary.

    Direct statement: ταχύ [ADV] G5035 — "quickly" (speed/urgency, not eschatological imminence).

    Original language: The manner-of-action use of tachy — urgent departure from prison has nothing to do with temporal nearness of an eschatological event.

    Relationship to other evidence: This non-eschatological use confirms the semantic range of tachy includes manner-of-action alongside temporal imminence.     Direct statement: ἐγγύς [ADV] G1451 — "at hand" (engus). Pauline use — temporal, relational, or both.

    Original language: The G1451 word study notes Php 4:5 "sits on the boundary between temporal nearness and relational proximity." The semantic range of engus does not restrict to one meaning.

The Revelation inclusio may carry both — the time is prophetically appointed and near, not merely a calendar countdown.

    Patterns Identified¶

    Pattern 1: The Ginomai Completion Arc¶

    The verb ginomai (G1096) traces Revelation's temporal progression through three grammatical stages:

    • Proleptic aorist egeneto (11:15; 12:10): declared as accomplished before narrative execution
    • Perfect singular gegonen (16:17): bowl sequence completed, results in effect
    • Perfect plural gegonan (21:6): all things accomplished at new creation

    The movement from aorist (certainty declared) to perfect singular (sequence completed) to perfect plural (everything completed) is Revelation's own grammatical spine for the already/not-yet. TM197 / SP096 (already registered) catalog this arc.

    Pattern 2: The Nikao Tense Taxonomy¶

    The verb nikaō (G3528) creates a complete taxonomy of the already/not-yet paradox: - Christ's completed self-originated victory (aorist indicative: Rev 3:21, 5:5) - Believer's ongoing overcoming (present participle: Rev 3:21, 21:7) - Beast's permitted dependent conquest (aorist infinitive after edothē: Rev 13:7) - Eschatological Lamb victory (future indicative: Rev 17:14)

    TM195 / IC075 (already registered) catalog this taxonomy.

    Pattern 3: The Ballo Arc¶

    The verb ballō (G906) traces the dragon's three-stage displacement: - Heaven→earth (eblēthē aorist passive, 12:9) - Earth→abyss (ébalen aorist active, 20:3) - Abyss→lake of fire (eblēthē aorist passive, 20:10)

    TM196 / SP095 (already registered) catalog this arc.

    Pattern 4: The Divine Formula Reduction¶

    The divine temporal formula reduces from three elements (ho ōn + ho ēn + ho erchomenos, at 1:4, 1:8, 4:8) to two elements (ho ōn + ho ēn, erchomenos absent, at 11:17 and 16:5). The reduction occurs at the structural moments of realized arrival — the "coming one" element drops when the anticipated coming is realized in the immediate context.

    Pattern 5: The Hypomonē Cluster¶

    Hypomonē (G5281) appears at structurally significant positions — especially in pairs (13:10 / 14:12) at the points of maximum eschatological tension during the beast's permitted authority. The calls to endurance are embedded precisely where the not-yet is most severe.

    Pattern 6: The Proleptic Hōra Formula¶

    The hōra + ēlthen formula (judgment hour declared arrived — 14:7; harvest hour declared arrived — 14:15; Babylon's judgment declared arrived — 18:10) constitutes a structural series of proleptic declarations within narrative proclamation. Each uses the same aorist ēlthen to announce what has not yet been narratively executed.

    Pattern 7: The Daniel-Revelation Seal Inversion¶

    The seal vocabulary creates a four-step canonical arc: Daniel seals for distance (Dan 12:4, chatham/satam imperatives) → Daniel describes the sealed state (Dan 12:9, chatham/satam participles) → Revelation seals only the seven thunders (Rev 10:4 — the one exception) → Revelation prohibits sealing because time is near (Rev 22:10, mē sphragisēs). SP090 / TM023 (already registered) catalog this arc.

    Pattern 8: The En Tachei / Tachu Frame¶

    The en tachei inclusio (Rev 1:1 / 22:6, TM015) and the tachy vocabulary throughout (TM189, TM020) create an urgency-consciousness that pervades the entire book. The semantic range allows both temporal imminence and manner-of-action readings.

    Word Study Integration¶

    Ginomai (G1096)¶

    The ginomai word study confirms that egeneto is the dominant form (233 of 668 forms = 34.9%). The proleptic aorist use at 11:15 and 12:10 (declared) vs. the perfect singular at 16:17 (completed) vs. perfect plural at 21:6 (all completed) represents Revelation's most concentrated temporal arc in a single verb.

    Nikao (G3528)¶

    28 NT occurrences — 17 in Revelation (61%). The tense taxonomy across the book's nikao occurrences (aorist indicative for Christ/heaven; present participle for believers; aorist infinitive after edothē for beast; future indicative for eschatological Lamb) encodes the entire already/not-yet framework within a single verb family.

    Ballō (G906)¶

    127 NT occurrences; 23 in Revelation (highest density at 5.7 per 100 verses). The three-stage dragon displacement arc uses the same G906 root at each stage — verbal unity across chapters 12 and 20 that makes the arc explicit.

    Hypomonē (G5281)¶

    32 NT occurrences — 7 in Revelation (highest density at 1.7 per 100 verses). The seven occurrences are not random — they cluster at structural points of tribulation narrative (letters, seal interlude, beast section). Hebrew source H4723 miqveh (hope/expectation vocabulary) suggests that hypomonē in Revelation is not mere stoic endurance but hope-grounded waiting.

    Arti (G737)¶

    36 NT occurrences — 1 in Revelation (12:10). The single Revelation use appears at the most theologically loaded moment in the book: the heaven-perspective declaration of accomplished salvation following the dragon's expulsion. The punctiliar "this very moment" sense of arti marks the arti egeneto as the sharpest already-marker in the book.

    Chronos (G5550) vs. Kairos (G2540)¶

    All four Revelation chronos occurrences carry "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" meaning. All five Revelation kairos occurrences mark appointed prophetic seasons. The distinction governs Rev 10:6: it is chronos (measured duration) that ceases, not kairos (appointed seasons). The not-yet interval is a chronos-bounded period, not a kairos-bounded one.

    Engus (G1451) and En Tachei (G5034)¶

    The engus inclusio (1:3 / 22:10) and the en tachei frame (1:1 / 22:6) are complementary but not identical: engus marks the appointed season as near (stative); en tachei marks the events as soon/swiftly (manner-of-action or temporal). Both contribute to the already/not-yet temporal consciousness without resolving the three-way debate between imminence readings.

    Cross-Testament Connections¶

    Isaiah 21:9 → Rev 14:8 / 18:2¶

    The Hebrew prophetic perfect doublet (naphal naphal) in Isa 21:9 is the direct OT source for the Greek proleptic aorist doublet (epesen epesen) at Rev 14:8 and 18:2. The convention travels: Hebrew qatal → LXX aorist → Revelation proleptic aorist. John is deliberately invoking Isaiah's prophetic certainty through vocabulary echo.

    Isaiah 53:4-5 → Revelation's Proleptic Aorists¶

    The most concentrated OT prophetic perfect passage (Isaiah's Servant Song) establishes the grammatical convention for declaring future events as accomplished through qatal forms. The LXX translation creates the Greek precedent. Revelation inherits and uses this established pattern.

    Daniel 2:28 → Rev 1:1¶

    The verbatim echo "ha dei genesthai" (what must come to pass) converts Daniel's "in the last days" to Revelation's "in a short time" — the same divine necessity language but with a nearness marker substituted for Daniel's distance marker (AN083, already registered).

    Daniel 12:4,9 / 8:26 → Rev 22:10¶

    The chatham (H2856) / satam (H5640) imperatives and participles in Daniel create the sealing-for-distance framework that Rev 22:10's mē sphragisēs (prohibitive aorist subjunctive of G4972 sphragizō) inverts. The LXX translation chain makes the inversion explicit through shared vocabulary.

    Daniel 12:7 → Rev 10:5-7¶

    The lifted-hand oath in Dan 12:7 is echoed in Rev 10:5-6 — the same gesture, the same swearing by the eternal living God. Dan 12:7's oath is about the duration of the 3.5-year period; Rev 10:6's oath is about the cessation of chronos (the remaining delay). The structural parallel (confirmed in parallels file) shows Rev 10 as the NT fulfillment-announcement of what Daniel swore about.

    John 12:31-33 → Rev 12:9¶

    The cross-event in John (ekblēthēsetai future, G1544) and the casting in Revelation (eblēthē aorist, G906) are related through cognate roots (G1544 ekballō is a compound of G906 ballō) and shared referent. John anticipates; Revelation looks back. Different tenses, different vantage points, same cosmic reality.

    Rom 8:30 → Revelation's Proleptic Aorists¶

    The Pauline prophetic aorist (glorified — edoxasen, for future believers) establishes that the grammatical device is a recognized NT convention, not a Revelation peculiarity. The proleptic aorist in Romans confirms the exegetical legitimacy of reading Revelation's aorists the same way.

    Difficult or Complicating Passages¶

    1. The En Tachei / Tachu Debate¶

    The three-position debate (chronological imminence / prophetic imminence across all ages / manner-of-action) is not fully resolved by lexical data alone. BDAG gives "soon, in a short time" for en tachei at Rev 1:1, supporting temporal imminence. Acts 12:7 and 22:18 demonstrate that tachy can mean manner-of-speed without temporal imminence. Rom 16:20 uses en tachei in a clear temporal context. The structural argument (SP089: hypomonē co-occurrence presupposes temporal extension) argues against strict immediate fulfillment. Isaiah 13:22 shows that OT prophetic "at hand" language can span centuries.

    The most honest position is that both senses are present — the events are both temporally near (in the sense that the end-time fulfillment phase has been inaugurated by Christ's first advent) and swiftly executed (when they come, they come without delay). Neither sense fully excludes the other. IC071 (beginning of fulfillment phase) and IC072 (manner-of-action) both represent valid textual observations, as documented by revs-39. This study does not add a new interpretive claim beyond what IC071/IC072 already register.

    2. The Timing of the Dragon's Casting Down¶

    Rev 12:9-10 presents the casting as accomplished (aorists), and Rev 12:10's arti marks it as "now" from the heavenly perspective. Bohr and Beale both argue this corresponds to the cross event (John 12:31-33). The vocabulary connection is real (G1544 ekballō / G906 ballō are cognate), but not identical roots. Luke 10:18 ("Satan fall as lightning") is Jesus' pre-cross statement about the outcome already visible in the disciples' exorcisms.

    The grammatical data supports that the casting of Rev 12:9 is presented as accomplished before the dragon's ongoing earthly activity in 12:13-17. Whether this corresponds specifically to the cross (Bohr, Beale, EGW) or to a primordial pre-creation expulsion or to the parousia is a chronological judgment the grammar itself does not resolve with precision. What the grammar clearly shows is: the casting is "already" (completed aorist), the earthly activity is "not yet ended" (present and imperfect tenses). The specific historical event that constitutes the "already" is the exegetical question the grammar opens but does not close.

    3. The Beast's 42-Month Reign vs. Christ's Already-Completed Victory¶

    The beast's edothē (divine passive, aorist) authority for 42 months operates concurrently with Christ's already-completed nikao (5:5, aorist). This is not a contradiction — the beast's authority is explicitly derivative (edothē = permitted) and bounded (42 months = built-in time limit). But the coexistence of Christ's completed victory and the beast's ongoing permitted conquest is the central tension of the book.

    Beale's solution (the 42 months spans the entire church age from ascension) is consistent with the grammar: edothē (aorist passive) points to a past grant of authority (consistent with ascension), and the present-tense activity of the beast throughout Rev 13 supports an ongoing not-yet period. The IC077 hybrid model (revs2-20) accommodates this without requiring a specific dating of when the 42 months began.

    4. Chronos at Rev 10:6¶

    "Time shall be no longer" (chronos ouketi estai) is either (a) the end of measured sequential time itself (supported by the eschatological context) or (b) the end of the delay period before the mystery is finished (supported by the ESV rendering and Dan 12:7 parallel). The G5550 word study's identification of all four Revelation chronos occurrences as "Brief Remaining Period / Space for Action" supports reading (b) — the cessation declared is the cessation of the remaining delay interval, not the abolition of time itself. But reading (a) remains grammatically available. The Dan 12:7 oath parallel (parallels file confirmed) is the strongest contextual argument for reading (b): Daniel's oath is about when the 3.5-year period ends; Rev 10:6's oath echoes that structure, declaring the delay before the mystery is finished will end.

    5. Revelation 11:17's KJV Addition¶

    KJV's "art to come" at Rev 11:17 is a translation addition not supported by the Greek text. The parsing confirms: ὁ ὢν + ὁ ἦν only — two elements. The addition obscures one of the most significant structural signals in the book. Any analysis depending on the KJV text of 11:17 to support a full three-element reading misses the point of the reduction.

    Preliminary Synthesis¶

    The evidence from all 76 verses points consistently in one direction: Revelation's already/not-yet temporal framework is not incidental to the book but architecturally embedded in its grammar, vocabulary, and structural design.

    The grammatical evidence is the strongest pillar: - Proleptic aorists systematically declare future events as accomplished (SP093) - Perfect tenses mark completions whose results stand (gegonen, gegonan, eilephas, kekritai) - Present participles mark ongoing activities in tension with completed acts (nikōn alongside enikesan; plananōn alongside eblēthē) - The divine formula reduction (erchomenos dropped at 11:17 and 16:5) signals realized arrival at structural moments

    The vocabulary evidence is the second pillar: - Arti (G737) at Rev 12:10 marks the punctiliar "this moment" of realized salvation - En tachei / tachu frame the entire book in urgency - Hypomonē clusters at tribulation-peak moments, marking the not-yet structurally - Chronos (measured duration) is distinguished from kairos (appointed season) throughout

    The structural evidence is the third pillar: - Heaven-earth dual perspective at every major seam (SP094) — heaven celebrates accomplished realities while earth endures ongoing tension - The ballo arc spans chapters 12 to 20 with verbal unity (TM196) - The nikao taxonomy spans the book's seven sections with grammatical precision (TM195) - The Daniel sealed / Revelation unsealed contrast creates a canonical already/not-yet at the level of prophetic disclosure itself (SP090)

    Revelation does not present events as either strictly past or strictly future. It presents the decisive victory as already accomplished (from heaven's perspective, in Christ's aorist victories, in the proleptic hymns), while the full earthly realization remains not-yet (beast still reigns, dragon still rages, saints still endure). These are not two separate timelines — they are two perspectives on the same reality. The book is written to help its readers inhabit both perspectives simultaneously.
