    Verses¶

    Question¶

    How do the seven heads/mountains/kings of the beast (Rev 17:9-11) function as a structural and symbolic element? What does the 'five are fallen, and one is, and the other is not yet come' formula (17:10) reveal about the beast's temporal structure? How do the seven beatitudes (makarios — Rev 1:3, 14:13, 16:15, 19:9, 20:6, 22:7, 22:14) create a structural spine distributed through the entire book? Do the beatitudes form a chiastic or progressive pattern? Document each beatitude's position in the book's narrative and its thematic content.

    Dragon with seven heads — first appearance of the seven-headed entity¶

    And there appeared another wonder in heaven; and behold a great red dragon, having seven heads and ten horns, and seven crowns upon his heads.

    Context: The heavenly sign sequence. A great red dragon appears after the woman clothed with the sun. This is the first of three entities bearing seven heads. Crowns (diadēmata) are on the heads, not on the horns (contrast Rev 13:1).

The wound is healed (etherapeuthē, aorist passive), reversing the death. The world's 'wondering after' (ethaumasthē) parodies worship.

    Scarlet beast with seven heads — third entity bearing the seven-headed symbol¶

    So he carried me away in the spirit into the wilderness: and I saw a woman sit upon a scarlet coloured beast, full of names of blasphemy, having seven heads and ten horns.

    Context: The fourth en pneumati vision (cf. 1:10, 4:2, 21:10). The scarlet beast is the third entity with seven heads and ten horns. Names of blasphemy now cover the entire beast ('full of'), not just the heads (13:1). A woman sits upon this beast.

    Notes: The vision-introduction formula 'in the spirit' marks this as the third of four major vision segments. The woman-on-beast image is the primary location for interpreting the seven heads.

    Angel's interpretation begins — beast's temporal parody formula¶

    And the angel said unto me, Wherefore didst thou marvel? I will tell thee the mystery of the woman, and of the beast that carrieth her, which hath the seven heads and ten horns. The beast that thou sawest was, and is not; and shall ascend out of the bottomless pit, and go into perdition: and they that dwell on the earth shall wonder, whose names were not written in the book of life from the foundation of the world, when they behold the beast that was, and is not, and yet is.

    Context: The angel offers to explain the mystery (mystērion) of both woman and beast. The beast's temporal formula appears: ēn (was, imperfect) + ouk estin (is not, present) + parestai (will be present, future). This inverts God's formula: ho ōn (present) + ho ēn (past) + ho erchomenos (future). The beast ascends from the abyss — counterfeit resurrection.

    Notes: The beast's formula ēn kai ouk estin kai parestai inverts the divine ōn kai ēn kai erchomenos. Key differences: (1) divine formula starts with present (IS), beast starts with past (WAS); (2) divine uses erchomenos (coming continuously), beast uses parestai (will be present — a fixed arrival); (3) divine has no negation phase, beast has 'is not' — a death/absence period.

    Core interpretive text — seven heads = mountains = kings, temporal formula, eighth king¶

    And here [is] the mind which hath wisdom. The seven heads are seven mountains, on which the woman sitteth. And there are seven kings: five are fallen, and one is, [and] the other is not yet come; and when he cometh, he must continue a short space. And the beast that was, and is not, even he is the eighth, and is of the seven, and goeth into perdition.

    Context: The angel's dual identification: heads = mountains AND kings. The temporal formula divides seven kings into past (five fallen, epesan aorist), present (one is, estin), and future (one not yet come, oupo ēlthen). The seventh continues oligon (a short space). The beast itself becomes the eighth (ogdoos, anarthrous) and is ek tōn hepta (of/from the seven, masculine genitive agreeing with basileis).

    Notes: Critical for Rev 17:10: if Daniel uses 'king' (melek H4428) interchangeably with 'kingdom' (malkuth), then Rev 17's 'seven kings' could equally mean 'seven kingdoms.' This governs the historicist interpretation.

    Fourth beast = fourth kingdom — confirms king/kingdom interchangeability¶

    Thus he said, The fourth beast shall be the fourth kingdom upon earth, which shall be diverse from all kingdoms, and shall devour the whole earth, and shall tread it down, and break it in pieces.

    Context: In the same vision, the beast is now called a kingdom (not a king as in 7:17). This confirms the interchangeability.

    Notes: Dan 7:17 says 'four kings'; Dan 7:23 says 'fourth kingdom.' The same entity is simultaneously a king and a kingdom. This Danielic convention is foundational for interpreting Rev 17's 'seven kings.'

    Four beasts from the sea — the composite source for Rev 13's beast¶

    And four great beasts came up from the sea, diverse one from another. The first [was] like a lion, and had eagle's wings: I beheld till the wings thereof were plucked, and it was lifted up from the earth, and made stand upon the feet as a man, and a man's heart was given to it. And behold another beast, a second, like to a bear, and it raised up itself on one side, and [it had] three ribs in the mouth of it between the teeth of it: and they said thus unto it, Arise, devour much flesh. After this I beheld, and lo another, like a leopard, which had upon the back of it four wings of a fowl; the beast had also four heads; and dominion was given to it.

    Context: Daniel's four beasts sequence: lion, bear, leopard (with four heads), and a terrible fourth beast. The leopard has four heads; the other beasts have one each. Total: 1+1+4+1=7 heads across all four beasts. Rev 13:1-2 collapses all four into one composite.

    Notes: Head arithmetic: lion(1) + bear(1) + leopard(4) + terrible beast(1) = 7 heads total. This provides the identification key for the seven heads on Rev 13:1's beast. The composite beast of Rev 13 reverses the order (leopard-bear-lion), looking backward through history.

    Graded numerical saying — three...four (X/X+1 pattern)¶

    The horseleach hath two daughters, [crying], Give, give. There are three [things that] are never satisfied, [yea], four [things] say not, [It is] enough: The grave; and the barren womb; the earth [that] is not filled with water; and the fire [that] saith not, [It is] enough.

    Context: Agur's numerical proverbs. The X/X+1 pattern: 'three...four' — lists three then adds a fourth to signal completeness-plus-one. This literary device may illuminate Rev 17:10-11's 'seven...eighth' pattern.

    Notes: The graded numerical saying uses consecutive numbers as a literary device indicating completeness + emphasis, not a strict count. If Rev 17's 'seven then eighth' follows this pattern, the eighth is not chronologically distinct but represents the apex/culmination of the seven.

    Graded numerical saying — three...four (second example)¶

    There be three [things which] are too wonderful for me, yea, four which I know not: The way of an eagle in the air; the way of a serpent upon a rock; the way of a ship in the midst of the sea; and the way of a man with a maid.

    Context: Second X/X+1 example. Three things...four things. The actual list contains four items, with the fourth being the climactic one.

    Notes: Another three/four pattern. The fourth item is the climactic focus, not a separate category from the three.

    Prophetic use of graded numerical saying — three...four transgressions¶

    Thus saith the LORD; For three transgressions of Damascus, and for four, I will not turn away [the punishment] thereof; because they have threshed Gilead with threshing instruments of iron:

    Context: Amos's oracle cycle. The pattern 'for three transgressions...and for four' is repeated for Damascus (1:3), Gaza (1:6), Tyre (1:9), Edom (1:11), Ammon (1:13), Moab (2:1), Judah (2:4), and Israel (2:6). The X/X+1 formula is used structurally throughout the prophetic oracle.

    Notes: The Amos cycle shows the X/X+1 pattern in a prophetic context (not just wisdom literature). Bauckham argues Rev 17:10-11 follows this convention: 'seven...and the eighth' as a literary device indicating fullness of transgression or dominion.

    Seven shepherds and eight principal men — OT seven/eight parallel¶

    And this [man] shall be the peace, when the Assyrian shall come into our land: and when he shall tread in our palaces, then shall we raise against him seven shepherds, and eight principal men.

    Context: Messianic prophecy. Seven shepherds and eight principal men — a direct seven/eight pairing in an OT prophetic text. This is the closest OT parallel to Rev 17's seven-then-eighth pattern.

    Notes: This is the most direct OT parallel for the seven-then-eight pattern. The seven and eight are parallel expressions of the same idea (sufficient/more-than-sufficient leadership), not two separate groups of 7+8=15 leaders. This supports Bauckham's graded numerical saying interpretation.

    Give a portion to seven and also to eight — wisdom seven/eight pattern¶

    Cast thy bread upon the waters: for thou shalt find it after many days. Give a portion to seven, and also to eight; for thou knowest not what evil shall be upon the earth.

    Context: Wisdom instruction. The seven/eight pairing expresses generous distribution — give to seven (a complete number), and even to eight (beyond completeness), because the future is uncertain.

    Notes: Seven and eight are not distinct counts but express 'completeness and then some.' This is the wisdom-literature usage of the seven/eight pattern. Together with Mic 5:5, Amos 1:3ff, and Prov 30, it builds a cumulative case for the graded numerical saying interpretation of Rev 17:10-11.

    Six troubles...seven — X/X+1 numerical pattern¶

    He shall deliver thee in six troubles: yea, in seven there shall no evil touch thee.

    Context: Eliphaz speaks. The pattern: six troubles...seven — deliverance covers both the expected and the unexpected. Another X/X+1 pattern in the wisdom tradition.

    Notes: The six/seven pattern complements the three/four and seven/eight patterns. In all cases, X represents completeness and X+1 represents exceeding completeness.

    Leviathan's heads broken — OT multi-headed beast archetype¶

    For God [is] my King of old, working salvation in the midst of the earth. Thou didst divide the sea by thy strength: thou brakest the heads of the dragons in the waters. Thou brakest the heads of leviathan in pieces, [and] gavest him [to be] meat to the people inhabiting the wilderness. Thou didst cleave the fountain and the flood: thou driedst up mighty rivers. The day [is] thine, the night also [is] thine: thou hast prepared the light and the sun. Thou hast set all the borders of the earth: thou hast made summer and winter.

    Context: Asaph's psalm recalling God's victory over chaos forces. 'Heads' (roshē, plural) of leviathan — multiple heads on the sea creature. God breaks them. This is the OT foundation for the multi-headed beast imagery in Revelation.

    Notes: Ps 74:14 uses 'heads' (roshē, plural of rosh H7218) for Leviathan — the only OT explicit multi-headed creature. Ps 74:13 also mentions 'heads of the dragons' (roshē tanninim) in the waters. God breaks/crushes these heads as a creative-salvific act. Rev 12:3's seven-headed dragon may deliberately evoke this chaos-monster tradition.

    Leviathan the piercing and crooked serpent — dragon/sea beast background¶

    In that day the LORD with his sore and great and strong sword shall punish leviathan the piercing serpent, even leviathan that crooked serpent; and he shall slay the dragon that [is] in the sea.

    Context: Eschatological judgment. Leviathan called both 'piercing serpent' (nachash bariach) and 'crooked serpent' (nachash aqallathon) and equated with 'the dragon (tannin) in the sea.' God slays the sea-dragon — the same vocabulary field as Rev 12-13.

    Notes: Isaiah's three terms (leviathan, serpent, dragon/tannin) map to Revelation's three terms (dragon/drakōn, beast/thērion, serpent/ophis). The sea location connects to Rev 13:1 (beast rising from the sea). This OT background suggests Revelation's seven-headed dragon-beast draws on ancient combat mythology transformed through prophetic tradition.

    Rahab and the dragon — divine conquest of sea monsters¶

    Awake, awake, put on strength, O arm of the LORD; awake, as in the ancient days, in the generations of old. [Art] thou not it that hath cut Rahab, [and] wounded the dragon? [Art] thou not it which hath dried the sea, the waters of the great deep; that hath made the depths of the sea a way for the ransomed to pass over?

    Context: Isaiah calls on God's arm to act as in ancient days — cutting Rahab and wounding the dragon (tannin). Links to the Exodus sea crossing. The dragon/sea monster imagery is presented as historical divine victory.

    Notes: Rahab (H7294) is another OT sea-monster name alongside Leviathan and Tannin. The 'cutting' (chalal) and 'wounding' (mecholeledh) of the dragon connect to Revelation's wound motif (Rev 13:3).

    Pharaoh as the great dragon — tannin/dragon applied to an empire¶

    Speak, and say, Thus saith the Lord GOD; Behold, I [am] against thee, Pharaoh king of Egypt, the great dragon that lieth in the midst of his rivers, which hath said, My river [is] mine own, and I have made [it] for myself.

    Context: God addresses Pharaoh as 'the great dragon' (hattannim haggadol) lying in the rivers. A political ruler is identified with the chaos-dragon. This provides OT precedent for equating empires/rulers with dragon/beast imagery.

    Notes: Pharaoh = dragon (tannin). This OT equation of a political ruler with the chaos-monster provides the interpretive bridge between mythological beast imagery and historical empire symbolism. Rev 12's dragon operates in the same conceptual space.

    Babylon is fallen, is fallen — OT source of the doubled epesan formula¶

    And, behold, here cometh a chariot of men, [with] a couple of horsemen. And he answered and said, Babylon is fallen, is fallen; and all the graven images of her gods he hath broken unto the ground.

    Context: Oracle against Babylon. The doubled 'is fallen, is fallen' (naphla naphla) formula is the source for Rev 14:8 and 18:2's 'Babylon is fallen, is fallen' (epesen epesen). The Hebrew naphal (H5307) maps to Greek pipto/epesan.

    Notes: The doubled naphal/epesan formula creates an intertextual chain: Isa 21:9 (Hebrew naphal) → Rev 14:8 (Greek epesan epesen) → Rev 18:2 (epesan epesan). The same pipto verb appears in Rev 17:10 (epesan, five are fallen). The fall of Babylon echoes the fall of the five kings.

    Babylon is fallen, is fallen — doubled epesan echoing Isaiah¶

    And there followed another angel, saying, Babylon is fallen, is fallen, that great city, because she made all nations drink of the wine of the wrath of her fornication.

    Context: Second angel's message. The doubled epesan (from pipto G4098) echoes Isa 21:9. The prophetic aorist treats a future event as already completed.

    Notes: The same pipto verb used in the temporal formula of 17:10 (epesan, five are fallen) is used here for Babylon's fall. The vocabulary chain links the fall of individual kingdoms (17:10) with the fall of the Babylon system (14:8, 18:2).

    Circumcision on the eighth day — eight as covenant/new beginning marker¶

    And he that is eight days old shall be circumcised among you, every man child in your generations, he that is born in the house, or bought with money of any stranger, which [is] not of thy seed.

    Context: The Abrahamic covenant. Circumcision on the eighth day — after the seven days of creation completeness, the eighth day marks the beginning of covenant relationship.

    Notes: Eight as the number of new beginning/covenant: circumcision on the eighth day (Gen 17:12), Noah's eight souls saved (1 Pet 3:20), Christ's resurrection on the first day of the new week (the eighth day). If the eighth king of Rev 17:11 carries this symbolism, it represents a new phase of the beast's existence — a counterfeit new beginning.

    Eight souls saved through water — eight as new creation number¶

    Which sometime were disobedient, when once the longsuffering of God waited in the days of Noah, while the ark was a preparing, wherein few, that is, eight souls were saved by water.

    Context: Noah's family of eight survived the flood — the old world ended and a new world began with eight. The number eight marks the transition from old to new.

    Notes: Eight souls through the flood = new beginning. Combined with Gen 17:12 (circumcision on 8th day) and Christ's resurrection (1st/8th day), the number eight consistently marks new creation. The beast's eighth phase in Rev 17:11 is then a counterfeit new creation — the beast's false resurrection.

    Mount Seir as enemy kingdom personified — mountain = political power¶

    Son of man, set thy face against mount Seir, and prophesy against it, And say unto it, Thus saith the Lord GOD; Behold, O mount Seir, I [am] against thee, and I will stretch out mine hand against thee, and I will make thee most desolate.     Context: God prophesies against Mount Seir (Edom). The 'mount' is addressed as a political entity with cities, hatred, and military aggression. Mountain = kingdom/political power.

    Notes: Mount Seir is not a geological feature in this oracle but a political entity — Edom. The mountain has cities, conducts warfare, and bears moral guilt. This reinforces the OT pattern where 'mountain' symbolizes kingdom/political power (alongside Jer 51:25 and Dan 2:35).

    Mountain of the LORD's house exalted — mountain = kingdom authority¶

    But in the last days it shall come to pass, [that] the mountain of the house of the LORD shall be established in the top of the mountains, and it shall be exalted above the hills; and people shall flow unto it.

    Context: Eschatological vision. The LORD's mountain is established above all other mountains — God's kingdom authority surpasses all earthly kingdoms. Mountains here clearly symbolize political/religious authority centers.

    Notes: The mountain of the LORD is above other mountains — divine kingdom authority above human kingdom authority. This parallels Dan 2:35 where the stone-mountain fills the whole earth. Consistent OT pattern: mountain = kingdom/authority.

    Christ's existence formula — alive, was dead, alive forevermore¶

    [I am] he that liveth, and was dead; and, behold, I am alive for evermore, Amen; and have the keys of hell and of death.

    Context: Christ's self-identification: ho zōn (the living one) + egenomēn nekros (I became dead) + zōn eimi eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn (I am living forever and ever). Christ genuinely died and rose; the beast's parody in 17:8 (was, is not, yet is) mirrors this but counterfeit.

    Notes: Christ's pattern: living → dead → alive forevermore. Beast's pattern: was → is not → will be present. The parallelism is structural: both involve a death-absence phase followed by a return-to-life phase. But Christ's resurrection is genuine (zōn eis tous aiōnas), while the beast goes to destruction (eis apōleian).
