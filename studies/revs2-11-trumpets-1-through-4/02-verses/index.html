    Verses¶

    Question¶

    What is the structural pattern of trumpets 1-4 — the '1/3 destruction' pattern? Document what is struck, what fraction is affected, and what the result is for each trumpet. How do trumpets 1-4 parallel the Exodus plagues? Use Exodus 7-12 as the source text and document the element-by-element mapping. What structural distinction separates the first four trumpets from the three woes (trumpets 5-7)?

    Incense prelude — sanctuary context for the trumpet sequence¶

    And I saw the seven angels which stood before God; and to them were given seven trumpets. And another angel came and stood at the altar, having a golden censer; and there was given unto him much incense, that he should offer [it] with the prayers of all saints upon the golden altar which was before the throne. And the smoke of the incense, [which came] with the prayers of the saints, ascended up before God out of the angel's hand. And the angel took the censer, and filled it with fire of the altar, and cast [it] into the earth: and there were voices, and thunderings, and lightnings, and an earthquake.

    Context: After the seventh seal opening (8:1), John sees the incense prelude establishing the sanctuary context. The same censer (libanotos G3031) carries prayers upward (8:3-4) then fire downward (8:5), pivoting from intercession to judgment. The Sinai-theophany formula at 8:5 (voices, thunderings, lightnings, earthquake — 4 elements) signals that the trumpet judgments emanate from the sanctuary.

    Notes: This passage was analyzed in revs2-10. The DOA censer parallel (AN022), Sinai-theophany escalation (TM057), and censer reversal are established findings. The 4-element theophany formula here will escalate to 5 elements at 11:19 (adding great hail).

    Formulaic introduction — seven angels prepare to sound¶

    And the seven angels which had the seven trumpets prepared themselves to sound.

    Context: Transitional verse between the incense prelude and the first trumpet. The seven angels with seven trumpets (introduced at 8:2) now prepare to sound, establishing the sevenfold sequence.

    Notes: The verb 'prepared themselves' (hetoimazo, G2090) marks the deliberate, structured nature of the sequence — these are not random calamities but ordered judgments.

    First trumpet — hail and fire mingled with blood; 1/3 trees and all green grass burnt¶

    The first angel sounded, and there followed hail and fire mingled with blood, and they were cast upon the earth: and the third part of trees was burnt up, and all green grass was burnt up.

    Context: The first trumpet initiates the four nature-trumpets. The formulaic pattern: ho protos esalpisen (aorist active indicative 3S). Three destruction agents (chalaza G5464, pyr G4442, haima G129) target the earth domain (ge G1093). The 'mingled with blood' element has no parallel in the Exodus hail plague — blood is from Plague 1, suggesting composite allusion.

    Second trumpet — great mountain burning with fire cast into sea; triple 1/3¶

The mountain image sources from Jer 51:25 ('destroying mountain...burnt mountain'). Three 1/3 fractions in rapid succession: sea to blood, creatures dying, ships destroyed.     Context: The third trumpet is uniquely structured: a named star (ho Apsinthos G894) falls on rivers (potamos G4215) and fountains (pege G4077). The article variation is structurally notable: 'ho Apsinthos' (with article — named entity, parsed N-NSM) vs. 'apsinthon' (without article — substance, parsed N-ASF). The verb epikranthesan (pikraino G4087, aorist passive indicative 3P) describes the result. The OT source is not Exodus but Jeremiah: Jer 9:13-15 links wormwood (laanah H3939) to apostasy.

    Fourth trumpet — 1/3 sun, moon, stars smitten; rhetorical climax with 5 occurrences of to triton¶

    And the fourth angel sounded, and the third part of the sun was smitten, and the third part of the moon, and the third part of the stars; so as the third part of them was darkened, and the day shone not for a third part of it, and the night likewise.

    Context: The fourth trumpet contains 5 occurrences of to triton (G5154) in a single verse — the rhetorical climax of the first four trumpets. The verb eplege (plesso G4141, aorist passive indicative 3S) describes the sun being 'smitten.'     And I also have given you cleanness of teeth in all your cities, and want of bread in all your places: yet have ye not returned unto me, saith the LORD. And also I have withholden the rain from you, when [there were] yet three months to the harvest: and I caused it to rain upon one city, and caused it not to rain upon another city: one piece was rained upon, and the piece whereupon it rained not withered. So two [or] three cities wandered unto one city, to drink water; but they were not satisfied: yet have ye not returned unto me, saith the LORD. I have smitten you with blasting and mildew: when your gardens and your vineyards and your fig trees and your olive trees increased, the palmerworm devoured [them]: yet have ye not returned unto me, saith the LORD. I have sent among you the pestilence after the manner of Egypt: your young men have I slain with the sword, and have taken away your horses; and I have made the stink of your camps to come up unto your nostrils: yet have ye not returned unto me, saith the LORD. I have overthrown [some] of you, as God overthrew Sodom and Gomorrah, and ye were as a firebrand plucked out of the burning: yet have ye not returned unto me, saith the LORD. Therefore thus will I do unto thee, O Israel: [and] because I will do this unto thee, prepare to meet thy God, O Israel.

    Context: Amos presents five escalating judgments (famine, drought, blight, pestilence, overthrow), each followed by the refrain 'yet have ye not returned unto me' (velo-shavtem aday). This is the closest OT structural parallel to the trumpet sequence as a whole: escalating judgments followed by failed repentance. Amos 4:10 ('after the manner of Egypt') explicitly links escalating judgments to the Exodus paradigm.

    Notes: The five Amos judgments progress in severity: (1) famine, (2) drought, (3) agricultural destruction, (4) pestilence/'manner of Egypt,' (5) Sodom-level overthrow. The final warning 'prepare to meet thy God' echoes the trumpet sequence's function as warning before ultimate judgment. Joel 2:12-14 offers the possibility of repentance that the trumpet-hearers in Revelation refuse.

    Destroying mountain / burnt mountain — OT source for 'great mountain burning with fire'¶

    Behold, I [am] against thee, O destroying mountain, saith the LORD, which destroyest all the earth: and I will stretch out mine hand upon thee, and roll thee down from the rocks, and will make thee a burnt mountain.

    Context: Jeremiah addresses Babylon as a 'destroying mountain' (har hammashchith) that God will make into a 'burnt mountain' (har serephah). This provides the OT source for the second trumpet's 'great mountain burning with fire' (hos oros mega pyri kaiomenon, Rev 8:8). The connection is through the 'burning/burnt mountain' imagery rather than through Exodus plague vocabulary.

    Notes: The 'mountain' image in the second trumpet has no Exodus parallel — its source is prophetic judgment on Babylon. This adds a Babylon-judgment dimension to the second trumpet that the Exodus-plague mapping alone does not account for.

    Psalm recapitulation of plagues in non-Exodus order — OT precedent for reordering¶

    He sent darkness, and made it dark; and they rebelled not against his word. He turned their waters into blood, and slew their fish. Their land brought forth frogs in abundance, in the chambers of their kings. He spake, and there came divers sorts of flies, [and] lice in all their coasts. He gave them hail for rain, [and] flaming fire in their land. He smote their vines also and their fig trees; and brake the trees of their coasts. He spake, and the locusts came, and caterpillers, and that without number, And did eat up all the herbs in their land, and devoured the fruit of their ground. He smote also all the firstborn in their land, the chief of all their strength.

    Context: Psalm 105 recapitulates the Egyptian plagues in a reordered sequence: darkness (P9), blood (P1), frogs (P2), flies/lice (P3-4), hail (P7), locusts (P8), firstborn (P10). Plagues 5-6 (cattle disease, boils) are omitted. This demonstrates that the OT itself does not require strict sequential ordering of plagues in later references.

    Notes: Both Psa 105:28-36 and Psa 78:43-51 provide OT precedent for non-sequential plague recitation. Revelation's non-sequential mapping (T1=P7, T2=P1, T3=non-plague, T4=P9) follows this established OT practice.

    Second OT precedent for non-sequential plague mapping¶

    How he had wrought his signs in Egypt, and his wonders in the field of Zoan: And had turned their rivers into blood; and their floods, that they could not drink. He sent divers sorts of flies among them, which devoured them; and frogs, which destroyed them. He gave also their increase unto the caterpiller, and their labour unto the locust. He destroyed their vines with hail, and their sycomore trees with frost. He gave up their cattle also to the hail, and their flocks to hot thunderbolts. He cast upon them the fierceness of his anger, wrath, and indignation, and trouble, by sending evil angels [among them]. He made a way to his anger; he spared not their soul from death, but gave their life over to the pestilence; And smote all the firstborn in Egypt; the chief of [their] strength in the tabernacles of Ham:

    Context: Psalm 78 also reorders the plagues: blood (P1), flies (P4), frogs (P2), locusts (P8), hail (P7), cattle/pestilence (P5), firstborn (P10). Different order from both Exodus and Psalm 105 — a second independent precedent for non-sequential plague recitation.

    Notes: The two Psalm passages demonstrate that reordering plagues was an established literary practice in Israel's worship tradition. This OT precedent undermines any argument that Revelation 'should' follow the Exodus plague order.

    Sinai-theophany formula origin — 3 elements at the throne¶

    And out of the throne proceeded lightnings and thunderings and voices: and [there were] seven lamps of fire burning before the throne, which are the seven Spirits of God.

    Context: The origin point of the Sinai-theophany escalation formula. Three elements: astrapai (lightnings G796), brontai (thunderings G1027), phonai (voices G5456). This formula reappears at 8:5 with 4 elements (adding seismos G4578), at 11:19 with 5 elements (adding chalaza megale), and at 16:18-21 with expanded description.

    Notes: The astrape word study confirms that astrape appears exclusively in these four Sinai-theophany passages within Revelation: 4:5, 8:5, 11:19, 16:18. The escalation from 3→4→5→5+ elements is the structural skeleton connecting the throne room to each judgment sequence climax.

    Context: The fifth element (chalaza megale, great hail — the same chalaza G5464 used in trumpet 1) is added to the theophany formula at the seventh trumpet. This creates a verbal inclusio bracketing the trumpet sequence: chalaza at 8:7 (first trumpet) and chalaza megale at 11:19 (seventh trumpet). The temple opening reveals the ark of the testament — the MHP furniture.

    Notes: The Sinai-theophany escalation (TM057): 4:5 (3 elements) → 8:5 (4, +earthquake) → 11:19 (5, +great hail) → 16:18-21 (5+ expanded). This constitutes a structural progression observable across all four occurrences.

    Joel's call to repentance — the possibility that trumpet-hearers refuse¶

    Therefore also now, saith the LORD, turn ye [even] to me with all your heart, and with fasting, and with weeping, and with mourning: And rend your heart, and not your garments, and turn unto the LORD your God: for he [is] gracious and merciful, slow to anger, and of great kindness, and repenteth him of the evil. Who knoweth [if] he will return and repent, and leave a blessing behind him; [even] a meat offering and a drink offering unto the LORD your God?

    Context: Joel offers the possibility of repentance after the warning judgments — 'turn ye even to me with all your heart.' The question 'who knoweth if he will return and repent?' presents repentance as a real but uncertain possibility. This contrasts sharply with Rev 9:20-21 where the trumpet-hearers definitively 'repented not.'

    Notes: Joel provides the template: warning judgments → call to repentance → possibility of divine relenting. Revelation follows the template but with a negative outcome: warning judgments → no repentance → continued/escalated judgment.
